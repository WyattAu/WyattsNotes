### Mitigation and Adaptation
<PracticeProblem questions={[
  {question: "The Paris Agreement (2015) aimed to limit global warming to:", options: ["A) 1.0 degrees C above pre-industrial levels", "B) Well below 2 degrees C above pre-industrial levels, with efforts to limit warming to 1.5 degrees C", "C) 4.0 degrees C above pre-industrial levels", "D) Zero net warming by 2020"], correct: 1, explanation: "The Paris Agreement, signed by 196 countries, committed to limiting global warming to well below 2 degrees C above pre-industrial levels while pursuing efforts to limit it to 1.5 degrees C. Countries submit Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs) outlining their emissions reduction targets. The 1.5 degrees C target requires global CO2 emissions to reach net zero by approximately 2050. Current pledges are insufficient -- projected warming under current NDCs is approximately 2.7 degrees C by 2100."},
  {question: "Carbon trading (cap and trade) is a mitigation strategy that:", options: ["A) Bans the burning of all fossil fuels", "B) Sets a cap on total emissions and allows companies to buy and sell emission permits, creating a market price for carbon", "C) Requires all countries to reduce emissions by the same percentage", "D) Provides free carbon credits to all developing nations"], correct: 1, explanation: "Cap and trade systems set an overall limit (cap) on greenhouse gas emissions and issue or auction permits that companies can trade. Companies that reduce emissions below their allowance can sell surplus permits to those exceeding their limit. The EU Emissions Trading System (EU ETS) is the largest such scheme. The economic rationale is that it reduces emissions cost-effectively by allowing the market to determine where reductions are cheapest. Critics argue it can be vulnerable to fraud, lobbying, and price volatility."},
  {question: "Which of the following is an example of climate change adaptation rather than mitigation?", options: ["A) Installing solar panels to replace coal-fired power", "B) Building sea walls to protect coastal communities from rising sea levels", "C) Planting trees to absorb CO2 from the atmosphere", "D) Implementing a carbon tax on industrial emissions"], correct: 1, explanation: "Mitigation reduces the causes of climate change (reducing greenhouse gas emissions or enhancing carbon sinks). Adaptation adjusts to the actual or expected effects of climate change. Building sea walls, developing drought-resistant crops, improving flood defences, relocating vulnerable communities, and enhancing early warning systems are all adaptation measures. The IPCC emphasises that both mitigation and adaptation are necessary -- mitigation alone cannot prevent all impacts, and adaptation alone cannot address the root cause."},
  {question: "Afforestation is considered a mitigation strategy because:", options: ["A) It reduces the amount of sunlight reaching the ground", "B) Trees absorb CO2 from the atmosphere through photosynthesis, acting as a carbon sink", "C) Forests reduce wind speeds and lower temperatures directly", "D) Tree planting is cheaper than renewable energy investment"], correct: 1, explanation: "Afforestation (planting trees on previously unforested land) and reforestation (replanting degraded forests) are important mitigation strategies because trees sequester carbon dioxide through photosynthesis, storing carbon in biomass and soils. The IPCC estimates that forests absorb approximately 2.6 billion tonnes of CO2 annually -- about 30% of human emissions. However, forests can become carbon sources if they are degraded, burned, or cleared. Large-scale afforestation programmes exist in China, Ethiopia, and India, though their effectiveness depends on species selection, maintenance, and long-term protection."},

While solar variation does influence Earth's climate, satellite measurements since 1978 show that solar output has not increased enough to account for the observed rapid warming. The IPCC concludes that solar forcing is a relatively minor factor compared to anthropogenic greenhouse gas emissions. The period of strongest recent warming (since the 1970s) has occurred despite a slight decrease in solar irradiance, ruling out the sun as the primary driver."},

Heatwaves will become more frequent, longer, and hotter. Heavy rainfall events will intensify (a warmer atmosphere holds more moisture -- approximately 7% more per degree C of warming). Tropical cyclones may become less frequent but more intense with higher wind speeds and rainfall. Droughts will become more prolonged in some regions. These changes create compound risks where multiple extreme events occur simultaneously or sequentially."}
