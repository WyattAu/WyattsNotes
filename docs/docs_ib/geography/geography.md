# IB Geography (SL/HL)

## Course Overview

IB Geography is a two-year course that examines the relationships between individuals, societies, and the physical environment at a range of scales, from local to global. The course integrates physical and human geography, requiring students to analyse spatial patterns, evaluate competing theories, and apply geographic methodologies to real-world problems. Both Standard Level (SL) and Higher Level (HL) students study the same core themes, but HL students additionally complete the Global Interactions extension and an extra examination paper.

The course is distinctive in its emphasis on geographic skills, fieldwork, and the synthesis of quantitative and qualitative data. Students are expected to engage with case studies drawn from a variety of global contexts, including countries at different stages of economic development. The course assumes no prior geographic knowledge beyond a basic secondary-school foundation, but it rapidly advances to a level of rigour comparable to introductory university courses.

## Core Themes

### Part 1: Geographic Themes (Optional Themes)

All students study **two** of the following seven optional themes, selected by the school:

1. **Freshwater -- issues and conflicts** -- water scarcity, drainage basin hydrology, flood management, and water quality
2. **Oceans and their coastal margins** -- ocean currents, coral reefs, coastal management, and marine pollution
3. **Extreme environments** -- glacial, periglacial, hot, arid, and semi-arid environments
4. **Geophysical hazards** -- earthquakes, volcanic eruptions, tsunamis, and mass movement
5. **Leisure, tourism, and sport** -- the growth of tourism, eco-tourism, and the Olympic Games
6. **Food and health** -- food security, malnutrition, and the geography of disease
7. **Urban environments** -- urbanisation trends, urban models, megacities, and sustainable urban planning

These optional themes are examined in **Paper 1**, which consists of structured questions requiring extended responses. The selection of themes is a school-level decision and varies by institution.

### Part 2: Geographic Perspectives -- Global Change (Core Extension)

All students study the following compulsory core, which is examined in **Paper 2**:

- **Population distribution -- changing population** -- demographic transition, population policies, migration, and population-resource relationships
- **Global climate -- vulnerability and resilience** -- causes and evidence of climate change, impacts, mitigation, and adaptation
- **Global resource consumption and security** -- resource pathways, food, water, and energy security

This core extension integrates physical and human geography and demands that students evaluate competing explanations and policy responses.

## HL Extension: Global Interactions (Paper 3)

Higher Level students additionally study the **Global Interactions** extension, which examines the processes and consequences of increasing global interconnectedness. Key topics include:

- **Power, places, and networks** -- the spatial distribution of power, global hubs, and flows
- **Human development and diversity** -- cultural exchange, cultural diffusion, and the erosion of local cultures
- **Global risks and resilience** -- global-scale hazards, systemic risks, and geopolitical threats
- **Sustainable development goals** -- SDG evaluation, trade-offs, and implementation challenges

The HL extension is assessed through **Paper 3**, which requires students to synthesise knowledge from the entire course in response to extended essay-style questions.

## Assessment Structure

### Paper 1 (SL: 1 hour 30 minutes; HL: 1 hour 30 minutes)

- Covers the two optional geographic themes
- Structured and extended-response questions based on stimulus material (maps, photographs, graphs, text)
- Worth **35%** of total marks at SL; **25%** at HL

### Paper 2 (SL: 1 hour 30 minutes; HL: 1 hour 30 minutes)

- Covers the compulsory core extension (Geographic Perspectives -- Global Change)
- Structured and extended-response questions based on stimulus material
- Worth **35%** of total marks at SL; **25%** at HL

### Paper 3 (HL only: 1 hour)

- Covers the HL extension: Global Interactions
- Three extended-response essay questions; students answer one
- Worth **20%** of total marks at HL

### Internal Assessment (IA): Fieldwork Report

- All students (SL and HL) complete a fieldwork investigation of 2500 words (SL) or 3000 words (HL)
- Students must identify a geographic question or hypothesis, collect primary data through fieldwork, analyse data using appropriate techniques, and evaluate the methodology
- Worth **30%** of total marks at SL; **20%** at HL

## Geographic Skills and Fieldwork

Throughout the course, students develop a range of geographic skills that are assessed both in the IA and in the written examinations:

- **Map skills** -- interpreting topographic maps, calculating scale, using grid references, drawing cross-sections from contour lines
- **Data collection** -- designing questionnaires, conducting field observations, using secondary data sources
- **Data presentation** -- constructing and interpreting graphs, maps, and diagrams
- **Statistical analysis** -- measures of central tendency and dispersion, inferential statistics (Spearman's rank correlation, chi-squared test)
- **Geographic Information Systems (GIS)** -- using digital mapping tools, layering spatial data, and conducting spatial analysis
- **Fieldwork methodology** -- identifying research questions, selecting sampling methods, evaluating reliability and validity
