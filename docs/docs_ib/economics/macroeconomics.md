## National Income Accounting

### Gross Domestic Product (GDP)

GDP measures the total monetary value of all final goods and services produced within a country's
borders in a given time period.

**Three methods of measuring GDP:**

1. **Expenditure method**: $GDP = C + I + G + (X - M)$
   - $C$ = household consumption
   - $I$ = investment (gross fixed capital formation + changes in inventories)
   - $G$ = government spending
   - $X - M$ = net exports (exports minus imports)

2. **Income method**: sums all factor incomes (wages, rent, interest, profit) earned by residents

3. **Output (value-added) method**: sums the value added at each stage of production to avoid
   double-counting

**Nominal vs. real GDP:**

$$\mathrm{Real GDP} = \frac{\mathrm{Nominal GDP}}{\mathrm{GDP Deflator}} \times 100$$

Real GDP adjusts for price level changes and is a better measure of actual output growth.

**GDP per capita** ($\mathrm{GDP} / \mathrm{Population}$) is used as a rough proxy for living
standards but has significant limitations: it does not account for income distribution, non-market
activities (subsistence farming, unpaid domestic work), environmental degradation, or the value of
leisure time.

**Gross National Income (GNI)** measures the total income earned by a nation's residents regardless
of where it is earned: $\mathrm{GNI} = \mathrm{GDP} + \mathrm{Net income from abroad}$.

**Purchasing Power Parity (PPP)** adjusts GDP for differences in the cost of living between
countries, allowing more meaningful international comparisons.

## Inflation

### Measuring Inflation

Inflation is the sustained increase in the general price level over time. The primary measure is the
**Consumer Price Index (CPI)**, which tracks the price of a weighted basket of goods and services
representative of household consumption.

$$\mathrm{Inflation rate} = \frac{\mathrm{CPI}_t - \mathrm{CPI}_{t-1}}{\mathrm{CPI}_{t-1}} \times 100$$

**Limitations of CPI:**

- **Substitution bias**: the basket is updated infrequently, so consumers who substitute away from
  goods that have become relatively more expensive are not properly accounted for
- **New product bias**: new goods and services are not immediately included
- **Quality change bias**: improvements in quality that justify price increases are difficult to
  measure
- **Outlet bias**: consumers may switch to discount retailers, which is not captured

### Types of Inflation

**Demand-pull inflation** occurs when aggregate demand exceeds aggregate supply at full employment,
creating an inflationary gap:

$$\mathrm{AD} > \mathrm{LRAS} \implies \mathrm{Price level rises}$$

**Cost-push inflation** occurs when costs of production increase (e.g., rising wages, higher raw
material prices), shifting SRAS leftward and raising prices while reducing output.

**Built-in inflation** (wage-price spiral): workers demand higher wages to keep up with rising
prices, and firms pass these higher labour costs onto consumers as higher prices, creating a
self-reinforcing cycle.

### Consequences of Inflation

- **Redistribution**: inflation redistributes income from lenders (creditors) to borrowers (debtors)
  because the real value of repayments falls. It also harms those on fixed incomes.
- **Uncertainty**: high and unpredictable inflation discourages investment and long-term planning.
- **Menu costs**: firms must frequently change prices, incurring administrative costs.
- **Shoe-leather costs**: individuals spend time and resources managing cash holdings to minimise
  the inflation tax.
- **International competitiveness**: if a country's inflation rate exceeds that of its trading
  partners, its exports become less competitive and imports become relatively cheaper, worsening the
  current account.

**Deflation** (a sustained fall in the general price level) can be more damaging than moderate
inflation: it increases the real burden of debt, discourages spending (as consumers delay purchases
expecting lower prices), and can lead to a deflationary spiral.

### The Phillips Curve

The short-run Phillips Curve depicts an inverse relationship between unemployment and inflation. As
unemployment falls, labour markets tighten, wages rise, and cost-push inflation increases.

In the long run, the Phillips Curve is vertical at the **natural rate of unemployment** (NAIRU —
Non-Accelerating Inflation Rate of Unemployment). Any attempt to keep unemployment below the NAIRU
through demand-side policies leads only to accelerating inflation.

## Unemployment

### Measuring Unemployment

The **unemployment rate** is the percentage of the labour force that is actively seeking work but
unable to find it:

$$\mathrm{Unemployment rate} = \frac{\mathrm{Number unemployed}}{\mathrm{Labour force}} \times 100$$

The labour force includes all employed persons plus those actively seeking work. It excludes
discouraged workers and those not seeking work.

### Types of Unemployment

- **Structural unemployment**: caused by a mismatch between workers' skills and the requirements of
  available jobs, or by geographical immobility. Examples include technological change (automation
  replacing jobs) and deindustrialisation.
- **Frictional unemployment**: short-term unemployment that occurs when workers are between jobs,
  entering the labour force for the first time, or searching for better opportunities. It is always
  present in a dynamic economy.
- **Cyclical (demand-deficient) unemployment**: caused by a deficiency in aggregate demand during a
  recession. Output falls below potential GDP, and firms lay off workers.
- **Seasonal unemployment**: occurs when workers are employed only during certain times of the year
  (e.g., agriculture, tourism).
- **Real-wage unemployment**: caused by wages being set above the equilibrium level (e.g., through
  minimum wage laws or strong trade unions), leading to an excess supply of labour.

## Aggregate Demand and Aggregate Supply

### Aggregate Demand (AD)

AD is the total planned expenditure on goods and services at each price level:

$$\mathrm{AD} = C + I + G + (X - M)$$

The AD curve slopes downward due to:

1. **Wealth effect**: as the price level rises, the real value of money balances falls, reducing
   consumption
2. **Interest rate effect**: higher price levels increase the demand for money, raising interest
   rates, which reduces investment and consumption
3. **Exchange rate effect**: higher domestic price levels reduce export competitiveness and make
   imports relatively cheaper, reducing net exports

### Shifts in AD

AD shifts rightward when:

- Consumer confidence rises (increasing $C$)
- Interest rates fall (increasing $I$ and $C$)
- Government spending increases (increasing $G$)
- The exchange rate depreciates (increasing $X$, reducing $M$)
- Foreign income rises (increasing $X$)

### Short-Run Aggregate Supply (SRAS)

The SRAS curve slopes upward because, in the short run, some input prices (particularly wages) are
sticky. As the price level rises, firms find it profitable to increase output since revenues rise
faster than costs.

SRAS shifts rightward when:

- Production costs fall (lower wages, cheaper raw materials)
- Technology improves
- Productivity increases
- Subsidies reduce costs
- The exchange rate depreciates (cheaper imported inputs)

### Long-Run Aggregate Supply (LRAS)

The LRAS curve is vertical at the **full employment level of output** (potential GDP). In the long
run, all input prices are flexible, and output is determined by the economy's productive capacity
(factors of production and technology).

Shifts in LRAS (representing **economic growth**) are caused by:

- Increases in the quantity or quality of factors of production (labour, capital, land)
- Technological progress
- Institutional improvements (better property rights, reduced corruption)
- Education and training (improving human capital)

## Fiscal Policy

Fiscal policy involves government decisions about taxation and spending to influence the economy.

### Expansionary Fiscal Policy

Used during recessions to stimulate aggregate demand:

- **Increase government spending** ($G \uparrow$) — direct injection into the economy
- **Decrease taxes** ($T \downarrow$) — increases disposable income, boosting consumption ($C$) and
  investment ($I$)

### Contractionary Fiscal Policy

Used to reduce inflationary pressures:

- **Decrease government spending** ($G \downarrow$)
- **Increase taxes** ($T \uparrow$)

### The Multiplier Effect

The fiscal multiplier captures the idea that an initial change in spending generates a larger total
change in national income:

$$k = \frac{1}{1 - \mathrm{MPC}} = \frac{1}{\mathrm{MPS}}$$

Where MPC is the marginal propensity to consume and MPS is the marginal propensity to save.

An initial government spending increase of $\Delta G$ leads to a total change in output of:

$$\Delta Y = k \times \Delta G = \frac{\Delta G}{1 - \mathrm{MPC}}$$

**The multiplier with taxation:**

$$k_T = \frac{1}{1 - \mathrm{MPC}(1 - t)}$$

Where $t$ is the marginal tax rate. Higher taxes reduce the multiplier because they leak spending
power out of the circular flow.

### Budget Position

- **Budget deficit**: $G > T$ (government spending exceeds tax revenue)
- **Budget surplus**: $T > G$
- **Balanced budget**: $G = T$
- **Public debt**: the accumulated total of past budget deficits

### Limitations of Fiscal Policy

- **Time lags**: recognition lag (identifying the problem), decision lag (political process),
  implementation lag (projects take time)
- **Crowding out**: increased government borrowing may raise interest rates, reducing private
  investment
- **Size of the multiplier**: if the multiplier is small, fiscal policy has limited impact
- **Political constraints**: tax increases and spending cuts are politically unpopular
- **Inefficiency**: government spending may not be directed to the most productive uses

## Monetary Policy

Monetary policy is conducted by a central bank and involves managing the money supply and interest
rates to influence economic activity.

### Expansionary Monetary Policy

- Lower the policy interest rate
- Reduce reserve requirements for commercial banks
- Purchase government bonds (open market operations) to increase the money supply
- Quantitative easing (large-scale asset purchases)

Lower interest rates reduce the cost of borrowing, encouraging consumption and investment. They also
reduce the incentive to save and may depreciate the exchange rate, boosting net exports.

### Contractionary Monetary Policy

- Raise the policy interest rate
- Increase reserve requirements
- Sell government bonds to reduce the money supply

Higher interest rates discourage borrowing and spending, reduce aggregate demand, and help control
inflation.

### Transmission Mechanism

1. Central bank changes the policy rate
2. Commercial banks adjust their lending and deposit rates
3. Changes in interest rates affect consumer spending ($C$) and business investment ($I$)
4. Changes in the exchange rate affect net exports ($X - M$)
5. Changes in asset prices (houses, shares) affect wealth and confidence
6. Aggregate demand shifts, affecting output, employment, and the price level

## Economic Growth

### Short-Run vs. Long-Run Growth

**Short-run growth** is an increase in real GDP caused by shifts in AD or SRAS, moving the economy
along its LRAS curve. It is associated with reductions in cyclical unemployment.

**Long-run growth** is an increase in the economy's productive capacity, represented by a rightward
shift of the LRAS curve. It is the only sustainable source of rising living standards.

### Sources of Long-Run Growth

**Solow growth model**: output depends on capital ($K$), labour ($L$), and technology ($A$):

$$Y = A \cdot f(K, L)$$

- **Capital accumulation**: increasing the stock of physical capital through investment
- **Human capital**: education, training, and health improvements that raise labour productivity
- **Technological progress**: innovation that increases total factor productivity (TFP)
- **Institutional quality**: rule of law, property rights, political stability, and effective
  governance

### Convergence Debate

The **absolute convergence** hypothesis suggests that poorer countries will grow faster than richer
ones and eventually catch up. The **conditional convergence** hypothesis holds that convergence only
occurs among countries with similar savings rates, population growth, and institutional quality.
Empirical evidence broadly supports conditional convergence.

## Common Pitfalls

- Confusing nominal and real GDP. Always clarify which measure you are using, especially when
  comparing growth rates over time or across countries.
- Stating that "unemployment is caused by recession" without specifying _cyclical_ unemployment.
  Structural and frictional unemployment exist even at full employment.
- Assuming that fiscal policy is always effective. Crowding out, time lags, and the size of the
  multiplier all limit its impact.
- Confusing a movement along the AD curve (caused by a change in the price level) with a shift of
  the AD curve (caused by changes in $C$, $I$, $G$, or $X - M$).
- Treating the Phillips Curve as a policy menu. The short-run trade-off does not exist in the long
  run.
- Forgetting that the LRAS curve is vertical. In the long run, demand-side policies only affect the
  price level, not output.

An economy has the following expenditure components (in billions): Consumption $= \$4200$,
Investment $= \$1100$, Government spending $= \$1500$, Exports $= \$800$, Imports $= \$950$.
Calculate GDP and net exports.

$$GDP = C + I + G + (X - M) = 4200 + 1100 + 1500 + (800 - 950)$$

$$GDP = 4200 + 1100 + 1500 + (-150) = \$6650 \mathrm{ billion}$$

Net exports $= X - M = 800 - 950 = -\$150$ billion (a trade deficit).

<summary>Problem 2: Inflation Rate Calculation</summary>

The CPI basket costs `USD 240` in year 1 and `USD 252` in year 2. Calculate the inflation rate between
year 1 and year 2.

$$\mathrm{Inflation rate} = \frac{252 - 240}{240} \times 100 = \frac{12}{240} \times 100 = 5\%$$

The inflation rate is $5\%$.

<summary>Problem 3: Fiscal Multiplier</summary>

The government increases spending by `USD 50` billion. The marginal propensity to consume is $0.8$.
Calculate the total change in national income.

$$k = \frac{1}{1 - \mathrm{MPC}} = \frac{1}{1 - 0.8} = \frac{1}{0.2} = 5$$

$$\Delta Y = k \times \Delta G = 5 \times 50 = \$250 \mathrm{ billion}$$

The total increase in national income is `USD 250` billion.

<details>
<summary>Problem 4: AD-AS Analysis</summary>

An economy is experiencing demand-pull inflation. Using the AD-AS framework, explain how a
contractionary monetary policy would address this, and discuss any potential drawbacks.

Demand-pull inflation occurs where AD intersects SRAS beyond the full employment level of output (to
the right of LRAS). The central bank raises interest rates, increasing the cost of borrowing. This
reduces consumption ($C$) and investment ($I$), shifting AD leftward. As AD shifts left, the price
level falls and output returns toward potential GDP.

Potential drawbacks:

- Higher interest rates may disproportionately affect borrowing by households with variable-rate
  mortgages.
- If the central bank overshoots, AD could fall too far, causing a recession and cyclical
  unemployment.
- Investment may be suppressed, reducing long-run growth.
- Higher interest rates may attract capital inflows, appreciating the exchange rate and worsening
the trade balance.
</details>

<summary>Problem 5: Types of Unemployment</summary>

Classify each scenario and explain: (a) A factory closes due to automation, laying off 200 workers.
(b) A university graduate is searching for her first job. (c) Workers are laid off during a
recession as consumer spending falls. (d) Agricultural workers are unemployed during the off-season.
(e) A minimum wage increase causes firms to reduce their workforce.

(a) **Structural unemployment**: the workers' skills are no longer needed due to technological
change (automation).

(b) **Frictional unemployment**: the graduate is temporarily between entering the labour force and
finding a suitable position.

(c) **Cyclical (demand-deficient) unemployment**: reduced aggregate demand during a recession causes
firms to cut employment.

(d) **Seasonal unemployment**: employment fluctuates predictably with the agricultural cycle.

(e) **Real-wage unemployment**: the minimum wage sets wages above the market-clearing level,
creating an excess supply of labour.
