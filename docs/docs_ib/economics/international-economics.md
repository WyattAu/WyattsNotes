## Free Trade and Comparative Advantage

### Absolute and Comparative Advantage

A country has an **absolute advantage** in producing a good if it can produce more output per unit
of resources than another country.

A country has a **comparative advantage** in producing a good if it can produce it at a lower
opportunity cost than another country. Comparative advantage is the basis for mutually beneficial
trade, even when one country has an absolute advantage in all goods.

**Ricardian model example:**

Consider two countries, A and B, producing two goods, wine and cloth. Output per worker per day:

|           | Wine (bottles) | Cloth (metres) |
| --------- | -------------- | -------------- |
| Country A | 6              | 3              |
| Country B | 2              | 4              |

Opportunity costs:

- Country A: 1 bottle of wine costs $3/6 = 0.5\;\mathrm{m}$ of cloth; 1 metre of cloth costs
  $6/3 = 2$ bottles of wine
- Country B: 1 bottle of wine costs $4/2 = 2\;\mathrm{m}$ of cloth; 1 metre of cloth costs
  $2/4 = 0.5$ bottles of wine

Country A has a comparative advantage in wine (lower opportunity cost: $0.5\;\mathrm{m}$ vs.
$2\;\mathrm{m}$). Country B has a comparative advantage in cloth (lower opportunity cost: $0.5$
bottles vs. $2$ bottles).

Both countries gain from specialising in the good in which they have a comparative advantage and
trading.

### Assumptions and Limitations

The simple comparative advantage model assumes:

- Only two countries and two goods
- Constant returns to scale
- Perfect factor mobility within countries, immobility between countries
- Zero transport costs
- Perfect competition
- No trade barriers

In reality, transport costs, economies of scale, imperfect competition, and factor immobility
complicate the picture. The **Heckscher-Ohlin model** extends the theory by linking comparative
advantage to factor endowments: countries export goods that intensively use their abundant factors.

## Terms of Trade

The **terms of trade** (ToT) measure the ratio of a country's export prices to its import prices:

$$\mathrm{ToT} = \frac{\mathrm{Index of Export Prices}}{\mathrm{Index of Import Prices}} \times 100$$

- An **improvement** in the terms of trade (ToT rises) means a country can buy more imports for a
  given volume of exports — a favourable change.
- A **deterioration** (ToT falls) means the country must export more to buy the same volume of
  imports.

A ToT improvement is not always beneficial: it may reflect declining export volumes if the
improvement is driven by reduced supply. The **Prebisch-Singer hypothesis** argues that the terms of
trade for primary commodity exporters tend to deteriorate over time relative to manufactured goods
exporters.

## Balance of Payments

The balance of payments is a record of all economic transactions between residents of a country and
the rest of the world over a given period.

### Current Account

1. **Balance of trade in goods**: exports minus imports of physical goods
2. **Balance of trade in services**: exports minus imports of services (tourism, banking, insurance,
   transport)
3. **Primary income**: earnings from investments abroad minus payments to foreign investors
   (dividends, interest, profits)
4. **Secondary income (current transfers)**: unilateral transfers such as remittances, foreign aid,
   and pensions

### Capital Account

Records transfers of capital that do not affect national income: debt forgiveness, transfers of
fixed assets, and capital transfers associated with migration.

### Financial Account

Records transactions in financial assets and liabilities:

- **Direct investment**: cross-border investment in enterprises (FDI)
- **Portfolio investment**: cross-border purchases of shares and bonds
- **Other investment**: bank deposits, trade credits, loans
- **Reserve assets**: changes in a country's official foreign exchange reserves held by the central
  bank

### The Balance of Payments Identity

$$\mathrm{Current Account} + \mathrm{Capital Account} + \mathrm{Financial Account} + \mathrm{Statistical Discrepancy} = 0$$

A current account deficit must be financed by a surplus on the capital and financial accounts (net
capital inflows). A current account surplus corresponds to net capital outflows.

### Current Account Deficits

A persistent current account deficit may indicate:

- Excessive domestic consumption relative to production
- Lack of international competitiveness
- Overvalued exchange rate
- High reliance on imported capital goods for development

A deficit financed by short-term capital inflows is more risky than one financed by long-term FDI,
as short-term flows can reverse rapidly ("sudden stop").

## Exchange Rates

### Types of Exchange Rate Systems

- **Floating exchange rate**: determined by market forces of supply and demand for the currency. The
  central bank does not intervene.
- **Fixed (pegged) exchange rate**: the central bank fixes the currency's value against another
  currency (or a basket). It maintains the peg by buying or selling foreign reserves.
- **Managed float (dirty float)**: the exchange rate is primarily market-determined, but the central
  bank intervenes occasionally to smooth excessive fluctuations.

### Determinants of Floating Exchange Rates

The exchange rate is the price of one currency in terms of another. In a floating system, it is
determined by supply and demand in the foreign exchange market.

**Factors that cause a currency to appreciate:**

- Higher interest rates (attracting capital inflows)
- Stronger economic growth (attracting investment)
- Improved current account position (higher demand for exports)
- Speculation (expectations of future appreciation)
- Political stability and investor confidence

**Factors that cause a currency to depreciate:**

- Lower interest rates relative to other countries
- Current account deficit
- Higher inflation (reducing export competitiveness)
- Capital outflows
- Political instability or economic uncertainty

### Effects of Exchange Rate Changes

**Depreciation:**

- Exports become cheaper for foreign buyers ($X \uparrow$)
- Imports become more expensive ($M \downarrow$)
- Current account may improve (assuming the Marshall-Lerner condition holds)
- Imported inflation (higher cost of imported goods and raw materials)
- Foreign debt denominated in foreign currency becomes more expensive to service

**Appreciation:**

- Exports become more expensive, imports become cheaper
- Current account may worsen
- Lower imported inflation
- Cheaper foreign travel and imports for domestic consumers

### The Marshall-Lerner Condition

A currency depreciation improves the current account balance only if the sum of the absolute values
of PED for exports and PED for imports exceeds 1:

$$|\mathrm{PED}_X| + |\mathrm{PED}_M| > 1$$

In the short run, demand tends to be inelastic (the **J-curve effect**): the current account
initially worsens after depreciation because import costs rise immediately while export volumes
adjust slowly. Over time, as demand becomes more elastic, the current account improves.

## Trade Protection

### Tariffs

A tariff is a tax on imports. It raises the domestic price of the imported good, reducing imports
and increasing domestic production. It generates government revenue but creates a deadweight loss
from reduced consumption and inefficient domestic production.

### Quotas

A quota restricts the quantity of a good that can be imported. It raises the domestic price (by
restricting supply) and benefits domestic producers, but the revenue from the higher price typically
accrues to foreign producers (unless the government auctions import licences).

### Subsidies

A production subsidy to domestic producers lowers their costs, enabling them to compete more
effectively against imports. Unlike tariffs, subsidies do not directly raise consumer prices, but
they impose a fiscal cost on the government and may provoke retaliation.

### Administrative Barriers

Non-tariff barriers include complex customs procedures, product standards, labelling requirements,
and health and safety regulations. While often justified on legitimate grounds, they can act as
disguised protectionism.

### Arguments for Protection

- **Infant industry argument**: new industries need temporary protection until they achieve
  economies of scale and competitiveness
- **Anti-dumping**: preventing foreign firms from selling below cost to drive out domestic
  competitors
- **Strategic industries**: protecting industries deemed essential for national security (e.g.,
  defence, energy)
- **Employment**: protecting jobs in industries facing intense foreign competition
- **Balance of payments**: reducing imports to correct a persistent current account deficit
- **Environmental and labour standards**: preventing a "race to the bottom" in regulation

### Arguments Against Protection

- Higher prices for consumers
- Reduced choice and variety
- Retaliation by trading partners (trade wars)
- Inefficient allocation of resources (domestic firms face less competitive pressure to innovate)
- Higher costs for downstream industries that use protected goods as inputs
- Deadweight welfare loss

## Economic Integration

### Levels of Integration

1. **Free Trade Area (FTA)**: member countries remove tariffs and quotas on trade with each other
   but maintain their own external trade policies (e.g., NAFTA/USMCA).
2. **Customs Union**: FTA plus a common external tariff on imports from non-members (e.g., the
   Southern African Customs Union).
3. **Common Market**: customs union plus free movement of factors of production — labour, capital,
   and enterprise (e.g., the EU single market).
4. **Economic and Monetary Union**: common market plus a common currency and a single central bank
   (e.g., the Eurozone).

### Trade Creation and Trade Diversion

**Trade creation**: removal of trade barriers leads to the replacement of higher-cost domestic
production with lower-cost imports from a partner country. This improves welfare.

**Trade diversion**: removal of trade barriers leads to the replacement of lower-cost imports from a
non-member country with higher-cost imports from a member country. This reduces welfare.

The net welfare effect depends on the relative sizes of trade creation and trade diversion.

## The World Trade Organisation (WTO)

The WTO is the international body that oversees the rules of global trade. Its key functions
include:

- Administering WTO trade agreements
- Providing a forum for trade negotiations (e.g., the Doha Round)
- Settling trade disputes between member states through the Dispute Settlement Body
- Monitoring national trade policies

**Principles:**

- **Most Favoured Nation (MFN)**: any advantage granted to one member must be extended to all WTO
  members (with exceptions for FTAs)
- **National treatment**: imported goods must be treated no less favourably than domestically
  produced goods once they have entered the market
- **Non-discrimination**: equal treatment of all trading partners

**Criticism of the WTO:**

- Decision-making can be dominated by large economies
- The Dispute Settlement Body has been weakened by US blockage of appointments
- Environmental and labour standards are not adequately addressed
- Developing countries may face asymmetric bargaining power

## Common Pitfalls

- Confusing absolute advantage with comparative advantage. A country can have an absolute advantage
  in all goods but still benefit from trade based on comparative advantage.
- Stating that a current account deficit is always bad. A deficit may reflect productive investment
  in capital goods that will generate future export capacity.
- Assuming that a currency depreciation automatically improves the current account. The
  Marshall-Lerner condition and the J-curve effect must be considered.
- Confusing trade creation with trade diversion. Trade creation improves welfare; trade diversion
  reduces it.
- Describing the WTO as a "world government" or suggesting it has enforcement powers comparable to
  national governments. It relies on member compliance and dispute resolution.

<summary>Problem 1: Comparative Advantage</summary>

Country X can produce 10 tonnes of wheat or 5 tonnes of steel per worker per day. Country Y can
produce 6 tonnes of wheat or 6 tonnes of steel per worker per day. Determine which country should
specialise in which good and explain the gains from trade.

Opportunity costs:

- Country X: 1 tonne of wheat costs $5/10 = 0.5$ tonnes of steel; 1 tonne of steel costs $10/5 = 2$
  tonnes of wheat
- Country Y: 1 tonne of wheat costs $6/6 = 1$ tonne of steel; 1 tonne of steel costs $6/6 = 1$ tonne
  of wheat

Country X has a comparative advantage in wheat ($0.5$ &lt; $1$). Country Y has a comparative
advantage in steel ($1$ &lt; $2$).

Both gain: Country X specialises in wheat, Country Y in steel, and they trade at a mutually
beneficial terms of trade between the two opportunity cost ratios (e.g., 1 wheat for 0.75 steel).

<summary>Problem 2: Terms of Trade</summary>

A country's export price index is 120 and its import price index is 150. Calculate the terms of
trade and interpret the result.

$$\mathrm{ToT} = \frac{120}{150} \times 100 = 80$$

A ToT of 80 means the country must export $20\%$ more goods to purchase the same volume of imports
compared to the base year. This represents a deterioration in the terms of trade, making the country
relatively worse off in its trade relationships.

<summary>Problem 3: Balance of Payments Identity</summary>

A country has a current account deficit of $\$40$ billion, a capital account surplus of $\$2$
billion, and net errors and omissions of $\$1$ billion. Calculate the financial account balance.

$$\mathrm{Current Account} + \mathrm{Capital Account} + \mathrm{Financial Account} + \mathrm{Errors} = 0$$

$$-40 + 2 + \mathrm{Financial Account} + 1 = 0$$

$$\mathrm{Financial Account} = 40 - 2 - 1 = \$37 \mathrm{ billion surplus}$$

The financial account surplus of $\$37$ billion represents net capital inflows that finance the
current account deficit.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Problem 4: Exchange Rate Effect</summary>

The exchange rate between the euro and the US dollar changes from $\$1.10$ per euro to $\$1.20$ per
euro. Explain the impact on European exporters and American tourists visiting Europe.

The euro has **appreciated** against the dollar (from $\$1.10$ to $\$1.20$).

For European exporters selling to the US: their goods become more expensive for American buyers (a
product priced at $100$ euros now costs $\$120$ instead of $\$110$). Export volumes are likely to
fall, reducing European export revenue.

For American tourists: their dollars buy fewer euros ($\$1$ now buys approximately $0.833$ euros
instead of $0.909$ euros). The cost of European goods, accommodation, and services increases for
American tourists, potentially reducing tourism demand.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Problem 5: Trade Creation vs. Trade Diversion</summary>

Country A forms a customs union with Country B. Before the union, Country A imported a good at
$\$10$ from Country C (the most efficient global producer), with a $\$3$ tariff. Country B produces
the good at $\$11$. After the union, the tariff is removed on imports from B but retained on imports
from C. Analyse the welfare effects.

Before the union: the domestic price in A is $\$10 + \$3 = \$13$. Imports come from C.

After the union: imports from B cost $\$11$ (no tariff); imports from C still cost $\$13$ (with
$\$3$ tariff). Country A now imports from B at $\$11$ instead of C at $\$13$.

This is **trade diversion**: production shifts from the more efficient producer (C at $\$10$) to the
less efficient producer (B at $\$11$). Although the domestic price falls from $\$13$ to $\$11$ (a
consumer benefit), the world as a whole becomes less efficient because resources are allocated to
the higher-cost producer.

</details>
