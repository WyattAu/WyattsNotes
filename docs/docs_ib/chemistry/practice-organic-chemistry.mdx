### Homologous Series and Functional Groups

<PracticeProblem question="Which functional group is present in a carboxylic acid?" options={['-COOH', '-OH', '-CHO', '-CO-']} correctAnswer={0} explanation="A carboxylic acid contains the carboxyl group, -COOH (or -C(=O)OH). This is the defining functional group. The -OH alone is an alcohol, -CHO is an aldehyde, and -CO- (C=O) is found in ketones, aldehydes, and other carbonyl compounds but not alone in carboxylic acids. The carboxyl group is a combination of a carbonyl (C=O) and a hydroxyl (O-H) attached to the same carbon atom." difficulty="easy" />

<PracticeProblem question="Which compound is an ester?" options={['CH₃COOCH₂CH₃', 'CH₃COOH', 'CH₃CH₂OH', 'CH₃CHO']} correctAnswer={0} explanation="CH₃COOCH₂CH₃ (ethyl ethanoate) is an ester. Esters have the general formula RCOOR' where R and R' are alkyl/aryl groups. They contain the ester linkage -COO- connecting two carbon chains. CH₃COOH is ethanoic acid (carboxylic acid), CH₃CH₂OH is ethanol (alcohol), and CH₃CHO is ethanal (aldehyde). Esters are formed from the condensation reaction between a carboxylic acid and an alcohol." difficulty="easy" />

<PracticeProblem question="What is the general formula for alkanes?" options={['CₙH₂ₙ₊₂', 'CₙH₂ₙ', 'CₙH₂ₙ₋₂', 'CₙH₂ₙ₊₁OH']} correctAnswer={0} explanation="Alkanes are saturated hydrocarbons with only single C-C and C-H bonds. Alkenes have CₙH₂ₙ (one double bond), alkynes have CₙH₂ₙ₋₂ (one triple bond), and alcohols have CₙH₂ₙ₊₁OH (with the -OH functional group replacing one hydrogen)." difficulty="easy" />

<PracticeProblem question="Which of the following compounds contains both a ketone and an alcohol functional group?" options={['CH₃COCH₂CH₂OH', 'CH₃CH₂COOH', 'CH₃CH(OH)CH₃', 'CH₃COOCH₃']} correctAnswer={0} explanation="CH₃COCH₂CH₂OH contains a ketone group (C=O, in CH₃CO-) and a primary alcohol group (-OH, on the terminal carbon). ### Nomenclature and Isomerism

<PracticeProblem question="What is the IUPAC name of CH₃CH(CH₃)CH₂CH₃?" options={['2-methylbutane', '3-methylbutane', 'isopentane', '1-methylbutane']} correctAnswer={0} explanation="The longest continuous carbon chain has 4 carbons (butane). There is a methyl group attached to carbon 2 of the main chain. Numbering from the end nearest to the substituent gives the lowest possible number. The systematic name is 2-methylbutane. 'Isopentane' is a common (trivial) name that is not accepted in IUPAC nomenclature. '3-methylbutane' would be incorrect because numbering must give the lowest number to the substituent. '1-methylbutane' would actually just be pentane (the methyl extends the chain), so it is not a valid name." difficulty="easy" />

<PracticeProblem question="How many structural isomers are there with the molecular formula C₅H₁₂?" options={['3', '4', '5', '2']} correctAnswer={0} explanation="C₅H₁₂ (pentane) has three structural isomers: n-pentane (CH₃CH₂CH₂CH₂CH₃), 2-methylbutane ((CH₃)₂CHCH₂CH₃), and 2,2-dimethylpropane (neopentane, C(CH₃)₄). Structural isomers have the same molecular formula but different arrangements of carbon atoms. C₄H₁₀ has 2 isomers, C₅H₁₂ has 3, C₆H₁₄ has 5, and C₇H₁₆ has 9. The number of isomers increases rapidly with carbon number." difficulty="easy" />

### Reaction Mechanisms

<PracticeProblem question="What is the first step in the electrophilic addition of HBr to propene (CH₃CH=CH₂)?" options={[ 'The π electrons of the C=C bond attack the H⁺ (from HBr), forming a carbocation intermediate and Br⁻.', 'The Br⁻ ion attacks the double bond directly.', 'The hydrogen atom adds to both carbon atoms simultaneously.', 'The HBr molecule adds across the double bond without any intermediate.', ]} correctAnswer={0} explanation="Electrophilic addition proceeds via a two-step mechanism. Step 1: The electron-rich π bond of the alkene acts as a nucleophile, attacking the electrophilic H of HBr. This forms a carbocation intermediate and a bromide ion. The H adds to the less substituted carbon (following Markovnikov’s rule), giving the more stable secondary carbocation (CH₃CH⁺CH₃) rather than the less stable primary carbocation (CH₃CH₂CH₂⁺). Step 2: Br⁻ attacks the carbocation to give CH₃CHBrCH₃ (2-bromopropane)." difficulty="medium" />

<PracticeProblem question="In an SN2 reaction of 1-bromopropane with aqueous NaOH, what happens to the stereochemistry at the carbon undergoing substitution?" options={[ 'Inversion of configuration occurs (Walden inversion).', 'Racemisation occurs (50:50 mixture of both configurations).', 'Retention of configuration occurs.', 'No stereochemical change since the substrate is not chiral.', ]} correctAnswer={3} explanation="1-bromopropane (CH₃CH₂CH₂Br) is a primary alkyl halide with the Br on a non-chiral carbon (CH₂Br has two identical H atoms). An SN2 reaction causes inversion of configuration at the reacting carbon, but since the starting material is achiral (the reacting carbon is not a stereocentre), the product is also achiral and there is no observable stereochemical consequence. If the substrate were chiral (e.g., 2-bromobutane), SN2 would give inversion. Racemisation is characteristic of SN1, and retention occurs in specific mechanisms like neighbouring group participation." difficulty="medium" />

<PracticeProblem question="Which factor does NOT affect the rate of an SN1 reaction?" options={['Concentration of the nucleophile', 'Stability of the carbocation intermediate', 'Nature of the leaving group', 'Polarity of the solvent']} correctAnswer={0} explanation="SN1 reactions are unimolecular in the rate-determining step (only the substrate is involved). The rate law is rate = k[substrate]; the nucleophile does not appear in the rate equation. Therefore, the concentration of the nucleophile does NOT affect the rate of an SN1 reaction. The rate IS affected by: carbocation stability (more stable carbocations form faster, so tertiary > secondary >> primary), the nature of the leaving group (better leaving groups increase rate), and solvent polarity (polar protic solvents stabilise the carbocation and leaving group, increasing rate). The nucleophile concentration matters for SN2 but not SN1." difficulty="hard" />

<PracticeProblem question="The dehydration of ethanol with concentrated H₂SO₄ at 170 °C produces ethene. Which mechanism describes this elimination reaction?" options={[ 'E1 (unimolecular elimination)', 'E2 (bimolecular elimination)', 'SN1', 'Free radical elimination']} correctAnswer={0} explanation="The dehydration of a primary alcohol with a strong acid at high temperature proceeds via an E1-like mechanism (sometimes classified as E1 for secondary/tertiary alcohols). The acid protonates the -OH group, forming -OH₂⁺ (good leaving group). Water departs as a leaving group, creating a carbocation (slow, rate-determining step). A base (HSO₄⁻ or H₂O) then removes a β-hydrogen, forming the C=C double bond. For ethanol specifically, the primary carbocation is unstable, and the mechanism may have E2 character. In the IB syllabus context, this is typically described as acid-catalysed elimination where the alcohol is protonated first, water leaves, and then deprotonation occurs." difficulty="hard" />
