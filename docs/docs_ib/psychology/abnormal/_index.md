# Abnormal Psychology

This section covers the HL extension unit on abnormal psychology, examining how psychological
disorders are defined, classified, and explained from biological, cognitive, and sociocultural
perspectives.

## Overview

Abnormal psychology is an HL extension unit that examines how psychological disorders are defined,
classified, and explained. The IB approach requires students to evaluate biological, cognitive, and
sociocultural explanations of specific disorders and to assess corresponding treatments on their
effectiveness, appropriateness, and ethical grounds. The two disorders examined in this section are
anxiety disorders (phobias and OCD) and depression.

The biological approach explains disorders through genetic inheritance, neurotransmitter
dysregulation, and neural circuit abnormalities. For OCD, the serotonin hypothesis (supported by
SSRI efficacy) and the cortico-striato-thalamo-cortical circuit model (orbitofrontal cortex and
caudate nucleus hyperactivity) provide the primary biological explanations. For depression, the
monoamine hypothesis (serotonin and norepinephrine deficiency) and HPA axis dysregulation
(hypercortisolism, hippocampal damage via excitotoxicity) are central. Caspi et al. (2003) bridges
biological and sociocultural levels by demonstrating that the 5-HTTLPR short allele moderates the
effect of stressful life events on depression, illustrating gene-environment interaction.

The cognitive approach locates the cause of disorders in maladaptive thought patterns. Beck's (1967)
cognitive triad (negative views of self, world, future) and cognitive distortions
(overgeneralisation, dichotomous thinking, selective abstraction) explain the maintenance of
depression. Salkovskis (1985) applies similar principles to OCD: intrusive thoughts are interpreted
as significant and dangerous (thought-action fusion, inflated responsibility), and compulsions are
maintained by negative reinforcement. Seligman's (1975) learned helplessness model and the
reformulated attributional style (Abramson, Seligman, and Teasdale, 1978) offer a complementary
cognitive account of depression vulnerability.

The sociocultural approach emphasises the role of environmental stressors, socioeconomic factors,
and cultural norms. Brown and Harris (1978) demonstrated that severe life events involving
humiliation or entrapment predict depression onset. Gender differences in depression prevalence (2:1
female-to-male) are explained through rumination (Nolen-Hoeksema, 1991), social role stress, and
diagnostic bias. Cultural factors influence both the expression of disorders and the appropriateness
of treatments: CBT's focus on individual cognitive restructuring may conflict with collectivist
values that prioritise family and community.

Treatment evaluation requires a comparative framework. Elkin et al. (1989) found that for mild
depression, placebo was as effective as CBT, imipramine, and IPT, raising questions about the
specificity of treatment effects. Hollon et al. (2005) demonstrated that CBT produces more durable
treatment gains than pharmacotherapy after discontinuation (30% vs 70% relapse at 1 year). Kirsch et
al. (2008) found that SSRI-placebo differences were clinically significant only for severe
depression, challenging the monoamine hypothesis.

## Exam Strategy

For Paper 1 SAQs within the abnormal option, questions in most cases ask students to outline one
explanation of a disorder or one treatment approach. Prepare biological and cognitive explanations
for both OCD and depression. For OCD biology: serotonin hypothesis, CSTC circuit. For OCD cognitive:
Salkovskis (1985), thought-action fusion. For depression biology: monoamine hypothesis, HPA axis.
For depression cognitive: Beck's cognitive triad, learned helplessness. Always include a study as
evidence.

For Paper 2 ERQs (22 marks), questions require a comprehensive essay addressing how a disorder can
be explained or treated. Use at least two explanations (e.g., biological and cognitive for OCD) with
two studies each, ensuring each study is described with methodology, findings, and evaluation.
Treatment questions should follow the same structure: describe the treatment, explain the rationale
(link it to the explanation), present evidence for effectiveness, and evaluate. Always address
ethical considerations (informed consent, right to refuse treatment, non-maleficence) and cultural
appropriateness as part of evaluation.

HL essays should integrate content across approaches. The highest-scoring responses demonstrate how
biological and cognitive explanations complement each other (e.g., serotonin reuptake inhibition
restores neurotransmitter balance, while CBT addresses the maladaptive cognitions that maintain the
disorder). The treatment evaluation sub-topic provides a framework for comparative evaluation:
efficacy, safety, cultural appropriateness, and ethical considerations.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Explaining OCD from Two Perspectives

**Problem:** Explain obsessive-compulsive disorder using biological and cognitive approaches.
**Solution:** **Biological:** OCD is associated with abnormalities in the frontal lobe and basal
ganglia, particularly overactivity in the orbitofrontal cortex. Serotonin reuptake inhibitors
(SSRIs) are the primary biomedical treatment, supporting the neurotransmitter hypothesis.
**Cognitive:** The cognitive approach suggests that individuals with OCD experience intrusive
thoughts (obsessions) and believe they are responsible for preventing harm. Compulsive behaviours
are maintained by negative reinforcement (anxiety reduction). Treatment: CBT with exposure and
response prevention (ERP).

### Example 2: Evaluating CBT for Depression

**Problem:** Evaluate the effectiveness of CBT as a treatment for depression. **Solution:** CBT is
effective for mild to moderate depression, with research showing comparable outcomes to
antidepressant medication in many trials (e.g., Butler et al. meta-analysis). Strengths: no
pharmacological side effects; teaches skills for long-term coping; addresses root cognitive
patterns. Limitations: requires motivated clients; may be less effective for severe depression;
therapist availability varies. Cultural considerations: CBT's emphasis on individual cognitive
change may not align with collectivist cultural values.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Describing treatments without linking them to explanations:** Every treatment should be
  justified by the explanation it is derived from (e.g., SSRIs are justified by the serotonin
  hypothesis of depression).
- **Ignoring ethical considerations:** Informed consent, right to withdraw, protection from harm,
  and cultural sensitivity are all relevant when discussing psychological treatments and must be
  addressed.

## Summary

The abnormal psychology HL extension covers anxiety disorders, depression, and treatment evaluation.
Disorders are explained from biological (genetics, neurotransmitters), cognitive (maladaptive
schemas, cognitive biases), and sociocultural (social stressors, cultural norms) perspectives.
Treatments are evaluated on effectiveness, appropriateness, and ethical grounds.
