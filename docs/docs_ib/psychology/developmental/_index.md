# Developmental Psychology

This section covers developmental psychology, examining how behaviour changes across the lifespan
with a focus on attachment, cognitive development, and adolescence.

## Overview

Developmental psychology examines how behaviour, cognition, and social functioning change across the
lifespan. The IB curriculum focuses on three interrelated areas: attachment (the emotional bonds
formed in infancy), cognitive development (the progressive changes in thinking and reasoning), and
adolescent development (the biological, cognitive, and social transformations of the teenage years).
A unifying theme across all three is the interaction between biological maturation and environmental
influence, reflecting the broader nature-nurture debate that runs through the IB Psychology course.

Attachment theory, developed by Bowlby (1969) and operationalised by Ainsworth (1978), proposes that
infants are biologically predisposed to form an attachment to a primary caregiver during a critical
period. The quality of this attachment, determined by caregiver sensitivity, establishes internal
working models that guide relationship expectations across the lifespan. Harlow's (1958) monkey
studies demonstrated that contact comfort, not feeding, is the basis of attachment, challenging the
behaviourist account. Van IJzendoorn and Kroonenberg's (1988) meta-analysis of Strange Situation
studies across eight countries found that secure attachment was the most common type in all
cultures, supporting the universality of attachment, but also revealed important cultural variation
in the distribution of insecure types.

Cognitive development is framed by the contrast between Piaget's stage theory and Vygotsky's
sociocultural approach. Piaget proposed four invariant stages driven by equilibration, but his
methods underestimated infant competence (Baillargeon, 1987, found object permanence at 3.5 months
using violation-of-expectation) and the role of social interaction. Vygotsky's emphasis on the zone
of proximal development and scaffolding has been more influential in education, though his theory is
less precisely testable than Piaget's. The Sally-Anne test (Baron-Cohen, Leslie, and Frith, 1985)
provides evidence for theory of mind development around age 4, while Kohlberg's moral stages (1969)
extend the cognitive-developmental framework to moral reasoning, with Gilligan's (1982) ethics of
care critique highlighting gender and cultural bias.

Adolescent development integrates biological maturation (puberty, synaptic pruning, prefrontal
myelination) with psychosocial change. Erikson's identity versus role confusion stage (1968) and
Marcia's four identity statuses (1966) provide the primary theoretical framework for understanding
identity formation. Steinberg's dual-systems model (2008) explains adolescent risk-taking through
the temporal gap between early-maturing limbic reward circuitry and late-maturing prefrontal
regulatory control. Chein et al. (2011) demonstrated this mechanism neurobiologically: peer
observation increased ventral striatum activation and decreased prefrontal cortex activation in
adolescents but not adults, providing a neural explanation for why risk-taking peaks in social
contexts during adolescence.

## Exam Strategy

For Paper 1 SAQs, developmental questions in most cases ask students to explain one theory of
attachment, cognitive development, or adolescent development. Attachment is the most frequently
tested area: prepare Bowlby's key principles (monotropy, critical period, internal working models)
with Ainsworth's Strange Situation as empirical evidence. For cognitive development, memorise
Piaget's stage framework and one key limitation (Baillargeon's violation-of-expectation method or
Hughes' 1975 perspective-taking study). For adolescent development, have the dual-systems model
(Steinberg, 2008) and Erikson's identity versus role confusion ready with supporting studies.

For Paper 1 ERQs, questions may ask students to discuss theories of development or evaluate research
methods used in developmental psychology. Attachment essays should use Bowlby's theory as the
framework, supported by Harlow (1958), Ainsworth (1978), and Van IJzendoorn and Kroonenberg (1988),
with cross-cultural evaluation as a key paragraph. Cognitive development essays should compare
Piaget and Vygotsky, using specific studies to evaluate each theory's strengths and limitations.

For HL Paper 2, developmental content is critical for discussing how early experience influences
later behaviour. The continuity hypothesis (internal working models guiding adult relationships) can
be supported by Hazan and Shaver (1987). Cultural considerations are essential: the Strange
Situation may be biased toward Western independence norms (Rothbaum et al., 1986), and Marcia's
identity statuses reflect individualistic Western notions of identity formation. Always include at
least one paragraph on cultural variation in evaluation.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Using Research to Support a Developmental Theory

**Approach:** When asked to explain one theory of attachment, identify Bowlby's theory, describe its
key propositions (innate, critical period, secure base, internal working model), then provide
Ainsworth's Strange Situation as empirical evidence. Conclude by noting Van IJzendoorn's
meta-analysis supports universality but cultural variation exists.

### Example 2: Evaluating Cognitive Development Theories

**Approach:** Outline Piaget's stages and Vygotsky's ZPD. Compare their assumptions about
development (Piaget: individual discovery, age-linked; Vygotsky: social interaction, continuous).
Evaluate both with specific evidence (Baillargeon's criticism of Piaget's object permanence;
empirical support for scaffolding in classrooms).

## Common Pitfalls

- **Listing studies without connecting them to a concept.** Each study cited must be explicitly
  linked to the theory it supports or challenges.
- **Treating developmental theories as facts rather than explanations.** Use evaluative language:
  "suggests," "is supported by," "is limited by."
- **Ignoring cultural dimensions in developmental psychology.** Exams consistently require cultural
  consideration in evaluation.

## Summary

This section covers three core areas of developmental psychology: attachment theory (Bowlby,
Ainsworth, cross-cultural research), cognitive development (Piaget's stages, Vygotsky's
sociocultural theory), and adolescent development (Erikson's identity formation, Marcia's identity
statuses, the dual-systems model of risk-taking). Each sub-topic requires both theoretical
explanation and empirical evaluation.
