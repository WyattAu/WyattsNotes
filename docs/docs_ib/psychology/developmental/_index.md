# Developmental Psychology

This section covers developmental psychology, examining how behaviour changes across the lifespan
with a focus on attachment, cognitive development, and adolescence.

## Pages

- [Attachment Theory](./attachment-theory) -- Bowlby's theory, Ainsworth's Strange Situation,

  theory, and their implications for education.
- [Adolescent Development](./adolescent-development) -- physical, cognitive, and social changes

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Using Research to Support a Developmental Theory
**Approach:** When asked to explain one theory of attachment, identify Bowlby's theory, describe its key propositions (innate, critical period, secure base, internal working model), then provide Ainsworth's Strange Situation as empirical evidence. Conclude by noting Van IJzendoorn's meta-analysis supports universality but cultural variation exists.

### Example 2: Evaluating Cognitive Development Theories
**Approach:** Outline Piaget's stages and Vygotsky's ZPD. Compare their assumptions about development (Piaget: individual discovery, age-linked; Vygotsky: social interaction, continuous). Evaluate both with specific evidence (Baillargeon's criticism of Piaget's object permanence; empirical support for scaffolding in classrooms).

## Common Pitfalls

- **Listing studies without connecting them to a concept.** Each study cited must be explicitly linked to the theory it supports or challenges.
- **Treating developmental theories as facts rather than explanations.** Use evaluative language: "suggests," "is supported by," "is limited by."
- **Ignoring cultural dimensions in developmental psychology.** Exams consistently require cultural consideration in evaluation.

## Summary

This section covers three core areas of developmental psychology: attachment theory (Bowlby, Ainsworth, cross-cultural research), cognitive development (Piaget's stages, Vygotsky's sociocultural theory), and adolescent development (Erikson's identity formation, Marcia's identity statuses, the dual-systems model of risk-taking). Each sub-topic requires both theoretical explanation and empirical evaluation.
