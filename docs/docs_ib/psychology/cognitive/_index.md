# Cognitive Level of Analysis

## Overview

The cognitive level of analysis (CLOA) examines how mental processes -- memory, thinking, language,
and decision making -- shape behaviour. Unlike the biological level, which locates causation in
physiology, CLOA posits that behaviour is mediated by information processing: encoding, storage,
retrieval, and the systematic biases that distort these processes. The guiding assumption is that
mental processes can be studied scientifically through controlled experiments, computational
modelling, and, increasingly, neuroimaging.

Memory research within CLOA has produced two foundational models. Atkinson and Shiffrin's (1968)
multi-store model provided the first structural account, distinguishing sensory memory, short-term
memory, and long-term memory as separate stores with distinct capacities and durations. Baddeley and
Hitch's (1974) working memory model replaced the unitary STM with a multi-component active
processing system (central executive, phonological loop, visuospatial sketchpad, episodic buffer),
which has become the dominant framework for understanding individual differences in cognitive
capacity and educational attainment. Schema theory (Bartlett, 1932) offers a complementary
process-based account, explaining how prior knowledge shapes encoding and produces reconstructive
errors in recall.

The heuristics and biases programme (Tversky and Kahneman) and its successor, dual-process theory
(System 1/System 2), reveal that human judgment systematically departs from rationality. The
availability, representativeness, and anchoring heuristics produce predictable errors that prospect
theory formalises (loss aversion, reference dependence, framing effects). The language and thought
sub-topic engages with the Sapir-Whorf hypothesis, where Boroditsky (2001) and Winawer et al. (2007)
provide contemporary evidence for linguistic relativity while refuting linguistic determinism.

CLOA connects to the broader course through its methodological tools. Experimental paradigms
developed within cognitive psychology (dual-task interference, levels of processing, leading
questions) are used across all three levels of analysis. The reconstructive nature of memory has
direct implications for the reliability of eyewitness testimony studied in sociocultural and
abnormal contexts. Cognitive biases (confirmation bias, stereotyping) underpin the intergroup
processes examined in the sociocultural level and the cognitive distortions targeted in CBT for
depression and OCD.

## Exam Strategy

For Paper 1 SAQs, CLOA questions in most cases ask students to outline or explain one model, one
theory, or one study related to a cognitive process. Memory models are the most frequently tested
area: prepare Atkinson and Shiffrin (1968), Baddeley and Hitch (1974), and Bartlett (1932) with full
methodology, findings, and evaluation. For thinking and decision making, memorise the availability
heuristic (Tversky and Kahneman, 1973) and the Linda problem (conjunction fallacy, 1983). For
language and thought, have Boroditsky (2001) and Winawer et al. (2007) ready.

For Paper 1 ERQs, questions often address how cognitive processes influence behaviour or how
reliable one cognitive process is. Use three to four studies with alternating description and
evaluation. When addressing reliability, distinguish between structural limitations of models (the
MSM treats STM as unitary) and methodological limitations of supporting studies (laboratory
ecological validity). The reconstructive memory debate is a high-yield topic: use Bartlett (1932),
Brewer and Treyens (1981), and Loftus and Palmer (1974) with Yuille and Cutshall (1986) as a
counterpoint.

For HL Paper 2, CLOA content is critical for the abnormal psychology option. Beck's cognitive triad
and the cognitive distortions maintaining depression and OCD are derived from CLOA principles.
Salkovskis's (1985) cognitive model of OCD (thought-action fusion, inflated responsibility) and
Beck's (1967) negative automatic thoughts should be linked to their respective CBT treatments.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Applying Schema Theory

**Problem:** Explain how schema theory accounts for memory errors, using Bartlett's (1932) "War of
the Ghosts" study. **Solution:** Bartlett had British participants read a Native American folk tale
("War of the Ghosts") and recall it at increasing intervals. Participants systematically altered the
story: unfamiliar cultural elements (e.g., "ghosts") were omitted; the narrative was rationalised to
fit Western story schemas; details became more conventional. This demonstrates that memory is
reconstructive -- existing schemas shape encoding and retrieval, leading to systematic distortions
that make recall more consistent with prior knowledge.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Confusing availability and representativeness heuristics:** The availability heuristic judges
  frequency based on how readily examples come to mind; the representativeness heuristic judges
  probability based on similarity to a prototype. Know the distinction with examples.
- **Stating that schema theory is "wrong":** Schema theory is not falsified by reconstructive errors
  -- the errors are the predicted outcome of schema-driven processing. The limitation is that
  schemas are difficult to define and measure operationally.
