## What Is IB Psychology?

IB Psychology is a Group 3 subject (Individuals and Societies) that examines how biological, cognitive, and sociocultural factors interact to shape human behaviour and mental processes. The course is grounded in empirical research and requires students to evaluate theories and studies using critical thinking, ethical reasoning, and an understanding of research methodology.

The course is offered at both Standard Level (SL) and Higher Level (HL). HL students study additional content, including an extension unit and an additional assessment paper.

## The Levels of Analysis

IB Psychology is organised around four levels of analysis (LOAs). Each LOA provides a distinct lens through which behaviour can be explained. A core principle of the course is that no single level provides a complete explanation; behaviour is the product of interacting biological, cognitive, and sociocultural factors.

### Biological Level of Analysis

The biological LOA examines the physiological bases of behaviour, including the role of the brain, neurotransmitters, hormones, and genetics. It is rooted in the principle that behaviour can be explained in terms of biological structures and processes. Key topics include brain localisation, neuroplasticity, neurotransmission, genetic inheritance, and evolutionary explanations of behaviour.

### Cognitive Level of Analysis

The cognitive LOA focuses on mental processes such as perception, memory, language, and decision making. It operates on the principle that the mind can be studied scientifically by inferring internal mental states from observable behaviour. Key topics include models of memory, reconstructive memory, schema theory, thinking and decision-making heuristics, and the interaction between emotion and cognition.

### Sociocultural Level of Analysis

The sociocultural LOA investigates how the social environment and cultural context influence behaviour. It is based on the principle that human beings are social animals whose behaviour is shaped by the presence and actions of others. Key topics include social identity theory, conformity, obedience, prejudice, attribution, and cultural dimensions.

### Abnormal Psychology (HL Extension)

HL students study abnormal psychology as an additional unit. This unit examines how abnormality is defined, classified, and explained, as well as how psychological disorders are treated. It integrates all three core LOAs by exploring biological, cognitive, and sociocultural explanations and treatments for disorders including anxiety disorders, depressive disorders, and trauma-related disorders.

## Assessment Structure

| Component | SL | HL |
|---|---|---|
| Paper 1 | 2 hours. Three extended-response questions, one per core LOA. Each question is worth 22 marks. | 2 hours. Three extended-response questions, one per core LOA. Each question is worth 22 marks. |
| Paper 2 | 1 hour. One extended-response question from a choice of three options. Worth 22 marks. | 2 hours. Two extended-response questions from a choice of four options. Each worth 22 marks. |
| Paper 3 | Not assessed. | 1 hour. Three short-answer questions based on a research scenario. Tests understanding of research methods. Worth 24 marks. |
| Internal Assessment | Experimental study. Replication of a simple experiment with a written report (maximum 1,800 words). | Experimental study. Replication of a simple experiment with a written report (maximum 2,200 words). |

### Paper 1 — Core

Paper 1 assesses the three core levels of analysis. Each question requires students to demonstrate knowledge of theories, studies, and research methods, and to evaluate these using critical analysis.

### Paper 2 — Options

Paper 2 assesses applied psychology options. Students choose from topics such as abnormal psychology, developmental psychology, and health psychology. HL students must answer two questions; SL students answer one.

### Paper 3 — Research Methods (HL Only)

Paper 3 presents students with a novel research scenario and asks three questions testing their understanding of research design, sampling, methodology, and the evaluation of research. This paper requires a thorough command of the research methods syllabus.

### Internal Assessment

The IA is an experimental replication. Students work in groups to collect data but write individual reports. The report must include an introduction (with relevant theory and previous research), an exploration (hypothesis, research design, sampling, materials, procedure), an analysis (descriptive and inferential statistics), and an evaluation (discussion of findings, limitations, and modifications).

## Research Methods Overview

Research methodology is embedded throughout the IB Psychology course and is assessed across all components. Students must understand:

- **Experimental and non-experimental research designs**, including laboratory experiments, field experiments, quasi-experiments, correlational studies, case studies, and observational studies.
- **Sampling techniques**, including random, stratified, opportunity, and systematic sampling.
- **Ethical considerations**, including informed consent, deception, debriefing, confidentiality, protection from harm, and the right to withdraw.
- **Quantitative data analysis**, including descriptive statistics (measures of central tendency and dispersion) and inferential statistics (significance tests such as the t-test, Mann-Whitney U test, Wilcoxon signed-ranks test, and chi-squared test).
- **Qualitative research methods**, including interviews, observations, and thematic analysis (HL only).
- **Evaluation of research**, including issues of reliability, validity, generalisability, and bias.
