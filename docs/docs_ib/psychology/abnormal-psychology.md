## Defining Abnormality

Defining what constitutes "abnormal" behaviour is one of the most fundamental and contentious issues in clinical psychology. No single definition captures the complexity of abnormality, and each approach has strengths and limitations.

### Statistical Infrequency

Abnormality is defined in terms of deviation from the statistical norm. Any behaviour that is statistically rare (i.e., falls in the extreme tail of a normal distribution) is considered abnormal. Intelligence, for example, is normally distributed with a mean of 100 and a standard deviation of 15; individuals with IQs below 70 or above 130 are statistically infrequent.

**Strengths:** Provides an objective, quantitative criterion that is relatively easy to apply.

**Limitations:** Statistical rarity does not necessarily indicate pathology. A high IQ is statistically rare but is not considered a disorder. Conversely, depression is relatively common (affecting approximately 1 in 6 people during their lifetime) but is clearly considered abnormal. This definition also cannot account for the fact that the same behaviour may be considered abnormal in one context but not in another.

### Deviation from Social Norms

Abnormality is defined in terms of behaviour that violates unwritten social rules or norms. For example, talking to oneself in public, wearing inappropriate clothing, or exhibiting extreme emotional displays in public may be considered abnormal because they violate social expectations.

**Strengths:** Takes account of the social context in which behaviour occurs, which statistical infrequency does not.

**Limitations:** Social norms vary across cultures and historical periods. Behaviour that is considered abnormal in one culture (e.g., hearing voices, which may be interpreted as a sign of spiritual possession in some cultures) may be normative in another. Social norms also change over time: homosexuality was classified as a mental disorder in the DSM until 1973. This definition also risks pathologising deviant but harmless behaviour (e.g., political dissent, non-conformist lifestyles).

### Failure to Function Adequately

Abnormality is defined in terms of an inability to carry out the activities of daily living. Rosenhan and Seligman (1989) proposed several criteria for identifying abnormality based on failure to function:

1. Suffering (the individual experiences distress)
2. Maladaptiveness (the behaviour prevents the individual from achieving goals)
3. Vividness and unconventionality (the behaviour is strikingly different from what is expected)
4. Unpredictability and loss of control (the individual acts in ways that are unexpected)
5. Irrationality (the behaviour is incomprehensible to others)
6. Observer discomfort (the behaviour causes discomfort to others)

**Strengths:** This definition focuses on the practical impact of behaviour on the individual's life, which is arguably more important than statistical rarity.

**Limitations:** Some individuals who meet the criteria for psychological disorders (e.g., individuals with narcissistic personality disorder) function well in their daily lives and may not experience distress. Conversely, some individuals who do not have a psychological disorder may experience significant dysfunction (e.g., grief following bereavement).

### Deviation from Ideal Mental Health

Abnormality is defined as a deviation from an ideal standard of mental health. Jahoda (1958) proposed six criteria for ideal mental health:

1. Positive attitudes toward the self
2. Growth and self-actualisation
3. Integration (the ability to cope with stress)
4. Autonomy and independence
5. Accurate perception of reality
6. Environmental mastery (the ability to adapt to the environment)

**Strengths:** This definition provides a positive standard to aspire to, rather than merely defining abnormality as the absence of normality.

**Limitations:** The criteria are so demanding that very few people would meet all of them consistently. The concept of "ideal" mental health is culturally specific and reflects a Western, individualistic conception of psychological well-being.

<summary>Common Pitfalls: Defining Abnormality</summary>

- **Do not assume that the four definitions are mutually exclusive.** In practice, clinicians use multiple criteria in combination. A comprehensive assessment considers statistical infrequency, social norm violation, functional impairment, and deviation from ideal mental health.
- **Do not treat any single definition as definitive.** Each definition has significant limitations, and a critical evaluation of each is expected in IB Psychology.
- **Be careful with the term "abnormal."** In clinical psychology, the preferred term is "psychopathology" or "psychological disorder," which avoids the pejorative connotations of "abnormal."

## Classification Systems

### DSM-5 (Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, 5th Edition)

The DSM-5, published by the American Psychiatric Association in 2013, is the primary classification system used in the United States and many other countries. It uses a categorical approach, in which disorders are defined by specific diagnostic criteria (a checklist of symptoms). The DSM-5 is organised into 20 diagnostic categories and includes dimensional assessments for severity.

Key features of the DSM-5:

- **Multi-axial system removed:** The DSM-IV's multi-axial system (which classified disorders on five axes) was replaced by a non-axial approach in the DSM-5, integrating all diagnostic information into a single assessment.
- **Dimensional assessments:** The DSM-5 introduced dimensional assessments for some disorders (e.g., severity ratings for autism spectrum disorder), acknowledging that psychopathology exists on a continuum rather than in discrete categories.
- **Cultural formulation:** The DSM-5 includes a Cultural Formulation Interview to assess the influence of culture on the individual's symptoms, help-seeking behaviour, and treatment expectations.

### ICD-11 (International Classification of Diseases, 11th Revision)

The ICD-11, published by the World Health Organization in 2018 (effective 2022), is used internationally. It adopts a similar categorical approach but differs from the DSM-5 in several respects:

- The ICD-11 is organised around clinically relevant clusters of symptoms rather than diagnostic categories.
- It places greater emphasis on functional impairment as a diagnostic criterion.
- It includes a chapter on "Disorders specifically associated with stress" that conceptualises PTSD and complex PTSD within a single diagnostic framework.

### Issues with Classification

**Reliability:** Inter-rater reliability refers to the degree to which different clinicians arrive at the same diagnosis for the same patient. Reliability has improved with each edition of the DSM, but diagnostic disagreements remain common, particularly for personality disorders and disorders where symptom overlap is high (e.g., the comorbidity of depression and anxiety).

**Validity:** The validity of a diagnostic category refers to whether it accurately identifies a genuine disorder with a distinct aetiology, course, and response to treatment. Critics have argued that many DSM categories lack biological validity: the boundaries between categories are often arbitrary, and high rates of comorbidity suggest that the categorical system may not reflect the true structure of psychopathology. The Research Domain Criteria (RDoC) initiative, developed by the US National Institute of Mental Health, proposes replacing categorical diagnoses with a dimensional framework based on biological and psychological processes.

**Culture-bound syndromes:** Some disorders appear to be specific to particular cultural contexts (e.g., taijin kyofusho in Japan, a fear of offending or harming others through one's appearance or social behaviour; ataque de nervios in Latin American cultures, characterised by uncontrollable shouting, crying, and aggression). This raises questions about whether diagnostic categories developed in Western cultural contexts can be universally applied.

## Anxiety Disorders

### Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD)

GAD is characterised by persistent and excessive worry about a range of events or activities, occurring more days than not for at least six months. The worry is difficult to control and is accompanied by physical symptoms such as restlessness, fatigue, difficulty concentrating, irritability, muscle tension, and sleep disturbance.

### Phobias

A phobia is an intense, persistent, and disproportionate fear of a specific object or situation. Common phobias include specific phobias (e.g., arachnophobia, acrophobia), social anxiety disorder (fear of social situations in which the individual may be scrutinised by others), and agoraphobia (fear of open spaces or situations from which escape may be difficult).

### Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD)

OCD is characterised by the presence of obsessions (recurrent, intrusive, and distressing thoughts, images, or impulses) and compulsions (repetitive behaviours or mental acts performed in response to obsessions, typically according to rigid rules or to reduce distress). Common obsessions include fears of contamination, symmetry concerns, and aggressive or sexual thoughts. Common compulsions include hand-washing, checking, ordering, and counting.

Note: In the DSM-5, OCD was removed from the anxiety disorders category and placed in a separate category ("Obsessive-Compulsive and Related Disorders") alongside body dysmorphic disorder, hoarding disorder, and trichotillomania.

## Depressive Disorders

### Major Depressive Disorder (MDD)

MDD is characterised by a persistently depressed mood and/or loss of interest or pleasure in activities (anhedonia), present for at least two weeks. Additional symptoms include significant weight loss or gain, insomnia or hypersomnia, psychomotor agitation or retardation, fatigue, feelings of worthlessness or excessive guilt, diminished concentration, and recurrent thoughts of death or suicidal ideation.

### Persistent Depressive Disorder (Dysthymia)

Persistent depressive disorder involves a chronic depressed mood that lasts for at least two years. The symptoms are similar to but less severe than those of major depressive disorder. Individuals with persistent depressive disorder may experience brief periods of normal mood, but these periods last no more than two months.

## Trauma and Stress-Related Disorders

### Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)

PTSD develops following exposure to a traumatic event involving actual or threatened death, serious injury, or sexual violence. Symptoms fall into four clusters:

1. **Intrusion symptoms:** Recurrent, involuntary, and distressing memories, nightmares, or flashbacks of the traumatic event.
2. **Avoidance:** Persistent avoidance of stimuli associated with the traumatic event (e.g., avoiding conversations about the event, avoiding places that remind the individual of the event).
3. **Negative alterations in cognition and mood:** Persistent negative emotional states, diminished interest in activities, feelings of detachment from others, and persistent inability to experience positive emotions.
4. **Alterations in arousal and reactivity:** Hypervigilance, exaggerated startle response, irritability, reckless behaviour, and difficulty concentrating or sleeping.

Symptoms must persist for more than one month after the traumatic event and cause significant functional impairment.

## Explanations of Abnormality

### Biological Explanations

Biological explanations of psychological disorders focus on genetic, neurochemical, and neuroanatomical factors.

**Genetics:** Family, twin, and adoption studies have demonstrated that psychological disorders have a heritable component. Heritability estimates vary by disorder: approximately 40% for MDD, 60--80% for schizophrenia, and 30--50% for anxiety disorders. The serotonin transporter gene (5-HTTLPR) has been implicated in vulnerability to depression following stressful life events, as demonstrated by Caspi et al. (2003) in the [Biological Level of Analysis](./biological-level-of-analysis).

**Neurochemistry:** The monoamine hypothesis of depression proposes that depression results from a deficit in monoamine neurotransmitters (serotonin, noradrenaline, and dopamine). The GABA hypothesis of anxiety proposes that anxiety disorders involve underactivity of the GABA system. The serotonin hypothesis of OCD proposes that OCD involves dysfunction of the serotonin system, particularly in the cortico-striato-thalamo-cortical circuit.

**Brain structure:** Neuroimaging studies have identified structural and functional abnormalities associated with psychological disorders. For example, individuals with depression often show reduced volume in the hippocampus and prefrontal cortex, and increased activity in the amygdala. Individuals with PTSD show reduced volume in the hippocampus and hyperactivity of the amygdala.

### Cognitive Explanations

Cognitive explanations focus on maladaptive patterns of thinking that contribute to the development and maintenance of psychological disorders.

**Beck's cognitive model of depression (1976):** Beck proposed that depression is maintained by the cognitive triad of negative beliefs about the self, the world, and the future. These negative beliefs are maintained by cognitive distortions (systematic errors in thinking such as all-or-nothing thinking, overgeneralisation, and catastrophising). Depressed individuals also develop negative schemas (stable, enduring beliefs about the self and the world) that bias the processing of information toward the negative.

**Ellis's ABC model (1962):** Ellis proposed that psychological distress is caused not by events themselves but by the individual's beliefs about those events. In the ABC model:

- A (Activating event): An external event or situation
- B (Beliefs): The individual's irrational beliefs about the event
- C (Consequences): The emotional and behavioural consequences

Ellis argued that irrational beliefs (e.g., "I must be perfect," "Everyone must like me," "The world should be fair") are the primary cause of emotional distress. Rational emotive behaviour therapy (REBT) aims to identify and challenge these irrational beliefs and replace them with more rational alternatives.

**Seligman's learned helplessness model (1974):** Seligman proposed that depression results from the perception that one has no control over aversive outcomes. He demonstrated this using dogs that were subjected to inescapable electric shocks in a conditioning chamber. When later placed in a chamber where they could escape the shock by jumping over a barrier, the dogs did not attempt to escape and instead passively endured the shock. Seligman argued that humans who experience repeated uncontrollable negative events develop a similar sense of helplessness, leading to passivity, low motivation, and depressive symptoms.

The reformulated learned helplessness model (Abramson, Seligman, and Teasdale, 1978) introduced the concept of **attributional style**: individuals who attribute negative events to internal, stable, and global causes ("It is my fault, it will never change, and it affects everything") are more vulnerable to depression than those who attribute negative events to external, unstable, and specific causes.

### Sociocultural Explanations

Sociocultural explanations focus on the role of social, environmental, and cultural factors in the aetiology of psychological disorders.

**Stressful life events:** Major life events (bereavement, divorce, job loss, financial difficulties) are significant risk factors for depression and anxiety. The diathesis-stress model proposes that individuals with a biological or psychological vulnerability (diathesis) are more likely to develop a disorder when exposed to stress.

**Poverty and socioeconomic status:** Individuals in lower socioeconomic groups have higher rates of psychological disorders. This relationship is mediated by factors including chronic stress, limited access to resources, exposure to violence, and reduced social support.

**Social support:** Low social support is a significant risk factor for depression, PTSD, and anxiety disorders. Social support provides emotional, informational, and instrumental resources that buffer the effects of stress (the stress-buffering hypothesis; Cohen and Wills, 1985).

**Cultural factors:** Cultural norms and values influence the expression, interpretation, and treatment of psychological disorders. In some cultures, psychological distress is expressed through somatic symptoms (physical complaints) rather than emotional symptoms. Cultural factors also affect help-seeking behaviour: in cultures where mental illness is highly stigmatised, individuals may delay seeking treatment or may present to medical rather than mental health services.

## Treatment Approaches

### Biological Treatments

| Treatment | Mechanism | Disorders Treated | Evaluation |
|---|---|---|---|
| SSRIs (e.g., fluoxetine) | Block reuptake of serotonin, increasing synaptic serotonin | Depression, OCD, anxiety disorders | Effective for moderate to severe depression; side effects include nausea, insomnia, sexual dysfunction; delayed onset (2--4 weeks) |
| Benzodiazepines (e.g., diazepam) | Enhance GABA activity, increasing inhibitory neurotransmission | Short-term relief of anxiety | Rapid onset; risk of dependence and tolerance; not recommended for long-term use |
| ECT (electroconvulsive therapy) | Induces seizure under general anaesthesia; mechanism not fully understood | Severe, treatment-resistant depression | Highly effective for severe depression; side effects include memory impairment; ethical concerns about consent |
| Antipsychotics (e.g., chlorpromazine) | Block dopamine D2 receptors | Schizophrenia | Effective for positive symptoms; less effective for negative symptoms; side effects include tardive dyskinesia, weight gain |

**SSRIs:** Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors are the most commonly prescribed class of antidepressants. They increase synaptic serotonin levels by blocking the serotonin transporter (SERT), preventing the reuptake of serotonin from the synaptic cleft. While effective, SSRIs have a delayed onset of action (therapeutic effects typically begin after 2--4 weeks), which suggests that their antidepressant effect is not simply due to the immediate increase in synaptic serotonin but may involve downstream changes in receptor sensitivity and gene expression.

**Evaluation of biological treatments:**

- Biological treatments are generally effective, particularly for severe disorders, and are relatively easy to administer.
- They treat symptoms rather than underlying causes; relapse is common after discontinuation.
- Side effects can be significant, and long-term medication use carries risks (e.g., tardive dyskinesia from antipsychotics, dependence on benzodiazepines).
- The placebo effect accounts for a substantial proportion of the therapeutic response to antidepressants, particularly in mild to moderate depression (Kirsch et al., 2008).

### Cognitive Treatments

**Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT):** CBT is a structured, time-limited form of psychotherapy that aims to identify and modify maladaptive cognitions (thoughts, beliefs, attitudes) and behaviours that contribute to psychological distress. It is based on the premise that it is not events themselves that cause distress but the individual's interpretation of those events.

CBT for depression typically includes:

- **Cognitive restructuring:** Identifying automatic negative thoughts and cognitive distortions, and challenging them with evidence.
- **Behavioural activation:** Encouraging the individual to engage in activities that they previously enjoyed, to break the cycle of inactivity and low mood.
- **Goal setting:** Setting specific, achievable goals to restore a sense of competence and self-efficacy.

**Evaluation of CBT:**

- CBT is at least as effective as antidepressant medication for mild to moderate depression and is superior to medication in preventing relapse (Hollon et al., 2005).
- CBT requires motivated clients and skilled therapists, and may not be suitable for individuals with severe cognitive impairment.
- CBT has been criticised for focusing excessively on conscious thought processes and underestimating the role of unconscious processes, emotions, and interpersonal relationships.

### Sociocultural Treatments

**Group therapy:** Group therapy involves one or more therapists working with a small group of clients simultaneously. It provides opportunities for social support, normalisation of experiences, interpersonal learning, and modelling of adaptive behaviours. Group therapy is particularly effective for individuals who feel isolated or stigmatised.

**Community mental health:** Community-based approaches aim to provide accessible mental health services within the community, including crisis intervention, case management, supported employment, and housing assistance. The recovery model emphasises the individual's strengths and resilience rather than deficits and pathology.

**Family therapy:** Family therapy involves treating the family as a system, recognising that individual psychopathology both influences and is influenced by family dynamics. It is particularly effective for disorders where family factors play a significant role, such as eating disorders and substance use disorders.

## Key Studies

### Rosenhan (1973): On Being Sane in Insane Places

Rosenhan conducted a landmark study investigating the reliability and validity of psychiatric diagnosis. Eight pseudopatients (including Rosenhan himself) presented to psychiatric hospitals in five US states, complaining of hearing voices that said "empty," "hollow," and "thud." Apart from this single complaint, the pseudopatients gave entirely truthful accounts of their personal histories and behaved normally during their stay.

Key findings:

- All eight pseudopatients were admitted to psychiatric hospitals, seven with a diagnosis of schizophrenia and one with a diagnosis of manic-depressive psychosis.
- Despite behaving normally after admission, the pseudopatients were kept in hospital for an average of 19 days (range 7--52 days).
- The pseudopatients' normal behaviours were interpreted through the lens of their diagnosis: note-taking was recorded as "writing behaviour," pacing the corridor out of boredom was interpreted as "nervousness," and arriving early for meals was labelled "oral-acquisitive syndrome."
- None of the staff (including psychiatrists) identified any of the pseudopatients as impostors. However, approximately one-third of the genuine patients voiced suspicions.

In a follow-up study, Rosenhan informed a psychiatric hospital that one or more pseudopatients would attempt to gain admission over the next three months. Of the 193 patients admitted during this period, staff identified 41 as impostors; a further 42 were suspected by at least one staff member. In fact, no pseudopatients were sent.

**Evaluation:**

- The study demonstrated that psychiatric labels are powerful and can distort the perception of behaviour (labelling theory).
- The study raises serious questions about the reliability and validity of psychiatric diagnosis.
- The study used deception and raised ethical concerns about the disruption of clinical services and the welfare of genuine patients.
- The study has been criticised for using a highly atypical sample of pseudopatients (well-educated professionals) and for simulating only one type of symptom (auditory hallucinations). The generalisability to the full range of psychiatric diagnoses is uncertain.

### Beck (1976): Cognitive Theory and Therapy of Depression

Beck developed his cognitive theory of depression through systematic clinical observation of depressed patients. He identified the cognitive triad, cognitive distortions, and negative schemas as the core cognitive features of depression. He then developed cognitive therapy (now known as CBT) as a structured treatment aimed at identifying and modifying these maladaptive cognitions.

Beck et al. (1979) demonstrated the efficacy of cognitive therapy in a controlled trial comparing CBT with antidepressant medication and a placebo in the treatment of depression. CBT was found to be as effective as medication in reducing depressive symptoms and more effective in preventing relapse at follow-up.

### Seligman (1974): Learned Helplessness

As described above, Seligman's experiments with dogs demonstrated that exposure to uncontrollable aversive events produces a passive, helpless response that persists even when the individual subsequently has the opportunity to escape. The learned helplessness model has been highly influential in understanding the relationship between perceived control and depression.

### Freud (1909): Little Hans

Freud's case study of Little Hans, a five-year-old boy with a phobia of horses, is a foundational study in psychoanalytic theory. Freud interpreted Hans's phobia as a manifestation of the Oedipus complex: Hans was said to have repressed sexual desires for his mother and fear of castration by his father, which were displaced onto horses. The phobia was interpreted as a compromise formation that simultaneously expressed and defended against the unconscious conflict.

**Evaluation:**

- The case study illustrates psychoanalytic concepts and the method of psychoanalytic interpretation.
- The interpretation is highly speculative and cannot be empirically tested. Alternative explanations (e.g., classical conditioning: Hans may have been frightened by a real horse) are equally plausible.
- The study raises ethical concerns about the reliability of data obtained from a parent (Hans's father, who was a follower of Freud) and about the potential for suggestive questioning.

### Bandura (1969): Social Learning Theory and Aggression

Bandura's Bobo doll experiments demonstrated that children who observed an adult model behaving aggressively toward a Bobo doll were more likely to imitate the aggressive behaviour than children who did not observe an aggressive model. The study supported the social learning theory of aggression, which proposes that aggression is learned through observation, imitation, and reinforcement. This study is relevant to abnormal psychology because social learning theory provides an explanation for the acquisition of maladaptive behaviours and has informed the development of behavioural and cognitive-behavioural treatments.

- **Do not present biological, cognitive, and sociocultural explanations as competing alternatives.** Most contemporary psychologists adopt a biopsychosocial approach, recognising that psychological disorders arise from the interaction of multiple factors. IB examiners expect students to discuss multiple levels of explanation.
- **Do not confuse correlation with causation in genetic research.** Family and twin studies demonstrate that disorders are heritable, but they do not identify the specific genes involved or the mechanisms by which genetic vulnerability is expressed.
- **Do not overstate the effectiveness of any single treatment.** All treatments have limitations, and treatment effectiveness varies across disorders, severity levels, and individual characteristics.
- **Do not forget to discuss ethical considerations.** Ethical issues are particularly salient in abnormal psychology research, where participants may be vulnerable, and in clinical practice, where treatment decisions have significant consequences for patients' well-being.

## Diagnosis and Classification Issues

### Reliability

Reliability refers to the consistency of a diagnostic measure. In the context of classification, it encompasses:

- **Test-retest reliability:** Whether the same clinician arrives at the same diagnosis when assessing the same patient at different points in time.
- **Inter-rater reliability:** Whether different clinicians arrive at the same diagnosis when assessing the same patient.

Studies of diagnostic reliability have produced mixed results. Rosenhan's (1973) study demonstrated that psychiatric diagnosis could be unreliable: different hospitals gave different diagnoses, and once a label was applied, it persisted despite contradictory evidence. However, DSM-5 field trials reported generally good inter-rater reliability for most diagnostic categories.

### Validity

Validity refers to whether a diagnostic category measures what it is intended to measure. Key types of validity in the context of classification include:

- **Construct validity:** Whether the diagnostic category corresponds to a genuine underlying construct with a distinct aetiology and course.
- **Predictive validity:** Whether the diagnosis predicts the individual's response to treatment and clinical outcome.
- **Concurrent validity:** Whether the diagnosis is consistent with other measures of the same construct.

The high rates of comorbidity in the DSM system (e.g., the frequent co-occurrence of depression and anxiety) suggest that the current categorical system may not reflect the true structure of psychopathology. The Hierarchical Taxonomy of Psychopathology (HiTOP) proposes an alternative dimensional framework that organises psychopathology along a series of continuous spectra.

## Ethical Considerations in Abnormal Psychology Research

- **Informed consent:** Individuals with psychological disorders may have impaired decision-making capacity, raising questions about their ability to provide valid informed consent. Researchers must ensure that participants understand the nature, purpose, and potential risks of the research.
- **Protection from harm:** Research involving individuals with psychological disorders carries the risk of exacerbating symptoms, triggering distress, or exploiting vulnerable participants. The potential benefits of the research must be carefully weighed against these risks.
- **Confidentiality:** Psychological research often involves the collection of sensitive personal information. Strict confidentiality protocols must be in place, and data must be anonymised or pseudonymised.
- **Deception:** Deception is sometimes used in abnormal psychology research (e.g., Rosenhan's pseudopatient study), but it must be justified by the scientific value of the research and followed by thorough debriefing.
- **Use of diagnostic labels:** Diagnostic labels can be stigmatising and may affect the way individuals are treated by others (the self-fulfilling prophecy). Researchers and clinicians must be aware of the potential consequences of labelling.
