# Sociocultural Level of Analysis

This section covers the sociocultural level of analysis, investigating how the social environment and
cultural context influence behaviour.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Evaluating Social Identity Theory
**Approach:** Outline SIT (Tajfel and Turner, 1979). Describe how social categorisation, social identity, and social comparison produce in-group favouritism and out-group derogation. Support with Tajfel's minimal group experiments. Evaluate with limitations: laboratory studies lack ecological validity; SIT does not explain why some groups are targets of more extreme prejudice; real-world prejudice is influenced by historical, economic, and political factors beyond mere categorisation.

### Example 2: Strategies for Reducing Prejudice
**Approach:** Describe the contact hypothesis (Allport, 1954): equal status, common goals, intergroup cooperation, and institutional support as conditions for successful contact. Support with Sherif's Robers Cave study (superordinate goals reduced conflict). Evaluate with limitations: contact alone is insufficient without institutional change; self-categorisation theory suggests that recategorisation into a common in-group may be more effective.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Describing stereotypes without explaining their cognitive function.** Stereotypes are cognitive shortcuts that simplify social information processing. Their explanatory power (why they form) must be addressed alongside their consequences.
- **Confusing prejudice (attitude) with discrimination (behaviour).** These are distinct constructs that require separate explanation and evidence.
- **Stating that culture determines behaviour without qualification.** Culture influences but does not determine behaviour; individual differences, subcultures, and acculturation processes complicate simple cultural explanations.

## Summary

The sociocultural level of analysis investigates how social environments and cultural contexts shape behaviour. Social identity theory explains intergroup processes through categorisation, identification, and comparison. Cultural norms and dimensions (individualism-collectivism) influence attributional styles and self-concept. Prejudice originates from stereotyping, social categorisation, and scapegoat theory. Reduction strategies include the contact hypothesis, superordinate goals, and recategorisation.
