## Brain Development

Brain development is a protracted process that begins in the prenatal period and continues through adolescence and into early adulthood. Understanding the trajectory of brain development is essential for understanding cognitive, social, and emotional development across the lifespan.

### Prenatal Development

Neural development begins approximately three weeks after conception, when the neural plate forms and folds into the neural tube. The neural tube gives rise to the brain and spinal cord. By the end of the embryonic period (8 weeks), the basic brain structures are in place, and neurogenesis (the birth of new neurons) is occurring at a rate of approximately 250,000 neurons per minute.

**Teratogens:** Environmental agents that can disrupt prenatal brain development include alcohol (causing foetal alcohol spectrum disorders), nicotine, illicit drugs, and maternal infections. The timing of exposure is critical: different brain structures are most vulnerable at different stages of development.

### Childhood

During early childhood, the brain undergoes rapid growth. Synaptogenesis (the formation of new synaptic connections) proceeds at an extraordinary rate, with the density of synaptic connections in the cortex peaking at approximately age 3--4, far exceeding adult levels. This is followed by synaptic pruning, the process by which unused connections are eliminated, refining neural circuits and improving processing efficiency. The principle of "use it or lose it" applies: connections that are frequently activated are strengthened, while those that are rarely used are pruned away.

**Critical periods:** There are time windows during which the brain is particularly sensitive to specific types of environmental input. For example, the critical period for language acquisition extends from approximately 6 months to puberty; children who are not exposed to language during this period have permanent deficits in language ability (Lenneberg, 1967).

### Adolescence

Adolescence is characterised by significant structural and functional changes in the brain. The prefrontal cortex, which is responsible for executive functions (planning, decision making, impulse control, and social cognition), undergoes extensive remodelling during adolescence and does not fully mature until approximately age 25.

**The dual-systems model:** Steinberg (2008) proposed that adolescent risk-taking behaviour results from the asynchrony between two brain systems: the socioemotional system (including the limbic system and the striatum), which matures early and is highly responsive to rewards and social stimuli, and the cognitive control system (including the prefrontal cortex), which matures more slowly and is responsible for impulse control and long-term planning. This temporal gap explains why adolescents are disproportionately prone to risk-taking, particularly in the presence of peers.

## Cognitive Development

### Piaget's Theory of Cognitive Development

Jean Piaget proposed that cognitive development proceeds through four invariant stages, each characterised by qualitatively different ways of thinking. Development is driven by the interaction between biological maturation and environmental experience, through the processes of assimilation and accommodation (discussed in the [Cognitive Level of Analysis](./cognitive-level-of-analysis)).

| Stage | Age Range | Key Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| Sensorimotor | Birth to approximately 2 years | Knowledge develops through sensory experience and motor action. Object permanence develops (understanding that objects continue to exist even when not perceived). |
| Preoperational | Approximately 2 to 7 years | Development of symbolic thought and language. Children are egocentric (unable to take the perspective of others). Thinking is characterised by animism, centration, and irreversibility. |
| Concrete operational | Approximately 7 to 11 years | Children can perform logical operations on concrete objects. They develop conservation (understanding that quantity remains the same despite changes in appearance), seriation (ordering objects by size), and reversibility. Egocentrism declines. |
| Formal operational | Approximately 11 years and older | Individuals can think abstractly, reason hypothetically, and engage in systematic problem solving. Metacognition (thinking about thinking) develops. |

**Conservation experiments:** Piaget demonstrated that preoperational children fail conservation tasks because they focus on one salient dimension (usually height) and fail to compensate by considering the other dimension (width). For example, if liquid is poured from a short, wide glass into a tall, narrow glass, a preoperational child will say that the tall glass contains more liquid, even though the quantity has not changed.

**Evaluation of Piaget's theory:**

- Piaget's stage theory has been enormously influential and is supported by evidence that children's reasoning becomes more sophisticated with age.
- Subsequent research has challenged several aspects of Piaget's theory. Baillargeon (1987) used the violation-of-expectation method to demonstrate that infants as young as 3--4 months have some understanding of object permanence, far earlier than Piaget proposed. This suggests that Piaget may have underestimated infants' cognitive abilities because he relied on behavioural measures (reaching, searching) that require motor skills that young infants have not yet developed.
- The stage model may be too rigid. Research has shown that children can exhibit features of more advanced stages in some domains while remaining at an earlier stage in others (horizontal decalage).
- Piaget's theory has been criticised for being culturally biased. Dasen (1972) found that conservation develops earlier in cultures where children are exposed to activities that require measurement and quantification (e.g., pottery-making), suggesting that cultural experience influences the pace of cognitive development.

### Vygotsky's Sociocultural Theory

Lev Vygotsky proposed that cognitive development is fundamentally a social process. Unlike Piaget, who emphasised the child's independent exploration of the environment, Vygotsky argued that cognitive development occurs through social interaction with more knowledgeable others.

**Zone of proximal development (ZPD):** The ZPD is the gap between what a child can do independently and what the child can do with guidance and support from a more knowledgeable other (a teacher, parent, or peer). Learning occurs within the ZPD, and what a child can do with support today, the child can do independently tomorrow.

**Scaffolding:** Wood, Bruner, and Ross (1976) introduced the concept of scaffolding to describe the temporary support provided by a more knowledgeable other to help a child complete a task within the ZPD. Effective scaffolding involves adjusting the level of support to match the child's current competence, gradually withdrawing support as the child becomes more proficient.

**Private speech:** Vygotsky observed that young children talk to themselves while performing tasks (private speech), and argued that this self-directed speech serves a regulatory function, guiding the child's thinking and behaviour. As children develop, private speech becomes internalised as inner speech (silent verbal thought).

**Evaluation of Vygotsky's theory:**

- Vygotsky's emphasis on the social nature of cognitive development has been highly influential in educational psychology, informing approaches such as collaborative learning and peer tutoring.
- The theory is less precise than Piaget's in specifying the stages and mechanisms of development. The ZPD is a useful metaphor but is difficult to operationalise and measure empirically.
- Vygotsky's theory may underestimate the role of individual exploration and biological maturation in cognitive development.

### Bruner's Scaffolding

Jerome Bruner extended Vygotsky's ideas by emphasising the role of language and culture in cognitive development. Bruner proposed three modes of representation through which knowledge is encoded:

1. **Enactive representation:** Knowledge is stored in the form of motor responses (actions).
2. **Iconic representation:** Knowledge is stored in the form of visual images.
3. **Symbolic representation:** Knowledge is stored in the form of language and other symbolic systems.

Bruner's concept of scaffolding (developed with Wood and Ross, 1976) has been widely applied in education. Effective scaffolding involves:

- Recruiting the child's interest in the task
- Reducing the number of steps required to complete the task
- Maintaining the child's pursuit of the goal
- Highlighting critical features of the task and discrepancies between the child's current performance and the desired outcome
- Controlling frustration during problem solving

## Social Development

### Attachment Theory

Attachment is a deep and enduring emotional bond that connects one person to another across time and space. Attachment theory, developed primarily by John Bowlby, proposes that infants are biologically predisposed to form attachments to their primary caregivers, and that the quality of these early attachments has profound consequences for later social and emotional development.

#### Bowlby (1944): Forty-Four Juvenile Thieves

Bowlby studied 44 adolescents who had been referred to a child guidance clinic for stealing. He compared them with a control group of 44 adolescents who had emotional problems but had not been referred for stealing. Key findings:

- 14 of the 44 thieves (32%) were diagnosed as "affectionless characters" (individuals who showed no guilt or affection for others).
- Of these 14, 12 had experienced prolonged early separation from their mothers (before age 5).
- In the control group, only 2 of the 44 adolescents had experienced prolonged early separation.

Bowlby concluded that prolonged early separation from the primary caregiver caused affectionless psychopathy, and that maternal deprivation in early childhood was a major risk factor for delinquency and emotional disturbance.

**Evaluation:**

- The study relied on retrospective data (mothers' accounts of early separation), which may be unreliable.
- The direction of causality is unclear: did separation cause the emotional problems, or were mothers of emotionally disturbed children more likely to separate from them?
- Bowlby's conclusion was based on a small, unrepresentative sample (all participants were from lower socioeconomic backgrounds and had been referred to a clinic), limiting generalisability.
- Rutter (1972) distinguished between deprivation (the loss of an attachment figure) and privation (the failure to form an attachment in the first place), arguing that Bowlby had conflated these two distinct phenomena.

#### Harlow (1958): Attachment in Rhesus Monkeys

Harlow conducted a series of experiments with rhesus monkeys to investigate the nature of attachment. Infant monkeys were separated from their mothers shortly after birth and placed in cages with two surrogate "mothers": one made of wire mesh equipped with a feeding bottle, and one made of soft terrycloth without a feeding bottle.

Key findings:

- The infant monkeys spent significantly more time clinging to the cloth mother than the wire mother, even when the wire mother was the source of food.
- When a frightening stimulus (a mechanical toy) was introduced, the monkeys ran to the cloth mother for comfort, not to the wire mother.
- Monkeys raised with only wire mothers showed severe social and emotional deficits in later life: they were unable to form normal social relationships, engaged in self-directed aggression, and showed abnormal sexual behaviour.

These findings challenged the dominant behaviourist view (represented by the cupboard love theory) that attachment was based on the association between the caregiver and the provision of food. Harlow demonstrated that contact comfort, not food, was the primary basis for attachment formation.

**Evaluation:**

- The study had high internal validity due to controlled experimental conditions.
- The use of monkeys raises ethical concerns about the welfare of the animals and the applicability of findings to humans.
- The study has been criticised for overgeneralising: human attachment is more complex than monkey attachment, involving cognitive, linguistic, and cultural factors that Harlow's paradigm could not capture.

### Attachment Types

Mary Ainsworth developed the Strange Situation Procedure (SSP) to assess the quality of infant-caregiver attachment. The SSP involves a series of separations and reunions between the infant and the caregiver in an unfamiliar laboratory room, with the presence of a stranger.

#### Ainsworth et al. (1978): The Strange Situation

Based on infants' behaviour during the SSP, Ainsworth identified three attachment patterns:

| Attachment Type | Behaviour in Strange Situation | Prevalence | Caregiver Behaviour |
|---|---|---|---|
| Secure (Type B) | Uses caregiver as a secure base for exploration; distressed by separation; seeks contact upon reunion; easily comforted | Approximately 65% | Sensitive, responsive, consistent |
| Insecure-avoidant (Type A) | Does not seek proximity to caregiver; shows little distress during separation; avoids contact upon reunion | Approximately 15--20% | Rejecting, emotionally distant |
| Insecure-resistant/ambivalent (Type C) | Shows intense distress during separation; seeks contact upon reunion but simultaneously resists it; difficult to comfort | Approximately 10--15% | Inconsistent, unpredictable |

Main and Solomon (1986) later identified a fourth category:

| Disorganised (Type D) | Contradictory behaviours (e.g., approaching the caregiver while looking away); freezing, rocking, or other signs of fear | Approximately 5--10% | Caregiver is a source of fear (e.g., abuse, unresolved trauma) |

**Evaluation:**

- The SSP has been widely used and has generated a large body of research on attachment.
- The procedure is a brief, artificial laboratory observation that may not reflect the quality of attachment in natural settings (low ecological validity).
- The SSP has been criticised for cultural bias: the categories were developed based on North American samples, and the distribution of attachment types varies across cultures (see below).

### Cultural Variations in Attachment

Van IJzendoorn and Kroonenberg (1988) conducted a meta-analysis of 32 Strange Situation studies across 8 countries. Key findings:

- Secure attachment was the most common type in all countries studied, supporting the universality of secure attachment as the norm.
- There was more variation in attachment types within cultures than between cultures.
- However, there were some cross-cultural differences: insecure-avoidant attachment was more common in Western, individualist cultures (e.g., Germany, where independence is valued), while insecure-resistant attachment was more common in non-Western, collectivist cultures (e.g., Japan, where close maternal proximity is the norm).

These findings support Bowlby's claim that attachment is a universal biological process, while also demonstrating that cultural practices influence the distribution of attachment types.

### Effects of Deprivation and Privation

**Deprivation** refers to the loss of an attachment figure after a secure attachment has been formed. Bowlby argued that prolonged deprivation leads to negative outcomes, but Rutter (1972) demonstrated that the effects of deprivation depend on the age of the child, the quality of the alternative care provided, and the child's individual temperament.

**Privation** refers to the failure to form any attachment during the critical period of attachment formation. The most compelling evidence on the effects of privation comes from studies of children raised in institutional care.

#### Rutter et al. (1998): The English and Romanian Adoptees Study

Rutter and colleagues studied 165 Romanian children who were adopted into British families after spending their early years in extremely deprived orphanages in Romania. The children were assessed at ages 4, 6, 11, and 15, and were compared with a group of British children adopted within the UK.

Conditions in the Romanian orphanages were stark: children had minimal contact with caregivers, little stimulation, and inadequate nutrition. Many children showed severe developmental delays upon arrival in the UK.

Key findings:

- Children adopted before age 6 months showed near-normal development by age 4, demonstrating substantial recovery.
- Children adopted between 6 months and 2 years showed significant improvement but had higher rates of attachment difficulties and cognitive deficits than the UK adoptee group.
- Children adopted after age 2 showed the most persistent difficulties, including disinhibited attachment (excessive friendliness with strangers), cognitive deficits, and quasi-autistic features.

These findings demonstrate that the effects of privation are not inevitable but are strongly moderated by the timing and quality of the intervention. Early adoption into a supportive family environment can substantially mitigate the effects of early privation, but recovery is less complete when privation extends beyond the first two years of life.

### Internal Working Models

Bowlby proposed that early attachment experiences give rise to **internal working models** (IWMs) -- mental representations of the self and others that guide expectations about relationships throughout the lifespan. A child who experiences sensitive, responsive caregiving develops a positive IWM ("I am worthy of love; others are reliable and supportive"). A child who experiences rejection or inconsistency develops a negative IWM ("I am unworthy of love; others are unreliable or unresponsive").

Internal working models influence later relationships by shaping:

- **Expectations:** How individuals expect others to behave in close relationships.
- **Interpretation:** How individuals interpret others' behaviour (e.g., interpreting a partner's lateness as a sign of rejection vs. an unavoidable delay).
- **Behaviour:** How individuals behave in close relationships (e.g., seeking closeness vs. maintaining distance).

Hazan and Shaver (1987) demonstrated that adults' romantic attachment styles correspond closely to Ainsworth's infant attachment categories, supporting the continuity of internal working models from infancy to adulthood.

## Moral Development

### Kohlberg's Theory of Moral Development

Lawrence Kohlberg proposed that moral reasoning develops through a series of six stages organised into three levels. Each stage represents a qualitatively different way of thinking about moral dilemmas.

| Level | Stage | Moral Reasoning |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-conventional | 1. Obedience and punishment orientation | Moral behaviour is determined by the threat of punishment. |
| | 2. Instrumental relativism | Moral behaviour is determined by self-interest and exchange ("You scratch my back, I'll scratch yours"). |
| Conventional | 3. Good interpersonal relationships | Moral behaviour is determined by social approval and conformity to expectations. |
| | 4. Maintaining social order | Moral behaviour is determined by duty, respect for authority, and the maintenance of social order. |
| Post-conventional | 5. Social contract orientation | Moral behaviour is determined by a social contract that balances individual rights with the common good. |
| | 6. Universal ethical principles | Moral behaviour is determined by self-chosen ethical principles (justice, equality, human dignity) that are universal and in some cases may supersede law. |

Kohlberg assessed moral reasoning using the Heinz dilemma: a woman is dying of a rare disease, and the only drug that can save her is too expensive for her husband to afford. The husband breaks into the pharmacist's shop to steal the drug. Should he have done so, and why?

The stage of moral reasoning is determined not by whether the individual says yes or no, but by the reasoning behind their response. A stage 1 response ("He should not steal because he will go to jail") and a stage 6 response ("He should steal because the right to life is a universal ethical principle that supersedes property rights") may lead to the same behavioural conclusion but reflect fundamentally different levels of moral reasoning.

#### Kohlberg (1963): Moral Development

Kohlberg conducted a longitudinal study in which he presented moral dilemmas to boys aged 10--16 and followed them for several years. He found that moral reasoning developed through the stages in the fixed sequence described above, and that no individual skipped stages or regressed to earlier stages.

**Evaluation:**

- Kohlberg's theory is well-supported by longitudinal research showing that moral reasoning develops in the predicted sequence.
- The theory has been criticised for focusing exclusively on justice as the basis of moral reasoning, neglecting other moral foundations such as care, loyalty, and authority (Haidt, 2001).
- The theory may be biased toward Western, educated, male populations. Kohlberg found that men tended to reach higher stages than women, but this may reflect a gender bias in the measurement of moral reasoning rather than a genuine difference in moral competence.

### Gilligan's Critique

Carol Gilligan (1982) argued that Kohlberg's theory reflects a male perspective on morality that privileges justice and individual rights over care and interpersonal responsibility. She proposed an alternative ethic of care in which moral reasoning is centred on the preservation of relationships and the responsiveness to the needs of others.

Gilligan's research suggested that women tend to approach moral dilemmas with a focus on care and responsibility ("If Heinz does not steal the drug, he is failing in his responsibility to care for his wife"), whereas men tend to approach the same dilemmas with a focus on justice and rights ("Heinz has a right to steal the drug because the pharmacist is being unjust").

**Evaluation:**

- Gilligan's critique has been influential in broadening the scope of moral psychology beyond the justice orientation.
- Subsequent research has found that both men and women use justice and care reasoning, and that gender differences in moral reasoning are small and context-dependent.
- Walker (1984) found that when both justice and care reasoning are assessed, no significant gender differences emerge in overall moral maturity.

## Gender Development

### Biological Explanations

Biological explanations of gender development focus on the role of chromosomes, hormones, and brain structure.

- **Chromosomes:** Individuals with XX chromosomes typically develop as females, and individuals with XY chromosomes typically develop as males. The SRY gene on the Y chromosome triggers the development of testes, which produce testosterone, which in turn drives the development of male internal and external genitalia.
- **Hormones:** Prenatal exposure to androgens (particularly testosterone) influences brain development and may contribute to gender-differentiated behaviour. Studies of individuals with congenital adrenal hyperplasia (CAH), who are exposed to high levels of androgens prenatally, have found that CAH females show more male-typical play behaviour than unaffected females.
- **Brain structure:** Meta-analyses have identified small but consistent sex differences in brain structure (e.g., males tend to have larger total brain volume, females tend to have greater cortical thickness in certain regions), but the relationship between these differences and gender-differentiated behaviour is not well understood.

### Social Learning Theory

Social learning theory (Bandura, 1977) proposes that gender development occurs through observation, imitation, and reinforcement. Children learn gender-appropriate behaviour by observing same-sex models (parents, peers, media characters) and by being reinforced for gender-typical behaviour and punished for gender-atypical behaviour.

**Evaluation:** Social learning theory accounts for the rapid acquisition of gender roles and the cultural variation in gender norms. However, it does not fully explain why children often imitate same-sex models more than opposite-sex models even when both are equally available (a phenomenon that suggests an active role for the child's own gender identity).

### Gender Schema Theory

Gender schema theory (Martin and Halverson, 1981) proposes that children actively construct gender knowledge through schemas -- cognitive frameworks that organise information about what it means to be male or female. Once a child has developed a basic gender identity (at approximately age 2--3), they begin to seek out information that is consistent with their gender schema and to attend to and remember gender-consistent information more than gender-inconsistent information.

Martin and Halverson (1983) demonstrated that children who were shown pictures of people performing gender-inconsistent activities (e.g., a man putting on lipstick) later recalled the activity in a gender-consistent manner (e.g., recalling that the man was shaving, or that a woman was putting on lipstick). This demonstrates that gender schemas distort memory, consistent with the reconstructive nature of memory discussed in the [Cognitive Level of Analysis](./cognitive-level-of-analysis).

## Adolescent Development

### Identity Formation

Erik Erikson (1968) proposed that the primary developmental task of adolescence is the formation of a coherent sense of identity -- a stable, integrated understanding of who one is, what one values, and what direction one's life will take. Erikson described this as the stage of "identity vs. role confusion."

According to Erikson, successful identity formation requires a period of exploration (the "psychosocial moratorium") during which adolescents experiment with different roles, values, and beliefs before committing to a stable identity. Failure to achieve a coherent identity leads to role confusion, characterised by uncertainty about one's goals, values, and place in society.

Marcia (1966) operationalised Erikson's theory into four identity statuses based on two dimensions: exploration (whether the individual is actively considering different identity options) and commitment (whether the individual has made a firm commitment to an identity):

| Identity Status | Exploration | Commitment | Description |
|---|---|---|---|
| Identity diffusion | Low | Low | No active exploration and no commitment; apathetic and directionless |
| Foreclosure | Low | High | Commitment without exploration; adopts the identity prescribed by parents or authority figures |
| Moratorium | High | Low | Active exploration without commitment; in the process of experimenting with different roles |
| Identity achievement | High | High | Active exploration followed by commitment; has developed a stable, autonomous identity |

### Risk-Taking Behaviour

Adolescents engage in higher rates of risk-taking behaviour (substance use, reckless driving, unsafe sex, criminal behaviour) than either children or adults. As discussed above, Steinberg's dual-systems model attributes this to the asynchrony between the early-maturing socioemotional system and the late-maturing cognitive control system.

Additional factors contributing to adolescent risk-taking include:

- **Peer influence:** Adolescents are more sensitive to peer pressure than either children or adults. Chein et al. (2011) found that the presence of peers increased activity in the reward circuitry of the adolescent brain, leading to riskier decision making.
- **Sensation seeking:** Adolescents have higher levels of sensation seeking than adults, which drives them to seek novel, exciting, and potentially dangerous experiences.
- **Temporal discounting:** Adolescents discount future rewards more steeply than adults, meaning they are more likely to choose immediate gratification over delayed but larger rewards.

<summary>Common Pitfalls: Developmental Psychology</summary>

- **Do not confuse deprivation and privation.** Deprivation is the loss of an attachment that has already been formed; privation is the failure to form any attachment. The effects, explanations, and implications of each are different.
- **Do not present Piaget and Vygotsky as mutually exclusive.** Both theories have empirical support, and contemporary developmental psychologists integrate elements of both. Piaget's stage theory describes the sequence of cognitive development, while Vygotsky's theory describes the social mechanisms that drive development.
- **Do not assume that gender differences are fixed and innate.** Gender differences are influenced by a complex interaction of biological, social, and cognitive factors, and many observed differences are small and context-dependent.
- **Do not confuse Kohlberg's stages with the content of moral reasoning.** The stage is determined by the reasoning process, not by whether the individual arrives at a particular moral conclusion.
