# Biological Level of Analysis

## Overview

The biological level of analysis (BLOA) operates on the premise that behaviour can be explained in
terms of physiological processes: neural activity, neurotransmitter systems, genetic inheritance,
and brain structure. Within the IB Psychology course, BLOA provides the materialist counterpoint to
the cognitive and sociocultural levels, grounding explanations of behaviour in measurable biological
mechanisms rather than inferred mental states or social constructs.

A central debate within BLOA is the nature-nurture question. Modern behaviour genetics has moved
beyond this dichotomy toward gene-environment interaction (GxE) and epigenetics, recognising that
genetic predispositions are expressed only in specific environmental contexts. Caspi et al. (2003)
demonstrated that the short allele of the serotonin transporter gene (5-HTTLPR) increased depression
risk only when combined with stressful life events, illustrating that neither genes nor environment
alone determines outcomes. Epigenetic research (Meaney's rat studies, McGowan et al. 2009) extends
this further by showing that environmental experiences can produce lasting changes in gene
expression without altering the DNA sequence itself.

BLOA connects to the broader course in several ways. Brain imaging techniques (fMRI, PET, EEG, TMS)
provide tools that are used across all levels of analysis -- cognitive neuroscientists use fMRI to
study memory and decision making, and clinical researchers use neuroimaging to investigate abnormal
psychology. Neuroplasticity provides the biological substrate for cognitive development and
learning, while neurotransmitter systems underpin the pharmacological treatments discussed in the
abnormal psychology option. Understanding BLOA is therefore a prerequisite for evaluating biological
explanations of disorders and for critically assessing the methodological strengths and limitations
of neuroscience research.

## Exam Strategy

For Paper 1 SAQs targeting BLOA, examiners consistently ask students to outline or explain one
assumption, one research method, or one study. Prepare two studies per sub-topic with full
methodology, findings, and one evaluation point. The Minnesota Twin Study (Bouchard et al., 1990)
and the Caspi et al. (2003) GxE study are the highest-yield studies for genetics questions. For
neuroplasticity, memorise Rosenzweig and Bennett (1972) and Draganski et al. (2004); for brain
imaging, know Haxby et al. (2001) and the strengths and limitations of at least three techniques.

For Paper 1 ERQs, questions typically require an essay addressing a general principle or research
method. Use three to four studies, ensuring each is described with methodology, findings, and
evaluation. Structure responses with a clear introduction stating the principle, body paragraphs for
each study (description followed by evaluation), and a conclusion that synthesises findings.
Methodology questions are common: be prepared to compare twin studies, adoption studies, and
neuroimaging techniques in terms of their ability to establish causation versus correlation.

For HL Paper 2, BLOA content frequently appears in the abnormal psychology option (biological
explanations of depression and OCD). Be ready to link neurotransmitter theories (serotonin,
dopamine) and neural circuit models (CSTC circuit in OCD) to specific treatment approaches (SSRIs,
antipsychotics), and evaluate these explanations using evidence from brain imaging and genetics
research.

For the Internal Assessment, BLOA offers well-established experimental paradigms with clear
operationalisable variables. Popular designs include the Stroop effect (interference in colour-word
processing), reaction time studies comparing conditions (single vs. dual task), and memory
experiments manipulating levels of processing. Ensure the independent variable is clearly
manipulated and the dependent variable is objectively measurable. Biological topics must be framed
in terms of their relevance to behaviour rather than physiology alone -- the IA assesses psychology,
not neuroscience.

A common error in BLOA extended responses is describing studies without evaluating them. For each
study included, follow the description with at least one evaluation point addressing methodology
(lab vs. field, sample size, operationalisation), generalisability (cross-cultural validity, species
generalisability from animal studies), or ethics (informed consent, right to withdraw, deception). A
response with four studies described but none evaluated will not reach the higher mark bands for
analysis.

When addressing nature-nurture questions, structure the argument around gene-environment interaction
rather than presenting genes and environment as opposing forces. Caspi et al. (2003) is the
canonical study, but supplement it with Meaney's epigenetics research and the Hariri et al. (2002)
neuroimaging study to demonstrate that genetic predispositions are expressed through neurobiological
mechanisms that are themselves shaped by environmental input.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Explaining the Role of Neurotransmitters

**Problem:** Explain how serotonin influences behaviour, using empirical evidence. **Solution:**
Serotonin is a neurotransmitter associated with mood regulation, sleep, and appetite. Low serotonin
levels are linked to depression (supporting the use of SSRIs). Kasamo et al. found that serotonin
transporter density was reduced in patients with major depressive disorder. However, the
relationship is not straightforward: serotonin also interacts with other neurotransmitters
(dopamine, norepinephrine) and varies across brain regions, meaning the simple "serotonin deficiency
= depression" model is an oversimplification.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Confusing fMRI and PET:** fMRI measures blood oxygenation (structure-function mapping); PET uses
  radioactive tracers to track neurotransmitter activity or glucose metabolism. Know the distinction
  for method questions.
- **Equating neuroplasticity with brain damage recovery:** Neuroplasticity includes both functional
  recovery after injury and experience-dependent changes (e.g., Maguire's taxi driver study). Both
  forms should be acknowledged.
