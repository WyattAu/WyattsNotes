## Principles of the Cognitive Level of Analysis

The cognitive level of analysis (CLOA) is guided by three foundational principles:

1. **The mind can be studied scientifically.** Although mental processes are not directly observable, they can be inferred from observable behaviour using controlled experimental methods. Cognitive psychologists develop models and theories that generate testable predictions about how mental processes operate.

2. **Human beings are information processors.** The mind is conceptualised as a computational system that receives input from the environment, processes that information through a series of stages (encoding, storage, retrieval), and produces behavioural output. This information-processing metaphor has been enormously influential in shaping cognitive psychology.

3. **Mental processes guide behaviour.** Internal mental states -- including perceptions, memories, expectations, and beliefs -- mediate between environmental stimuli and behavioural responses. Two people exposed to the same stimulus may behave differently because they process the information differently.

These principles underpin the study of memory, perception, language, thinking, and decision making. They also connect to the biological LOA (mental processes have a neural basis) and the sociocultural LOA (cognitive processes are shaped by cultural and social experience).

## Memory Models

### The Multi-Store Model (Atkinson and Shiffrin, 1968)

The multi-store model (MSM) proposes that memory consists of three structural stores: sensory memory, short-term memory (STM), and long-term memory (LTM). Information flows through these stores in a linear sequence, governed by processes of attention, rehearsal, and retrieval.

**Sensory memory** holds incoming sensory information for a very brief duration (approximately 200--500 milliseconds for visual information; approximately 2--3 seconds for auditory information). It has a large capacity but extremely limited duration. Sperling (1960) demonstrated the existence of iconic memory (visual sensory memory) using a partial-report technique: participants shown a 3-by-4 grid of letters for 50 milliseconds could recall only about 4--5 items in a full-report condition but could recall any row accurately when cued by a tone, indicating that all 12 items were briefly available in sensory memory.

**Short-term memory** has a limited capacity of approximately 7 plus or minus 2 items (Miller, 1956) and a limited duration of approximately 18--30 seconds without rehearsal (Peterson and Peterson, 1959). Information is maintained in STM through maintenance rehearsal (repetition of information without necessarily considering its meaning).

**Long-term memory** has a potentially unlimited capacity and a potentially unlimited duration. Information enters LTM through elaborative rehearsal (active processing of meaning and associations) rather than mere repetition.

**Evaluation of the MSM:**

- The model is supported by evidence from case studies such as Clive Wearing and HM, who had intact STM but severely impaired LTM due to hippocampal damage, suggesting that STM and LTM are distinct stores.
- The model is overly simplistic. It treats STM as a unitary store, whereas the working memory model (see below) demonstrates that STM consists of multiple components. It also treats LTM as a unitary store, whereas subsequent research has identified distinct types of LTM (episodic, semantic, procedural).
- The emphasis on rehearsal as the primary mechanism for transferring information to LTM has been challenged. Craik and Lockhart (1972) proposed the levels of processing framework, arguing that the depth of processing (shallow structural processing versus deep semantic processing) determines whether information is remembered, not the amount of rehearsal.

### The Working Memory Model (Baddeley and Hitch, 1974)

Baddeley and Hitch proposed a more detailed model of STM as a multi-component system consisting of:

- **The central executive:** An attentional control system that allocates processing resources to the other components. It has limited capacity and is responsible for coordinating the slave systems, suppressing irrelevant information, and switching between tasks. It is not a storage system.
- **The phonological loop:** Processes auditory and verbal information. It consists of the phonological store (a temporary store holding auditory information that decays in approximately 2 seconds) and the articulatory rehearsal process (a subvocal rehearsal mechanism that refreshes the contents of the phonological store). Evidence: participants have more difficulty remembering lists of long words than short words (the word length effect), and they have more difficulty remembering phonologically similar words (the phonological similarity effect).
- **The visuospatial sketchpad:** Processes visual and spatial information. It is responsible for manipulating mental images and representing spatial relationships. Evidence: participants perform worse on a visual memory task when simultaneously performing a spatial tracking task, but not when performing a verbal task, indicating that the visuospatial sketchpad is functionally distinct from the phonological loop.
- **The episodic buffer (added later by Baddeley, 2000):** A limited-capacity temporary storage system that integrates information from the phonological loop, visuospatial sketchpad, and LTM into a coherent episodic representation. This component was added to explain how the working memory model accounts for the binding of information from different modalities.

#### Baddeley et al. (1975)

Baddeley, Grant, Wight, and Thomson investigated the dual-task interference predicted by the working memory model. Participants performed a verbal reasoning task (judging whether sentences such as "A follows B" were true or false given the sequence "BA") while simultaneously performing a secondary task. The secondary tasks were either verbal (reciting digits) or visual (tracking a moving light).

Results: Performance on the reasoning task was impaired more by the concurrent verbal task than by the visual task. This supports the existence of separate verbal and visuospatial processing systems, as predicted by the working memory model. If STM were a unitary store (as the MSM implies), any concurrent task should interfere equally with the reasoning task.

**Evaluation of the working memory model:**

- The model is well-supported by experimental evidence from dual-task paradigms, case studies (patients with specific deficits in verbal or visuospatial short-term memory), and neuroimaging studies.
- The central executive is underspecified. Baddeley acknowledged that it is the least well-understood component of the model, and its precise functions and mechanisms remain a topic of ongoing research.
- The model has been highly influential in applied settings, particularly in understanding working memory deficits in developmental disorders such as dyslexia and ADHD.

<details>
<summary>Common Pitfalls: Memory Models</summary>

- **Do not confuse the multi-store model with the working memory model.** The MSM is a structural model of how information flows between sensory memory, STM, and LTM. The working memory model is a functional model of the components of STM.
- **Do not describe the central executive as a "store."** The central executive is an attentional control system, not a storage component. Information is stored in the phonological loop, visuospatial sketchpad, and episodic buffer.
- **Do not equate STM with working memory.** Working memory refers to the active manipulation of information, not just its passive storage. STM is a component of working memory, but working memory involves additional processing.

</details>

## Memory Processes

### Encoding

Encoding is the process of transforming sensory input into a form that can be stored in memory. The effectiveness of encoding depends on the level of processing:

- **Structural encoding:** Processing the physical features of a stimulus (e.g., whether a word is written in uppercase or lowercase). This is the shallowest level of processing and produces the poorest recall.
- **Phonological encoding:** Processing the sound of a stimulus (e.g., whether a word rhymes with another word). This is an intermediate level of processing.
- **Semantic encoding:** Processing the meaning of a stimulus (e.g., whether a word fits into a sentence). This is the deepest level of processing and produces the best recall.

#### Craik and Tulving (1975)

Craik and Tulving provided the seminal evidence for the levels of processing effect. Participants were shown words one at a time and asked questions that required either structural, phonological, or semantic processing. For example:

- Structural: "Is the word written in capital letters?"
- Phonological: "Does the word rhyme with 'train'?"
- Semantic: "Does the word fit into the sentence: 'The _____ was cooking in the kitchen'?"

In a surprise recognition test, participants recognised significantly more words that had been processed semantically than those processed phonologically or structurally. This finding has been replicated extensively and is one of the most robust effects in memory research.

### Storage

Storage is the maintenance of encoded information over time. Information can be stored in two major forms of long-term memory:

| Type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Explicit (declarative) | Conscious recall of facts and events | Remembering what you ate for dinner yesterday |
| -- Episodic | Personal experiences tied to specific times and places | Recalling your first day of school |
| -- Semantic | General knowledge, facts, concepts | Knowing that Paris is the capital of France |
| Implicit (non-declarative) | Unconscious memory that influences behaviour without deliberate recall | Riding a bicycle, playing a piano piece |

### Retrieval

Retrieval is the process of accessing stored information. The effectiveness of retrieval depends on the availability of appropriate cues and the match between the conditions of encoding and the conditions of retrieval.

## Forgetting

### Interference Theory

Interference occurs when other information competes with the target information, reducing recall. There are two types:

- **Proactive interference:** Previously learned information interferes with the recall of newly learned information. For example, if you studied French before studying Spanish, your French vocabulary may interfere with your recall of Spanish words.
- **Retroactive interference:** Newly learned information interferes with the recall of previously learned information. For example, learning new phone numbers may cause you to forget old ones.

Underwood (1957) demonstrated proactive interference by showing that participants who had previously learned multiple lists of nonsense syllables showed progressively poorer recall of subsequent lists, suggesting that accumulated prior learning interferes with new learning.

### Retrieval Failure

Retrieval failure occurs when information is stored in LTM but cannot be accessed because the appropriate retrieval cues are absent. This is explained by the encoding specificity principle (Tulving and Thomson, 1973): recall is improved when the cues available at retrieval match the cues present at encoding.

Tulving and Pearlstone (1966) demonstrated retrieval failure using category cues. Participants recalled more words when given category headings as cues than when asked for free recall, even though the words were clearly stored in memory. This suggests that forgetting is often a retrieval problem rather than a storage problem.

### Decay Theory

Decay theory proposes that memories fade over time simply through the passage of time, as a result of the natural decay of neural traces. While decay is intuitively appealing, it is difficult to test empirically because the passage of time is inevitably confounded with the accumulation of intervening experiences (which could cause interference). Most contemporary psychologists consider interference to be a more important cause of forgetting than decay.

## Schema Theory

### What Is a Schema?

A schema (plural: schemas or schemata) is a cognitive framework or mental representation that organises knowledge about a concept, event, or situation. Schemas are derived from past experience and guide the processing of new information by providing expectations about what should occur in a given context. They influence what we attend to, how we encode information, and how we reconstruct memories.

### Bartlett (1932): War of the Ghosts

Bartlett's study is the foundational investigation of schema theory. He asked British participants to read a Native American folk tale called "The War of the Ghosts," which contained unfamiliar names, concepts, and a narrative structure that differed from Western storytelling conventions. Participants were asked to recall the story at increasing intervals (from immediately after reading to several weeks later).

Key findings:

- Participants systematically distorted the story to make it more consistent with their own cultural schemas.
- Unfamiliar elements were omitted or rationalised (e.g., "canoes" became "boats," "spirits of the dead" became "ghosts" or were omitted entirely).
- The story became shorter, more coherent, and more conventional with each retelling.
- Participants added details that were not in the original story but were consistent with their expectations.

These findings demonstrate that memory is not an accurate record of events but an active, reconstructive process shaped by prior knowledge and cultural expectations.

### Piaget and Schema Theory

Piaget used schema theory as the basis for his theory of cognitive development. He proposed that children develop schemas through two complementary processes:

- **Assimilation:** Incorporating new information into existing schemas. For example, a child who has a schema for "dog" may call a sheep a "dog" because it fits the existing schema.
- **Accommodation:** Modifying existing schemas or creating new schemas in response to information that does not fit. When the child learns that sheep say "baa" and have wool, they accommodate by creating a new schema for "sheep."

Piaget argued that cognitive development proceeds through a series of stages (sensorimotor, preoperational, concrete operational, formal operational), each characterised by qualitatively different schemas and ways of thinking. This theory is discussed further in [Developmental Psychology](./developmental-psychology).

## Reconstructive Memory

### The Principle of Reconstructive Memory

Reconstructive memory refers to the idea that memories are not fixed records of events but are actively reconstructed each time they are retrieved. During reconstruction, memories can be altered by post-event information, expectations, schemas, and the social context of recall.

### Loftus and Palmer (1974): Leading Questions

Loftus and Palmer conducted two experiments investigating the effect of leading questions on eyewitness testimony.

**Experiment 1:** Participants watched a film of a car accident and were then asked, "About how fast were the cars going when they hit each other?" The verb was varied across conditions: "smashed," "collided," "bumped," "hit," or "contacted." The estimated speed varied systematically with the verb: participants in the "smashed" condition estimated an average speed of 40.8 mph, compared with 31.8 mph in the "hit" condition.

**Experiment 2:** Participants were asked the same question with either "smashed" or "hit." One week later, they returned and were asked whether they had seen any broken glass (there was none in the film). Participants in the "smashed" condition were more than twice as likely to falsely report seeing broken glass (32% vs. 14%).

These findings demonstrate that the wording of a question can alter memory. The verb "smashed" led participants to reconstruct a faster, more severe accident, which in turn led them to "remember" details (broken glass) that were not present in the original event.

**Evaluation:**

- The study used a controlled experimental design with random assignment, strengthening internal validity.
- The stimulus was a film, not a real accident, which reduces ecological validity. Participants may have been less emotionally involved than real eyewitnesses.
- Yuille and Cutshall (1986) found that witnesses to a real armed robbery had highly accurate memories 4--5 months after the event, and that the accuracy of their memories was not affected by leading questions. This suggests that the Loftus and Palmer findings may not generalise to all eyewitness situations, particularly those involving high emotional arousal.
- The study has important practical implications for the legal system, supporting the use of cognitive interview techniques that avoid leading questions.

### Bartlett (1932)

As described above, Bartlett's "War of the Ghosts" study demonstrated that memory is reconstructive and influenced by cultural schemas. This study is relevant both to schema theory and to the topic of reconstructive memory.

### Anderson and Pichert (1978)

Anderson and Pichert investigated the role of schemas in the retrieval of information. Participants read a story about two boys burglarising a house from the perspective of either a burglar or a homebuyer. After a delay, participants were asked to recall the story. Half were asked to recall from the same perspective, and half were asked to switch perspectives.

Results: Participants recalled more information consistent with their assigned perspective. More importantly, when participants switched perspectives at recall, they recalled information that they had not previously reported but that was consistent with the new perspective. This demonstrates that information stored in memory is not simply lost; it can be retrieved if the appropriate schema is activated.

## Thinking and Decision Making

### Heuristics

Heuristics are mental shortcuts or rules of thumb that reduce the cognitive effort required for decision making. While heuristics are generally efficient and often produce accurate judgments, they can also lead to systematic errors (biases).

| Heuristic | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Availability heuristic | Judging the frequency or probability of an event by how easily examples come to mind | Overestimating the risk of aeroplane crashes because they receive extensive media coverage |
| Representativeness heuristic | Judging the probability that an object or event belongs to a category based on how similar it is to the prototype for that category | Assuming someone who wears glasses and reads a lot is a librarian, ignoring the much higher base rate of teachers in the population |
| Anchoring heuristic | Relying too heavily on an initial piece of information (the "anchor") when making judgments | Negotiating a salary: the first number mentioned tends to anchor subsequent offers |

Tversky and Kahneman (1973) demonstrated the availability heuristic by asking participants whether there are more English words beginning with "K" or with "K" as the third letter. Participants judged that there are more words beginning with "K," but in fact there are approximately twice as many words with "K" as the third letter. Words beginning with "K" are more available in memory because they are easier to retrieve (they correspond to the first letter of the word, which is how words are stored in the mental lexicon).

### Cognitive Biases

Cognitive biases are systematic patterns of deviation from rationality in judgment and decision making.

**Confirmation bias:** The tendency to seek, interpret, and remember information that confirms pre-existing beliefs while ignoring or discounting disconfirming evidence. Wason (1960) demonstrated this using the 2-4-6 task: participants were told that the number sequence 2-4-6 follows a rule, and were asked to generate sequences to discover the rule. Most participants tested only sequences consistent with their initial hypothesis (e.g., "increasing even numbers") and announced the rule prematurely, failing to test sequences that could disconfirm it.

**Hindsight bias:** The tendency to believe, after an event has occurred, that one would have predicted or expected it. This bias leads people to overestimate the predictability of events and can distort evaluations of decision-making quality. Fischhoff (1975) found that participants who were told the outcome of a historical event rated it as more likely than participants who were not told the outcome, demonstrating that knowledge of the outcome systematically alters the perception of what was predictable.

### Beck's Cognitive Triad

Aaron Beck (1976) proposed that depression is maintained by a cognitive triad of negative beliefs:

1. **Negative views about the self:** "I am worthless, incompetent, unlovable."
2. **Negative views about the world:** "The world is unfair, demanding, unrewarding."
3. **Negative views about the future:** "Things will never get better."

These negative beliefs are maintained by cognitive distortions, which are systematic errors in thinking, such as:

- **All-or-nothing thinking:** Seeing things in black-and-white categories (e.g., "If I am not perfect, I am a failure").
- **Overgeneralisation:** Drawing sweeping conclusions from a single event (e.g., "I failed this test, so I will fail everything").
- **Selective abstraction:** Focusing on a single negative detail while ignoring positive information.
- **Catastrophising:** Expecting the worst possible outcome in every situation.

Beck's cognitive theory of depression is the basis for cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT), which is discussed in [Abnormal Psychology](./abnormal-psychology).

## Emotion and Cognition

### Lazarus's Cognitive Appraisal Theory

Lazarus (1991) proposed that emotions arise from cognitive appraisals of events. The appraisal process involves two stages:

1. **Primary appraisal:** Evaluating whether the event is irrelevant, benign-positive, or stressful. If stressful, it is further evaluated as a harm/loss (damage already done), a threat (potential for future harm), or a challenge (an opportunity for growth or mastery).
2. **Secondary appraisal:** Evaluating one's ability to cope with the stressful event. This involves assessing available resources, options, and the likely effectiveness of different coping strategies.

Emotions are the product of the interaction between primary and secondary appraisals. For example, an upcoming examination may be appraised as a threat (primary appraisal) but also as manageable with adequate preparation (secondary appraisal), resulting in a mild anxiety response rather than terror.

Lazarus emphasised that cognitive appraisal precedes emotional experience, distinguishing his theory from theories that treat emotion as a purely physiological response.

### Schachter and Singer (1962): Two-Factor Theory

Schachter and Singer proposed that emotion results from the interaction of two factors: physiological arousal and cognitive labelling. According to this theory:

1. An event produces physiological arousal (which is general and undifferentiated).
2. The individual cognitively labels the arousal based on the situational context.
3. The combination of arousal and label produces the subjective experience of emotion.

In their classic experiment, participants were injected with either adrenaline (epinephrine) or a placebo. Participants who received adrenaline were divided into three conditions: informed (told about the expected side effects of adrenaline), misinformed (told about side effects that were actually caused by a different substance), and ignorant (told nothing about side effects). All participants were then placed in a room with a confederate who behaved either euphorically (throwing paper airplanes, playing with hula hoops) or angrily (stomping out of the room).

Results: Participants in the ignorant and misinformed conditions were more likely to display the emotion modelled by the confederate (euphoria or anger) than participants in the informed condition, who attributed their arousal to the injection. This supports the two-factor theory: when the source of arousal is known (informed condition), no cognitive labelling of the arousal in terms of emotion occurs; when the source is unknown, participants label their arousal based on the situational context.

**Evaluation:**

- The study has been highly influential but has proven difficult to replicate. Subsequent research has questioned whether arousal is truly undifferentiated (some evidence suggests that different emotions are associated with different patterns of autonomic activity).
- Dutton and Aron (1974) demonstrated a related phenomenon: male participants who crossed a high, suspension bridge (producing arousal from fear) rated a female interviewer as more attractive than participants who crossed a low, stable bridge, supporting the misattribution of arousal.
- The study raises ethical concerns about deception and the administration of a physiological stimulant without full informed consent.

## Eyewitness Testimony

The reliability of eyewitness testimony is a critical issue at the intersection of cognitive and forensic psychology. Research has identified several factors that affect the accuracy of eyewitness testimony:

**Estimator variables:** Factors that cannot be controlled by the criminal justice system, including the duration of exposure to the event, lighting conditions, the presence of a weapon (weapon focus effect), and the race of the perpetrator (cross-race identification effect).

**System variables:** Factors that can be controlled by the criminal justice system, including the wording of questions (leading questions, as demonstrated by Loftus and Palmer), the format of lineups (simultaneous vs. sequential presentation), and the use of cognitive interview techniques.

### The Cognitive Interview

Fisher and Geiselman (1992) developed the cognitive interview as a technique to improve the accuracy and completeness of eyewitness testimony. It is based on four principles derived from memory research:

1. **Context reinstatement:** Encouraging the witness to mentally recreate the physical and emotional context of the event (the environment, weather, sounds, feelings).
2. **Report everything:** Instructing the witness to report every detail, no matter how trivial or irrelevant it seems, without editing or filtering.
3. **Recall from different perspectives:** Asking the witness to recall the event from different perspectives (e.g., from the perpetrator's point of view).
4. **Recall in different orders:** Asking the witness to recall the event in reverse chronological order or starting from different points.

Meta-analyses (Kohnken et al., 1999) have found that the cognitive interview produces significantly more correct information than standard interview techniques, without a significant increase in incorrect information. However, the technique requires training and is more time-consuming than standard interviews.

<details>
<summary>Common Pitfalls: Eyewitness Testimony</summary>

- **Do not assume that confident eyewitnesses are accurate.** Confidence is a poor predictor of accuracy. Eyewitnesses who are highly confident may be no more accurate than those who express doubt, and confidence can be inflated by post-event feedback (e.g., confirming a witness's identification).
- **Do not overgeneralise the Loftus and Palmer findings.** Not all leading questions are equally effective, and not all eyewitnesses are equally susceptible. Individual differences (age, stress level, prior knowledge) moderate the effect.
- **Do not confuse estimator and system variables.** Estimator variables are characteristics of the event or witness that cannot be controlled; system variables are aspects of the justice system procedure that can be modified to improve accuracy.

</details>
