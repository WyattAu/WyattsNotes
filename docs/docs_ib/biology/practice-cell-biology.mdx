### Cell Division

<PracticeProblem question="During which phase of mitosis do chromosomes align at the metaphase plate and attach to spindle fibres via their centromeres?" options={['Prophase', 'Metaphase', 'Anaphase', 'Telophase']} correctAnswer={1} explanation="In metaphase, chromosomes are fully condensed and align at the cell equator (metaphase plate). Kinetochore microtubules from opposite poles attach to centromeres of sister chromatids. This alignment ensures equal distribution of chromosomes to daughter cells during anaphase." difficulty="easy" />

<PracticeProblem question="A cell with a diploid number (2n) of 6 undergoes meiosis. How many chromosomes are in each daughter cell and what is their genetic composition?" options={['6 chromosomes, all identical', '3 chromosomes, genetically unique', '6 chromosomes, genetically unique', '3 chromosomes, all identical']} correctAnswer={1} explanation="Meiosis produces haploid (n) cells. Starting with 2n = 6, each gamete has n = 3 chromosomes. Crossing over (prophase I) and independent assortment (metaphase I) ensure each gamete is genetically unique. Mitosis produces 2n = 6 identical cells, not meiosis." difficulty="medium" />

<PracticeProblem question="Which two processes during meiosis generate genetic diversity in gametes?" options={['DNA replication and cytokinesis', 'Crossing over and independent assortment', 'Spindle fibre formation and chromosome condensation', 'DNA replication and crossing over']} correctAnswer={1} explanation="Crossing over (prophase I): homologous chromosomes exchange segments of DNA, creating new combinations of alleles on each chromosome. Independent assortment (metaphase I): the random orientation of homologous chromosome pairs means different combinations of maternal and paternal chromosomes end up in each gamete. Together they produce enormous genetic variation." difficulty="medium" />

### Transport

<PracticeProblem question="Which type of transport requires ATP and moves molecules against their concentration gradient?" options={['Facilitated diffusion', 'Osmosis', 'Active transport', 'Simple diffusion']} correctAnswer={2} explanation="Active transport uses ATP (energy) to move molecules against the concentration gradient (from low to high concentration), via pump proteins in the membrane. Facilitated diffusion and simple diffusion are passive (no energy, move down the gradient). Osmosis is the passive movement of water." difficulty="easy" />

<PracticeProblem question="In co-transport in the small intestine, what drives the uptake of glucose into the epithelial cell against its concentration gradient?" options={['ATP directly hydrolysed by a glucose pump', 'The Na⁺ concentration gradient maintained by the Na⁺/K⁺ pump', 'Glucose diffusing through a channel protein', 'Water potential driving glucose through aquaporins']} correctAnswer={1} explanation="The Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase pump (on the basolateral membrane) actively pumps Na⁺ out of the cell, maintaining a low intracellular [Na⁺]. The energy comes indirectly from ATP (used by the Na⁺/K⁺ pump)." difficulty="hard" />
