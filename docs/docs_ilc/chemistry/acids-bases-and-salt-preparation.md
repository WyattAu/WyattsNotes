# Acids, Bases and Salt Preparation

This topic covers the properties of acids and bases, pH, strong and weak acids, buffers, and methods
of salt preparation. It is essential for both Ordinary and Higher Level.

## Definitions of Acids and Bases (OL/HL)

### Arrhenius Definition

- **Acid:** produces $\text{H}^+$ (or $\text{H}_3\text{O}^+$) in water.
- **Base:** produces $\text{OH}^-$ in water.

### Bronsted-Lowry Definition (HL)

- **Acid:** proton ($\text{H}^+$) donor.
- **Base:** proton acceptor.

### Conjugate Acid-Base Pairs (HL)

When an acid donates a proton, it forms its conjugate base. When a base accepts a proton, it forms
its conjugate acid.

$$
\text{HA} + \text{H}_2\text{O} \rightleftharpoons \text{H}_3\text{O}^+ + \text{A}^-
$$

- $\text{HA}$ and $\text{A}^-$ form a conjugate pair.
- $\text{H}_2\text{O}$ and $\text{H}_3\text{O}^+$ form a conjugate pair.

**Example (HL):** Identify the conjugate acid-base pairs in:

$$
\text{NH}_3 + \text{H}_2\text{O} \rightleftharpoons \text{NH}_4^+ + \text{OH}^-
$$

$\text{NH}_3/\text{NH}_4^+$ and $\text{H}_2\text{O}/\text{OH}^-$.

### Lewis Definition (HL)

- **Acid:** electron pair acceptor.
- **Base:** electron pair donor.

## Strong and Weak Acids and Bases (OL/HL)

### Strong Acids

Completely dissociate in water: $\text{HCl}$, $\text{HNO}_3$, $\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$ (first
dissociation).

$$
\text{HCl} + \text{H}_2\text{O} \to \text{H}_3\text{O}^+ + \text{Cl}^-
$$

### Weak Acids

Partially dissociate in water: $\text{CH}_3\text{COOH}$, $\text{HF}$, $\text{H}_2\text{CO}_3$.

$$
\text{CH}_3\text{COOH} + \text{H}_2\text{O} \rightleftharpoons \text{H}_3\text{O}^+ + \text{CH}_3\text{COO}^-
$$

### Strong Bases

Completely dissociate: $\text{NaOH}$, $\text{KOH}$, $\text{Ca(OH)}_2$.

### Weak Bases

Partially dissociate: $\text{NH}_3$.

$$
\text{NH}_3 + \text{H}_2\text{O} \rightleftharpoons \text{NH}_4^+ + \text{OH}^-
$$

## The pH Scale (OL/HL)

$$
\text{pH} = -\log_{10}[\text{H}_3\text{O}^+]
$$

At $25°$C: $[\text{H}_3\text{O}^+][\text{OH}^-] = 10^{-14}$.

| pH  | [H3O+]              | Description     |
| --- | ------------------- | --------------- |
| 0   | $1\text{ M}$        | Strongly acidic |
| 7   | $10^{-7}\text{ M}$  | Neutral         |
| 14  | $10^{-14}\text{ M}$ | Strongly basic  |

**Example (OL):** Find the pH of a $0.01\text{ M}$ solution of $\text{HCl}$.

$$
[\text{H}_3\text{O}^+] = 0.01 = 10^{-2}\text{ M} \implies \text{pH} = 2
$$

**Example (HL):** Find the pH of a $0.10\text{ M}$ solution of $\text{CH}_3\text{COOH}$
($K_a = 1.8 \times 10^{-5}$).

$$
K_a = \frac{[\text{H}_3\text{O}^+][\text{CH}_3\text{COO}^-]}{[\text{CH}_3\text{COOH}]} = \frac{x^2}{0.10 - x} \approx \frac{x^2}{0.10}
$$

$$
x = \sqrt{0.10 \times 1.8 \times 10^{-5}} = \sqrt{1.8 \times 10^{-6}} = 1.34 \times 10^{-3}\text{ M}
$$

$$
\text{pH} = -\log(1.34 \times 10^{-3}) = 2.87
$$

## Acid Dissociation Constant ($K_a$) (HL)

$$
K_a = \frac{[\text{H}_3\text{O}^+][\text{A}^-]}{[\text{HA}]}
$$

$$
\text{p}K_a = -\log K_a
$$

The larger the $K_a$, the stronger the acid.

**Relationship:** $\text{pH} + \text{pOH} = 14$ at $25°$C.

## pH Calculations (HL)

### Strong Monoprotic Acid

$$
\text{pH} = -\log[\text{H}_3\text{O}^+]
$$

### Strong Diprotic Acid

For $\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$ (first dissociation complete):

$$
[\text{H}_3\text{O}^+] = 2c \quad (\text{if both protons dissociate fully})
$$

### Dilution

$$
c_1 V_1 = c_2 V_2
$$

**Example (HL):** 25 mL of $0.10\text{ M}$ $\text{HCl}$ is diluted to 250 mL. Find the new pH.

$$
c_2 = \frac{0.10 \times 25}{250} = 0.010\text{ M}
$$

$$
\text{pH} = -\log(0.010) = 2
$$

## Buffers (HL)

A buffer solution resists changes in pH when small amounts of acid or base are added. It consists of
a weak acid and its conjugate base (or a weak base and its conjugate acid).

### Buffer pH Equation (Henderson-Hasselbalch)

$$
\text{pH} = \text{p}K_a + \log\frac{[\text{A}^-]}{[\text{HA}]}
$$

**Example (HL):** A buffer contains $0.10\text{ M}$ $\text{CH}_3\text{COOH}$ and $0.15\text{ M}$
$\text{CH}_3\text{COONa}$. Find the pH. ($\text{p}K_a = 4.76$)

$$
\text{pH} = 4.76 + \log\frac{0.15}{0.10} = 4.76 + \log 1.5 = 4.76 + 0.18 = 4.94
$$

### How Buffers Work

When acid ($\text{H}^+$) is added: the conjugate base reacts with $\text{H}^+$, removing it.

$$
\text{CH}_3\text{COO}^- + \text{H}_3\text{O}^+ \to \text{CH}_3\text{COOH} + \text{H}_2\text{O}
$$

When base ($\text{OH}^-$) is added: the weak acid reacts with $\text{OH}^-$, neutralising it.

$$
\text{CH}_3\text{COOH} + \text{OH}^- \to \text{CH}_3\text{COO}^- + \text{H}_2\text{O}
$$

## Neutralisation Reactions (OL/HL)

Acid + Base $\to$ Salt + Water

**Example (OL):**

$$
\text{HCl} + \text{NaOH} \to \text{NaCl} + \text{H}_2\text{O}
$$

$$
\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4 + 2\text{NaOH} \to \text{Na}_2\text{SO}_4 + 2\text{H}_2\text{O}
$$

## Salt Preparation (OL/HL)

### Methods of Preparation

1. **Acid + Metal:**

$$
\text{Zn} + \text{H}_2\text{SO}_4 \to \text{ZnSO}_4 + \text{H}_2
$$

2. **Acid + Base (neutralisation):**

$$
\text{HCl} + \text{NaOH} \to \text{NaCl} + \text{H}_2\text{O}
$$

3. **Acid + Carbonate:**

$$
\text{HCl} + \text{CaCO}_3 \to \text{CaCl}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O} + \text{CO}_2
$$

4. **Acid + Insoluble Base (titration method):**

Add the acid to the insoluble base (e.g., $\text{CuO}$) until no more dissolves. Filter and
evaporate.

5. **Precipitation (mixing two solutions):**

$$
\text{AgNO}_3 + \text{NaCl} \to \text{AgCl} + \text{NaNO}_3
$$

### Choosing the Method

| Desired salt                      | Method                      |
| --------------------------------- | --------------------------- |
| Sodium, potassium, ammonium salts | Titration or evaporation    |
| Soluble salts of other metals     | Metal + acid or base + acid |
| Insoluble salts                   | Precipitation               |

## Indicators (OL/HL)

| Indicator           | Colour in acid | Colour in base | pH range    |
| ------------------- | -------------- | -------------- | ----------- |
| Litmus              | Red            | Blue           | 4.5 -- 8.3  |
| Methyl orange       | Red            | Yellow         | 3.1 -- 4.4  |
| Phenolphthalein     | Colourless     | Pink           | 8.3 -- 10.0 |
| Universal indicator | Various        | Various        | 1 -- 14     |

### Choosing an Indicator for Titrations (HL)

The indicator must change colour within the pH range of the equivalence point.

- Strong acid + strong base: any indicator (pH change large).
- Strong acid + weak base: methyl orange (equivalence pH < 7).
- Weak acid + strong base: phenolphthalein (equivalence pH > 7).
- Weak acid + weak base: no suitable indicator.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Strong vs weak** -- strong acids/bases dissociate completely; weak ones partially.
2. **pH of weak acids** -- do not assume $[\text{H}^+] = c$; use $K_a$.
3. **Buffer calculations** -- the Henderson-Hasselbalch equation uses the ratio of base to acid.
4. **Diprotic acids** -- each mole can donate two protons.
5. **Indicator choice** -- match the indicator range to the equivalence point pH.

## Practice Questions

### Ordinary Level

1. Define acid and base using the Arrhenius definition.
2. Find the pH of a $0.001\text{ M}$ solution of $\text{NaOH}$.
3. Write the balanced equation for the reaction between $\text{HNO}_3$ and $\text{KOH}$.
4. Describe how to prepare a sample of $\text{ZnSO}_4$ crystals from zinc and dilute sulfuric acid.

### Higher Level

1. Calculate the pH of a $0.15\text{ M}$ solution of $\text{HF}$ ($K_a = 6.8 \times 10^{-4}$).
2. Calculate the pH of a buffer containing $0.20\text{ M}$ $\text{NH}_3$ and $0.25\text{ M}$
   $\text{NH}_4\text{Cl}$ ($K_b = 1.8 \times 10^{-5}$).
3. Explain why the pH of a $0.01\text{ M}$ solution of $\text{HCl}$ is 2 but the pH of a
   $0.01\text{ M}$ solution of $\text{CH}_3\text{COOH}$ is greater than 2.
4. 50 mL of $0.10\text{ M}$ $\text{NaOH}$ is added to 50 mL of $0.10\text{ M}$
   $\text{CH}_3\text{COOH}$. Calculate the pH of the resulting solution.
