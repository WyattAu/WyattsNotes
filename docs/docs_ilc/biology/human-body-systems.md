# Human Body Systems

This topic covers the major organ systems of the human body: the circulatory, respiratory,
digestive, nervous, endocrine, immune, and excretory systems.

## The Circulatory System

### Structure (OL/HL)

The human circulatory system is a **closed double circulatory system**:

- **Pulmonary circulation:** heart to lungs and back (deoxygenated blood to lungs, oxygenated blood
  returns).
- **Systemic circulation:** heart to body and back (oxygenated blood to body, deoxygenated blood
  returns).

### The Heart (OL/HL)

The heart has four chambers:

- **Right atrium:** receives deoxygenated blood from the body (via vena cava).
- **Right ventricle:** pumps deoxygenated blood to the lungs (via pulmonary artery).
- **Left atrium:** receives oxygenated blood from the lungs (via pulmonary vein).
- **Left ventricle:** pumps oxygenated blood to the body (via aorta).

**Valves:** prevent backflow of blood.

- Bicuspid (mitral) valve: between left atrium and left ventricle.
- Tricuspid valve: between right atrium and right ventricle.
- Semilunar valves: in the aorta and pulmonary artery.

### Blood Vessels (OL/HL)

| Vessel    | Structure                          | Function                                     |
| --------- | ---------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------- |
| Artery    | Thick walls, narrow lumen, elastic | Carry blood away from heart at high pressure |
| Vein      | Thin walls, wide lumen, valves     | Return blood to the heart                    |
| Capillary | One cell thick walls               | Exchange of materials with tissues           |

### Blood Composition (OL/HL)

- **Plasma:** liquid component (water, dissolved substances, proteins).
- **Red blood cells (erythrocytes):** transport oxygen (contain haemoglobin). No nucleus. Biconcave
  disc shape.
- **White blood cells (leucocytes):** part of the immune system (phagocytes, lymphocytes).
- **Platelets (thrombocytes):** blood clotting.

### Cardiac Cycle (HL)

The sequence of events in one heartbeat:

1. **Atrial systole:** atria contract, pushing blood into ventricles.
2. **Ventricular systole:** ventricles contract, pushing blood into arteries.
3. **Diastole:** all chambers relax; heart fills with blood.

### Blood Pressure (OL/HL)

Measured in mmHg. Normal: approximately 120/80.

- **Systolic pressure:** pressure during ventricular contraction.
- **Diastolic pressure:** pressure during relaxation.

Hypertension (high blood pressure) increases the risk of heart disease and stroke.

## The Respiratory System

### Structure (OL/HL)

Air path: nose $\to$ trachea $\to$ bronchi $\to$ bronchioles $\to$ alveoli.

### Gas Exchange (OL/HL)

Occurs in the alveoli by diffusion.

**Features of alveoli for efficient gas exchange:**

- Large surface area.
- Thin walls (one cell thick).
- Surrounded by many capillaries.
- Moist surface for gas dissolution.

**Oxygen** diffuses from alveoli into blood (high to low concentration).

**Carbon dioxide** diffuses from blood into alveoli.

### Breathing Mechanism (OL/HL)

**Inspiration:** intercostal muscles and diaphragm contract; thorax volume increases; lung pressure
decreases; air rushes in.

**Expiration:** intercostal muscles and diaphragm relax; thorax volume decreases; lung pressure
increases; air is pushed out.

## The Digestive System

### Structure (OL/HL)

Mouth $\to$ oesophagus $\to$ stomach $\to$ small intestine (duodenum, ileum) $\to$ large intestine
$\to$ rectum $\to$ anus.

Accessory organs: liver, pancreas, gall bladder.

### Digestion (OL/HL)

**Mechanical digestion:** chewing (mouth), churning (stomach), peristalsis.

**Chemical digestion:** breakdown by enzymes.

| Location | Enzyme  | Substrate | Product                |
| -------- | ------- | --------- | ---------------------- |
| Mouth    | Amylase | Starch    | Maltose                |
| Stomach  | Pepsin  | Proteins  | Peptides               |
| Duodenum | Trypsin | Proteins  | Amino acids            |
| Duodenum | Lipase  | Lipids    | Fatty acids + glycerol |
| Ileum    | Maltase | Maltose   | Glucose                |

### Absorption (OL/HL)

- Occurs in the small intestine (ileum).
- Villi increase surface area.
- Features of villi: large surface area, thin walls, dense capillary network, lacteal (for fat
  absorption).
- Glucose and amino acids absorbed into blood capillaries.
- Fatty acids and glycerol absorbed into lacteals (lymphatic system).

### The Liver (OL/HL)

Functions:

- Produces bile (emulsifies fats).
- Detoxification (breaks down alcohol, drugs).
- Storage of glycogen, iron, vitamins.
- Deamination of excess amino acids.
- Regulation of blood glucose levels.

## The Nervous System (OL/HL)

### Neurons

- **Sensory neurons:** carry impulses from receptors to the CNS.
- **Motor neurons:** carry impulses from the CNS to effectors.
- **Relay neurons:** connect sensory and motor neurons within the CNS.

### Structure of a Motor Neuron

- Cell body with nucleus.
- Dendrites (receive impulses).
- Long axon (transmits impulses).
- Myelin sheath (insulates axon, speeds up transmission).
- Synaptic terminals (at the ends of axons).

### Synapse (OL/HL)

The junction between two neurons.

1. Electrical impulse arrives at the synaptic terminal.
2. Neurotransmitter vesicles fuse with the membrane.
3. Neurotransmitter is released into the synaptic cleft.
4. Neurotransmitter binds to receptors on the post-synaptic membrane.
5. A new electrical impulse is generated in the next neuron.

### Reflex Arc (OL/HL)

An involuntary, rapid response to a stimulus.

Pathway: receptor $\to$ sensory neuron $\to$ relay neuron $\to$ motor neuron $\to$ effector
(muscle/gland).

**Example:** touching a hot object:

1. Heat receptors detect the stimulus.
2. Sensory neuron transmits impulse to spinal cord.
3. Relay neuron connects to motor neuron.
4. Motor neuron stimulates effector (arm muscle to withdraw hand).

## The Endocrine System (HL)

### Hormones

Chemical messengers secreted by endocrine glands into the bloodstream, targeting specific organs.

| Gland     | Hormone      | Function                                |
| --------- | ------------ | --------------------------------------- |
| Pituitary | ADH          | Regulates water reabsorption in kidneys |
| Pituitary | FSH/LH       | Control reproductive system             |
| Thyroid   | Thyroxine    | Regulates metabolism                    |
| Pancreas  | Insulin      | Lowers blood glucose                    |
| Pancreas  | Glucagon     | Raises blood glucose                    |
| Adrenal   | Adrenaline   | "Fight or flight" response              |
| Ovary     | Oestrogen    | Female secondary sexual characteristics |
| Testis    | Testosterone | Male secondary sexual characteristics   |

### Blood Glucose Regulation (HL)

**High blood glucose:** pancreas secretes insulin.

- Liver and muscle cells take up glucose.
- Glucose is converted to glycogen (glycogenesis).

**Low blood glucose:** pancreas secretes glucagon.

- Glycogen is converted back to glucose (glycogenolysis).
- Fat is broken down (lipolysis).

### Negative Feedback (HL)

Hormone levels are regulated by negative feedback. When the level rises above the set point,
mechanisms reduce it. When it falls below, mechanisms increase it.

## The Immune System (OL/HL)

### Non-Specific Defences

- Skin: physical barrier.
- Mucus membranes: trap pathogens.
- Stomach acid: destroys pathogens.
- Phagocytes: engulf and digest pathogens (phagocytosis).

### Specific Defences (HL)

**Lymphocytes:**

- **B cells:** produce antibodies. Each B cell produces one type of antibody specific to one
  antigen.
- **T cells:** destroy infected cells (T killer cells) and coordinate the immune response (T helper
  cells).

**Antibodies:**

- Y-shaped proteins.
- Bind to specific antigens on pathogens.
- Mark pathogens for destruction by phagocytes.
- Cause agglutination (clumping) of pathogens.

**Memory cells:** remain after infection, providing long-term immunity.

### Vaccination (OL/HL)

- Introduces a harmless form of the pathogen (or antigen).
- Stimulates the production of antibodies and memory cells.
- Provides active artificial immunity.

## The Excretory System (OL/HL)

### Kidneys (OL/HL)

Functions: remove urea, regulate water balance, regulate ion concentrations.

**Structure:** cortex (contains glomeruli and Bowman's capsules), medulla (contains loops of Henle
and collecting ducts).

### Urine Formation (HL)

1. **Ultrafiltration:** in the glomerulus, high blood pressure forces small molecules (water,
   glucose, urea, ions) into the Bowman's capsule. Large molecules (proteins, blood cells) remain.

2. **Selective reabsorption:** in the proximal convoluted tubule, all glucose, amino acids, and most
   water and ions are reabsorbed back into the blood.

3. **Water regulation:** in the loop of Henle and collecting duct, water is reabsorbed under the
   control of ADH (antidiuretic hormone).

### ADH and Water Balance (HL)

- High blood osmolarity (dehydration): more ADH is released $\to$ more water reabsorbed $\to$
  concentrated urine.
- Low blood osmolarity (overhydration): less ADH released $\to$ less water reabsorbed $\to$ dilute
  urine.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Arteries carry oxygenated blood** -- except the pulmonary artery (carries deoxygenated).
2. **Veins carry deoxygenated blood** -- except the pulmonary vein (carries oxygenated).
3. **Oxygen diffuses from alveoli to blood** -- down the concentration gradient.
4. **Insulin lowers blood glucose** -- glucagon raises it.
5. **Antibodies are produced by B cells** -- not T cells.
6. **Ultrafiltration vs reabsorption** -- know what is filtered and what is reabsorbed in the
   kidney.
