# Plant Biology

This topic covers plant structure, transport in plants, reproduction, growth, and plant responses to
stimuli.

## Plant Structure

### Plant Tissues (OL/HL)

**Dermal tissue (epidermis):** outer protective layer. May have a waxy cuticle to reduce water loss.
Root hair cells are specialised epidermal cells for absorption.

**Ground tissue (parenchyma):** fills the interior of the plant. Involved in photosynthesis,
storage, and support.

**Vascular tissue:** consists of xylem and phloem.

- **Xylem:** transports water and dissolved minerals from roots to leaves. Composed of dead, hollow
  cells strengthened by lignin.
- **Phloem:** transports organic substances (mainly sucrose) from leaves to the rest of the plant.
  Composed of living cells (sieve tube elements and companion cells).

### Leaf Structure (OL/HL)

**Adaptations for photosynthesis:**

- Large surface area.
- Thin -- short diffusion distance.
- Many stomata for gas exchange.
- Chloroplasts containing chlorophyll.
- Extensive vein network (xylem and phloem).

**Cross-section of a leaf:**

- **Upper epidermis:** covered by waxy cuticle.
- **Palisade mesophyll:** tightly packed cells with many chloroplasts (main photosynthetic tissue).
- **Spongy mesophyll:** loosely packed cells with air spaces for gas exchange.
- **Lower epidermis:** contains stomata with guard cells.
- **Vascular bundles:** xylem (top) and phloem (bottom).

### Root Structure (OL/HL)

**Functions:** anchorage, absorption of water and minerals.

**Root hair cells:** increase surface area for absorption.

**Root tip zones:**

1. **Zone of cell division (meristem):** cells actively divide.
2. **Zone of elongation:** cells grow longer.
3. **Zone of differentiation:** cells specialise.
4. **Zone of maturation:** root hairs develop.

### Stem Structure (HL)

**Dicot stem cross-section (from outside to inside):**

1. Epidermis.
2. Cortex (parenchyma).
3. Vascular bundles (arranged in a ring): each contains xylem (inner) and phloem (outer) with
   cambium between.
4. Pith (central parenchyma).

**Monocot stem:** vascular bundles are scattered.

## Transport in Plants

### Transpiration (OL/HL)

The loss of water vapour from the leaves through the stomata.

**Transpiration stream:**

1. Water is absorbed by root hair cells by osmosis.
2. Water moves across the root cortex by osmosis and through the symplastic pathway.
3. Water enters the xylem.
4. Water moves up the xylem by root pressure and, primarily, the **cohesion-tension theory**.

### Cohesion-Tension Theory (HL)

1. Water evaporates from the spongy mesophyll cells into the air spaces (transpiration).
2. This creates a tension (negative pressure) that pulls water up the xylem.
3. Water molecules cohere to each other (hydrogen bonding), creating a continuous column.
4. Water molecules adhere to the walls of the xylem.
5. The transpiration pull is the main force driving water up the plant.

### Factors Affecting Transpiration Rate (OL/HL)

| Factor          | Effect                                     |
| --------------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| Temperature     | Higher temperature increases rate          |
| Humidity        | Higher humidity decreases rate             |
| Wind speed      | Higher wind speed increases rate           |
| Light intensity | Higher light increases rate (stomata open) |

### Translocation (HL)

The transport of organic substances (mainly sucrose) in the phloem.

**Mass flow hypothesis (pressure flow hypothesis):**

1. Sucrose is actively loaded into the phloem at the source (e.g., leaves).
2. This lowers the water potential in the phloem.
3. Water enters by osmosis from the xylem, creating high pressure.
4. At the sink (e.g., roots, fruits), sucrose is unloaded.
5. Water leaves the phloem, lowering the pressure.
6. This creates a pressure gradient from source to sink, driving bulk flow.

**Evidence for translocation:**

- Aphids feed on phloem sap (high sucrose concentration).
- Ringing a stem (removing phloem) causes swelling above the ring.
- Radioactive tracers (${}^{14}\text{C}$) show movement from leaves to other parts.

## Plant Reproduction

### Sexual Reproduction in Flowering Plants (OL/HL)

### Flower Structure

- **Sepal:** protects the flower bud.
- **Petal:** attracts pollinators.
- **Stamen (male):** anther (produces pollen) + filament.
- **Carpel/Pistil (female):** stigma (receives pollen) + style + ovary (contains ovules).

### Pollination (OL/HL)

The transfer of pollen from anther to stigma.

**Self-pollination:** pollen from the same plant. **Cross-pollination:** pollen from a different
plant (genetically advantageous).

**Agents of pollination:** wind, insects, birds, water.

### Fertilisation (OL/HL)

1. Pollen grain lands on the stigma and germinates.
2. A pollen tube grows down the style to the ovule.
3. The pollen tube nucleus travels down the tube.
4. **Double fertilisation** occurs:
   - One male gamete fuses with the egg cell $\to$ zygote (2n).
   - The other male gamete fuses with two polar nuclei $\to$ endosperm (3n).

### Seed and Fruit Development (OL/HL)

- The zygote develops into the embryo.
- The endosperm provides nutrition.
- The ovule develops into the seed.
- The ovary develops into the fruit.

### Seed Structure (OL/HL)

- **Seed coat (testa):** protective outer layer.
- **Embryo:** consists of the radicle (future root), plumule (future shoot), and cotyledon(s) (seed
  leaves).
- **Endosperm:** food store (in some species; in others, food is stored in the cotyledons).

### Germination (OL/HL)

**Conditions for germination:**

- Water (for enzyme activation and metabolic processes).
- Oxygen (for aerobic respiration).
- Suitable temperature (for optimal enzyme activity).
- Some seeds also require light or specific temperature treatments (stratification).

**Process:**

1. Water is absorbed (imbibition).
2. Enzymes are activated.
3. Stored food (starch) is broken down into soluble sugars.
4. The radicle emerges first, followed by the plumule.
5. Photosynthesis begins once the plumule reaches light.

### Asexual Reproduction (Vegetative Propagation) (HL)

Methods include:

- **Runners** (strawberries).
- **Tubers** (potatoes).
- **Bulbs** (onions, daffodils).
- **Cuttings:** a piece of stem is cut and placed in soil, where it develops roots.

**Advantages:** rapid, no pollinator needed, produces genetically identical offspring.

**Disadvantages:** no genetic variation, susceptible to the same diseases.

## Plant Growth

### Growth Regions (OL/HL)

- **Apical meristems:** at the tips of roots and shoots (primary growth, increase in length).
- **Lateral meristems (cambium):** in the vascular bundles (secondary growth, increase in girth).

### Plant Hormones (HL)

| Hormone             | Effect                                             |
| ------------------- | -------------------------------------------------- |
| Auxin (IAA)         | Cell elongation, apical dominance, root initiation |
| Gibberellin         | Stem elongation, seed germination                  |
| Cytokinin           | Cell division, delays leaf senescence              |
| Ethylene            | Fruit ripening, leaf fall                          |
| Abscisic acid (ABA) | Inhibits growth, closes stomata (stress response)  |

### Apical Dominance (HL)

Auxin produced by the apical bud inhibits the growth of lateral buds. Removing the apical bud allows
lateral buds to grow (used in pruning).

### Tropisms (OL/HL)

A tropism is a growth response to a directional stimulus.

- **Phototropism:** response to light. Shoots grow towards light (positive phototropism); roots grow
  away (negative phototropism).
- **Geotropism (gravitropism):** response to gravity. Roots grow towards gravity (positive); shoots
  grow away (negative).

**Mechanism (HL):** Auxin redistributes unevenly:

- In shoots: more auxin on the shaded side causes cells to elongate more, bending towards light.
- In roots: more auxin on the lower side inhibits cell elongation, causing the root to bend
  downward.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Xylem vs phloem** -- xylem carries water and minerals up; phloem carries organic substances
   both up and down.
2. **Transpiration vs translocation** -- transpiration is water loss; translocation is sugar
   transport.
3. **Double fertilisation** -- one gamete fertilises the egg, the other fertilises the polar nuclei
   (unique to flowering plants).
4. **Tropisms** -- know the direction of response for both shoots and roots.
5. **Seed structure** -- monocots have one cotyledon; dicots have two.
