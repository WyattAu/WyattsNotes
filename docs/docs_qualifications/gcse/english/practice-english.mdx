### Poetry

<PracticeProblem question="A poet uses enjambment in their poem. What effect does this technique create?" options={[ 'It carries the meaning of a line over to the next line without a pause, creating a sense of flow or urgency', 'It repeats the same line at regular intervals to emphasise a key idea', 'It ends each line with a strong punctuation mark to create a slow, deliberate pace', "It arranges words in a visual shape that matches the poem’s subject", ]} correctAnswer={0} explanation="Enjambment is when a sentence or thought continues beyond the end of a line without any punctuation or pause. This pulls the reader forward, creating momentum and a sense of flow. It can also create tension, urgency, or a feeling of thoughts cascading. It contrasts with end-stopped lines, where each line forms a complete unit of sense with punctuation at the end." difficulty="medium" />

<PracticeProblem question="In a poem about war, the writer describes soldiers' boots as 'ground down to whispers.' What technique is being used here?" options={['Metaphor', 'Simile', 'Hyperbole', 'Alliteration']} correctAnswer={0} explanation="The phrase describes the boots as 'whispers,' directly stating they are something else without using 'like' or 'as.' This is a metaphor, comparing the worn-down state of the boots to whispers to convey silence, exhaustion, and the wearing down of the soldiers themselves. A simile would use 'like' or 'as' for the comparison. Hyperbole is deliberate exaggeration, and alliteration is repetition of initial consonant sounds." difficulty="easy" />
