# GCSE Chemistry Study Guide

A complete single-page revision guide covering the full GCSE Chemistry specification. Each section
contains key concepts, definitions, and essential facts. Use this alongside the
[full topic pages](chemistry/) for detailed derivations and further practice.

---

## 1. Atomic Structure

### 1.1 Subatomic Particles

| Particle | Relative mass            | Relative charge | Location        |
| -------- | ------------------------ | --------------- | --------------- |
| Proton   | 1                        | +1              | Nucleus         |
| Neutron  | 1                        | 0               | Nucleus         |
| Electron | $\approx \frac{1}{1836}$ | $-1$            | Electron shells |

- **Atomic number ($Z$):** The number of protons in the nucleus. Defines the element.
- **Mass number ($A$):** The total number of protons and neutrons.
- In a neutral atom: number of protons = number of electrons.

### 1.2 Isotopes

Isotopes of an element have the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons.

**Example.** Carbon-12 ($^{12}_{6}\text{C}$) and Carbon-14 ($^{14}_{6}\text{C}$):

- Both have 6 protons (both are carbon).
- Carbon-12 has 6 neutrons; Carbon-14 has 8 neutrons.

Isotopes have identical chemical properties (same electron configuration) but different physical
properties (different masses).

**Relative atomic mass ($A_r$):** The weighted average mass of an atom of an element relative to
1/12 the mass of a carbon-12 atom.

$$A_r = \frac{\sum (\text{isotope abundance} \times \text{isotope mass})}{\sum (\text{isotope abundance})}$$

### 1.3 Electron Configuration

Electrons occupy shells (energy levels). Maximum per shell: 2, 8, 18, 32.

**Shell notation:** Potassium ($Z = 19$) has the configuration 2.8.8.1.

Electrons in the outermost shell are called **valence electrons**. These determine the chemical
properties of the element.

Elements in the same group of the periodic table have the same number of valence electrons and
similar chemical properties.

### 1.4 Development of the Atomic Model

| Scientist  | Model           | Key idea                                                                       |
| ---------- | --------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Dalton     | Solid sphere    | Atoms are indivisible particles                                                |
| Thomson    | Plum pudding    | Electrons embedded in a positive sphere                                        |
| Rutherford | Nuclear model   | Dense positive nucleus; most of the atom is empty space (gold foil experiment) |
| Bohr       | Electron shells | Electrons orbit the nucleus in fixed energy levels                             |
| Chadwick   | Updated nuclear | Discovered the neutron                                                         |

## 2. Bonding

### 2.1 Ionic Bonding

Ionic bonds form between **metals and non-metals**. Metal atoms lose electrons to become positive
ions (cations); non-metal atoms gain electrons to become negative ions (anions). The electrostatic
attraction between oppositely charged ions is the ionic bond.

**Example.** Sodium chloride:

$$\text{Na} \rightarrow \text{Na}^+ + e^- \qquad \text{Cl} + e^- \rightarrow \text{Cl}^-$$

**Dot-and-cross diagram for NaCl:**

- Na: $\times \text{Na} \times$ (outer shell 1 electron transferred)
- Cl: $\cdot \text{Cl} \cdot$ (gains electron to complete outer shell)

**Properties of ionic compounds:**

- High melting and boiling points (strong electrostatic attraction in a giant lattice).
- Conduct electricity when molten or in solution (ions are free to move), but not when solid (ions
  are fixed in lattice).
- Brittle (shifting layers brings like charges together, causing repulsion).

### 2.2 Covalent Bonding

Covalent bonds form between **non-metals**. Atoms share pairs of electrons to achieve a full outer
shell.

**Simple covalent molecules** (e.g. $\text{H}_2\text{O}$, $\text{CO}_2$, $\text{CH}_4$):

- Low melting and boiling points (weak intermolecular forces between molecules, not the covalent
  bonds themselves).
- Do not conduct electricity (no ions or free electrons).
- In most cases gases or liquids at room temperature.

**Giant covalent structures:**

| Substance                        | Bonding                                              | Properties                                                  |
| -------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------- |
| Diamond                          | Each carbon bonds to 4 others                        | Extremely hard, very high melting point, insulator          |
| Graphite                         | Each carbon bonds to 3 others; delocalised electrons | Soft and slippery, high melting point, conducts electricity |
| Silicon dioxide ($\text{SiO}_2$) | Giant covalent lattice                               | Hard, high melting point, insulator                         |

### 2.3 Metallic Bonding

Metals consist of a **regular lattice of positive ions** surrounded by a **sea of delocalised
electrons**.

**Properties of metals:**

- High melting and boiling points (strong metallic bonding).
- Good conductors of heat and electricity (delocalised electrons carry charge and thermal energy).
- Malleable and ductile (layers of ions can slide over each other without breaking the metallic
  bonds).
- Shiny (delocalised electrons absorb and re-emit light at visible wavelengths).

**Alloys:** Mixtures of two or more metals (or a metal and non-metal). Different-sized atoms distort
the lattice, making it harder for layers to slide — alloys are harder than pure metals.

## 3. Chemical Changes

### 3.1 Reactivity Series

The reactivity series ranks metals from most reactive to least reactive:

$$\text{K} > \text{Na} > \text{Li} > \text{Ca} > \text{Mg} > \text{Al} > \text{(C)} > \text{Zn} > \text{Fe} > \text{(H)} > \text{Cu} > \text{Ag} > \text{Au}$$

- A more reactive metal displaces a less reactive metal from its compound.
- Carbon and hydrogen are included as reference points.

### 3.2 Displacement Reactions

**Metal displacement:** A more reactive metal displaces a less reactive metal from its salt
solution.

$$\text{Zn(s)} + \text{CuSO}_4\text{(aq)} \rightarrow \text{ZnSO}_4\text{(aq)} + \text{Cu(s)}$$

**Halogen displacement:** A more reactive halogen displaces a less reactive halogen from its
solution.

$$\text{Cl}_2\text{(aq)} + 2\text{KBr(aq)} \rightarrow 2\text{KCl(aq)} + \text{Br}_2\text{(aq)}$$

Reactivity of halogens decreases down the group: $\text{F} > \text{Cl} > \text{Br} > \text{I}$.

### 3.3 Extraction of Metals

- **Metals above carbon** in the reactivity series (e.g. aluminium, magnesium) are extracted by
  **electrolysis** of molten compounds. This is expensive and uses large amounts of energy.
- **Metals below carbon** (e.g. iron, copper) are extracted by **reduction** with carbon in a blast
  furnace.
- Gold is found uncombined and requires no extraction.

**Iron extraction in the blast furnace:**

$$\text{Fe}_2\text{O}_3 + 3\text{CO} \rightarrow 2\text{Fe} + 3\text{CO}_2$$

Carbon (coke) reduces iron(III) oxide to iron. Limestone removes impurities as slag.

### 3.4 Acids and Bases

| Acid              | Formula                 |
| ----------------- | ----------------------- |
| Hydrochloric acid | $\text{HCl}$            |
| Sulfuric acid     | $\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$ |
| Nitric acid       | $\text{HNO}_3$          |

**General reactions of acids:**

- Acid + metal $\rightarrow$ salt + hydrogen gas
- Acid + base $\rightarrow$ salt + water
- Acid + carbonate $\rightarrow$ salt + water + carbon dioxide

**Bases:** Substances that neutralise acids (pH > 7). Metal oxides and metal hydroxides are bases.
Soluble bases are called **alkalis**.

**pH scale:** 0–6 acidic, 7 neutral, 8–14 alkaline. Universal indicator changes colour across the
range.

### 3.5 Neutralisation and Salt Preparation

$$\text{acid} + \text{base} \rightarrow \text{salt} + \text{water}$$

**Preparing a soluble salt:**

1. Add the metal oxide/carbonate to dilute acid until the acid is in excess (or until no more
   reacts).
2. Filter to remove the excess solid.
3. Evaporate the filtrate to crystallise the salt.

**Naming salts:**

- Metal + hydrochloric acid $\rightarrow$ metal **chloride**
- Metal + sulfuric acid $\rightarrow$ metal **sulfate**
- Metal + nitric acid $\rightarrow$ metal **nitrate**

### 3.6 Electrolysis

Electrolysis uses electricity to break down ionic compounds in molten or dissolved form.

**Key terms:**

- **Electrode:** The conductor through which current enters or leaves the electrolyte.
- **Electrolyte:** The molten or dissolved ionic compound that conducts electricity.
- **Anode:** Positive electrode (oxidation occurs here).
- **Cathode:** Negative electrode (reduction occurs here).

At the cathode: metal ions gain electrons (reduction). At the anode: non-metal ions lose electrons
(oxidation).

**Example.** Electrolysis of molten lead(II) bromide:

- Cathode: $\text{Pb}^{2+} + 2e^- \rightarrow \text{Pb(l)}$
- Anode: $2\text{Br}^- \rightarrow \text{Br}_2\text{(g)} + 2e^-$

## 4. Quantitative Chemistry

### 4.1 Conservation of Mass

In a closed system, the total mass of reactants equals the total mass of products. This is because
no atoms are created or destroyed in a chemical reaction.

### 4.2 Relative Formula Mass ($M_r$)

$$M_r = \sum (\text{relative atomic masses of atoms in the formula})$$

**Example.** $M_r(\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4) = (2 \times 1) + 32 + (4 \times 16) = 98$

### 4.3 Moles

One mole of any substance contains $6.02 \times 10^{23}$ particles (Avogadro's constant, $N_A$).

$$\text{Number of moles} = \frac{\text{mass (g)}}{M_r}$$

**Example.** 24 g of carbon: $\frac{24}{12} = 2 \text{ mol}$

### 4.4 Reacting Masses

Using the mole ratio from a balanced equation to calculate masses of reactants or products.

**Example.** What mass of magnesium oxide is formed when 12 g of magnesium reacts with oxygen?

$$2\text{Mg} + \text{O}_2 \rightarrow 2\text{MgO}$$

1. Moles of Mg: $\frac{12}{24} = 0.5 \text{ mol}$
2. Ratio Mg:MgO is 2:2 = 1:1, so moles of MgO = 0.5 mol
3. Mass of MgO: $0.5 \times 40 = 20 \text{ g}$

### 4.5 Concentration

$$\text{Concentration (g/dm}^3\text{)} = \frac{\text{mass (g)}}{\text{volume (dm}^3\text{)}}$$

$$\text{Concentration (mol/dm}^3\text{)} = \frac{\text{moles}}{\text{volume (dm}^3\text{)}}$$

**Titration calculations:** Use the balanced equation mole ratio and the concentration and volume of
the known solution to find the concentration or volume of the unknown.

### 4.6 Yield and Atom Economy

**Percentage yield:**

$$\text{Percentage yield} = \frac{\text{actual mass of product}}{\text{theoretical mass of product}} \times 100$$

Yield is always less than 100% due to incomplete reactions, side reactions, product lost during
transfer or purification.

**Atom economy:**

$$\text{Atom economy} = \frac{\text{M}_r \text{ of desired product}}{\text{sum of M}_r \text{ of all products}} \times 100$$

Reactions with high atom economy produce less waste and are more sustainable. Addition reactions
always have 100% atom economy.

## 5. Organic Chemistry

### 5.1 Hydrocarbons

Hydrocarbons contain only carbon and hydrogen atoms. They are the basis of all organic chemistry.

### 5.2 Alkanes

**General formula:** $\text{C}_n\text{H}_{2n+2}$

Alkanes are **saturated** hydrocarbons — all carbon-carbon bonds are single bonds.

| Carbon atoms | Formula                   | Name            | State at room temperature |
| ------------ | ------------------------- | --------------- | ------------------------- |
| 1            | $\text{CH}_4$             | Methane         | Gas                       |
| 2            | $\text{C}_2\text{H}_6$    | Ethane          | Gas                       |
| 3            | $\text{C}_3\text{H}_8$    | Propane         | Gas                       |
| 4            | $\text{C}_4\text{H}_{10}$ | Butane          | Gas                       |
| 5+           | —                         | Pentane onwards | Liquid (then solid)       |

Properties: burn in air to produce carbon dioxide and water; less viscous and more volatile as chain
length decreases.

### 5.3 Alkenes

**General formula:** $\text{C}_n\text{H}_{2n}$

Alkenes are **unsaturated** — they contain at least one carbon-carbon double bond
($\text{C}=\text{C}$).

**Test for unsaturation:** Bromine water turns from orange to colourless when shaken with an alkene
(the double bond reacts with bromine). Alkanes do not decolourise bromine water.

### 5.4 Alcohols

**General formula:** $\text{C}_n\text{H}_{2n+1}\text{OH}$

- Functional group: $-$OH (hydroxyl group).
- Ethanol ($\text{C}_2\text{H}_5\text{OH}$) is the most common alcohol.
- Produced by fermentation of sugars (enzymes in yeast) or hydration of ethene.
- Properties: soluble in water, burns cleanly, used as solvents and fuels.

### 5.5 Carboxylic Acids

**General formula:** $\text{C}_n\text{H}_{2n+1}\text{COOH}$

- Functional group: $-$COOH (carboxyl group).
- Weak acids — partially ionise in solution.
- Ethanoic acid ($\text{CH}_3\text{COOH}$) is found in vinegar.
- React with alcohols to form **esters** (condensation reaction).

### 5.6 Polymers

**Addition polymers:** Made from alkenes (monomers with a $\text{C}=\text{C}$ double bond). The
double bond opens up and links monomers together.

**Example.** Poly(ethene) from ethene:

$$n\text{CH}_2=\text{CH}_2 \rightarrow \left[-\text{CH}_2-\text{CH}_2-\right]_n$$

**Condensation polymers:** Made from two different monomers; each monomer has two functional groups.
Water is released as a by-product.

**Key examples:**

- **Poly(ethene):** Plastic bags, bottles.
- **Poly(propene):** Crates, ropes.
- **Polyesters:** Fibres for clothing.
- **Polyamides (nylon):** Strong fibres, ropes.
- **Biodegradable polymers:** Cornstarch-based (polylactic acid), designed to break down more
  readily in landfill.

**Problems with polymers:** Most are non-biodegradable and persist in the environment. Dispose by
landfill, incineration (produces $\text{CO}_2$), or recycling.

## 6. Chemical Analysis

### 6.1 Pure Substances and Mixtures

- A **pure substance** has a fixed melting and boiling point. A **mixture** melts and boils over a
  range of temperatures.
- Paper chromatography separates substances in a mixture based on their different solubilities in a
  solvent.

### 6.2 Chromatography

The $R_f$ value identifies substances:

$$R_f = \frac{\text{distance travelled by substance}}{\text{distance travelled by solvent}}$$

$R_f$ values are always between 0 and 1. The same substance should give the same $R_f$ value in the
same solvent under the same conditions.

### 6.3 Tests for Gases

| Gas                            | Test                   | Observation          |
| ------------------------------ | ---------------------- | -------------------- |
| Hydrogen ($\text{H}_2$)        | Lighted splint         | "Squeaky pop"        |
| Oxygen ($\text{O}_2$)          | Glowing splint         | Relights the splint  |
| Carbon dioxide ($\text{CO}_2$) | Limewater              | Turns cloudy (milky) |
| Chlorine ($\text{Cl}_2$)       | Damp blue litmus paper | Bleaches it white    |

### 6.4 Tests for Cations and Anions

**Flame tests (cations):**

| Ion              | Flame colour                           |
| ---------------- | -------------------------------------- |
| $\text{Li}^+$    | Crimson red                            |
| $\text{Na}^+$    | Yellow                                 |
| $\text{K}^+$     | Lilac (view through cobalt blue glass) |
| $\text{Ca}^{2+}$ | Orange-red                             |
| $\text{Cu}^{2+}$ | Blue-green                             |

**Tests for specific ions:**

- $\text{Cu}^{2+}$: Add sodium hydroxide — blue precipitate.
- $\text{Fe}^{2+}$: Add sodium hydroxide — green precipitate.
- $\text{Fe}^{3+}$: Add sodium hydroxide — brown precipitate.
- $\text{OH}^-$: Universal indicator turns blue/purple.
- $\text{Cl}^-$: Add dilute $\text{HNO}_3$ then $\text{AgNO}_3$ — white precipitate (soluble in
  dilute ammonia).
- $\text{Br}^-$: Same test — cream precipitate (partially soluble in dilute ammonia).
- $\text{I}^-$: Same test — yellow precipitate (insoluble in dilute ammonia).
- $\text{SO}_4^{2-}$: Add dilute $\text{HCl}$ then $\text{BaCl}_2$ — white precipitate insoluble in
  acids.
- $\text{CO}_3^{2-}$: Add dilute acid — effervescence; gas turns limewater cloudy.

### 6.5 Instrumental Methods

Modern instrumental techniques offer higher accuracy, sensitivity, and speed compared with
traditional chemical tests.

- **Gas chromatography-mass spectrometry (GC-MS):** Separates components of a mixture by gas
  chromatography, then identifies each component by mass spectrometry. The retention time and mass
  spectrum act as a "fingerprint" for identification.

## 7. Rate of Reaction

### 7.1 Factors Affecting Rate

| Factor               | Effect         | Reason                                                                                     |
| -------------------- | -------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Temperature**      | Increases rate | Particles have more kinetic energy; more frequent and more energetic successful collisions |
| **Concentration**    | Increases rate | More particles per unit volume; more frequent collisions                                   |
| **Pressure** (gases) | Increases rate | Same as increasing concentration                                                           |
| **Surface area**     | Increases rate | More particles exposed; more frequent collisions                                           |
| **Catalyst**         | Increases rate | Provides an alternative pathway with lower activation energy; not used up                  |

### 7.2 Collision Theory

For a reaction to occur, particles must **collide** with each other. A reaction only happens if
collisions have:

1. **Sufficient energy** (at least the activation energy $E_a$).
2. The **correct orientation**.

Increasing the frequency of successful collisions increases the rate of reaction.

**Activation energy ($E_a$):** The minimum energy that colliding particles must have for a reaction
to occur. A catalyst lowers the activation energy without being consumed.

### 7.3 Calculating Rate

$$\text{Rate of reaction} = \frac{\text{amount of reactant used (or product formed)}}{\text{time taken}}$$

This can be measured as mass of gas produced, volume of gas, or change in concentration over time.

**Measuring rate:**

- Volume of gas in a gas syringe at regular time intervals.
- Mass lost (on a balance) at regular intervals.
- Colour change using a colorimeter (for reactions involving a colour change).

### 7.4 Reversible Reactions

Some reactions are reversible — the products can react to reform the original reactants.

$$\text{A} + \text{B} \rightleftharpoons \text{C} + \text{D}$$

The symbol $\rightleftharpoons$ indicates a reversible reaction.

At equilibrium, the rate of the forward reaction equals the rate of the backward reaction. The
concentrations of reactants and products remain constant (but not necessarily equal).

### 7.5 Le Chatelier's Principle

If a system at equilibrium is subjected to a change, the equilibrium shifts to **oppose** that
change.

| Change                             | Effect on equilibrium                                                     |
| ---------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Increase concentration of reactant | Equilibrium shifts to the right (towards products)                        |
| Increase temperature               | Shifts in the endothermic direction (absorbs heat)                        |
| Increase pressure                  | Shifts towards the side with fewer gas molecules                          |
| Add a catalyst                     | No effect on equilibrium position; only speeds up both directions equally |

**Example.** The Haber process:
$\text{N}_2 + 3\text{H}_2 \rightleftharpoons 2\text{NH}_3 \quad (\Delta H = -92 \text{ kJ/mol})$

- High pressure favours the forward reaction (fewer gas molecules on the right).
- Low temperature favours the forward reaction (exothermic).
- In practice: moderate temperature (balance of rate and yield), high pressure, iron catalyst.

## 8. Key Definitions

| Term                  | Definition                                                                                   |
| --------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Atom**              | The smallest part of an element that can take part in chemical reactions                     |
| **Ion**               | An atom or group of atoms with a net electric charge (formed by gaining or losing electrons) |
| **Isotope**           | Atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons                                 |
| **Molecule**          | Two or more atoms bonded together                                                            |
| **Compound**          | Two or more different elements chemically bonded together                                    |
| **Mixture**           | Two or more substances physically combined, not chemically bonded                            |
| **Mole**              | The amount of substance containing $6.02 \times 10^{23}$ particles                           |
| **Empirical formula** | The simplest whole-number ratio of atoms in a compound                                       |
| **Molecular formula** | The actual number of atoms of each element in a molecule                                     |
| **Yield**             | The amount of product obtained from a reaction, compared to the theoretical maximum          |
| **Atom economy**      | A measure of how efficiently the reactant atoms end up in the desired product                |
| **Catalyst**          | A substance that increases the rate of reaction without being used up                        |
| **Activation energy** | The minimum energy required for a reaction to occur                                          |
| **Equilibrium**       | The state where the rate of the forward reaction equals the rate of the backward reaction    |
| **Hydrocarbon**       | A compound containing only hydrogen and carbon                                               |
| **Saturated**         | Containing only single carbon-carbon bonds                                                   |
| **Unsaturated**       | Containing at least one carbon-carbon double bond                                            |
| **Polymer**           | A large molecule made from many small repeating units (monomers)                             |
| **Electrolysis**      | The decomposition of an ionic compound using electricity                                     |
| **Electrolyte**       | A molten or dissolved ionic compound that conducts electricity                               |
| **Exothermic**        | A reaction that transfers energy to the surroundings (temperature increases)                 |
| **Endothermic**       | A reaction that takes energy from the surroundings (temperature decreases)                   |

## 9. Exam Tips

1. **Learn the definitions word for word.** Many marks are available for precise recall. Use the
   glossary in Section 8 as flashcards and test yourself regularly until every definition is exact.

2. **Balance all symbol equations.** An unbalanced equation will not score full marks. Check that
   the number of atoms of each element is the same on both sides. Practise balancing equations with
   awkward coefficients (e.g. fractions then multiply through).

3. **Show all working for calculation questions.** Write out the formula, substitute values, include
   units, and give the answer to the correct number of significant figures. Even if the final answer
   is wrong, method marks are available for correct working.

4. **Learn the reactivity series and periodic table trends.** Know the order of metals in the
   reactivity series, the trends in Group 1 and Group 7, and the properties of transition metals.
   These are frequently tested and difficult to deduce from first principles in the exam.

5. **Use state symbols in equations.** (s) solid, (l) liquid, (g) gas, (aq) aqueous (dissolved in
   water). Marks are often specifically allocated for correct state symbols, especially in
   electrolysis questions.

6. **Distinguish between percentage yield and atom economy.** Percentage yield is about how much
   product you actually get from what you could get (efficiency of the process). Atom economy is
   about how much of the total mass of products is the desired product (waste produced). They
   measure different things.

7. **For six-mark questions, use a structured approach.** Identify the key points (in most cases 3–4
   distinct scientific ideas). Write a clear sentence for each, using scientific vocabulary. If the
   question asks you to "compare", explicitly state similarities AND differences. If it says
   "evaluate", give a conclusion with justification.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Using 2.8.8 instead of 2.8.1 for potassium.** Potassium has atomic number 19. The electron
   configuration is 2.8.8.1 (not 2.8.8, which is argon with 18 electrons). The extra electron goes
   into the fourth shell because the third shell can only hold 8 electrons at GCSE level.

2. **Confusing empirical and molecular formula.** The empirical formula is the simplest ratio. For
   glucose ($\text{C}_6\text{H}_{12}\text{O}_6$), the molecular formula is
   $\text{C}_6\text{H}_{12}\text{O}_6$ and the empirical formula is $\text{CH}_2\text{O}$. They are
   only the same when the simplest ratio equals the actual ratio.

3. **Not balancing equations before doing mole calculations.** Always check the stoichiometry. If
   the equation says 2 moles of product, then 1 mole of reactant gives 2 moles of product -- the
   ratio matters for every calculation.

4. **Mixing up flame test colours.** Sodium is yellow, potassium is lilac (must be viewed through
   cobalt blue glass to distinguish from sodium contamination), copper is blue-green. Learn them as
   a mnemonic or visual association.

5. **Forgetting that catalysts are not used up.** A catalyst speeds up a reaction by providing an
   alternative pathway with lower activation energy, but it is not consumed in the reaction. It
   appears on neither side of the chemical equation. If you write a catalyst as a product or
   reactant, it is wrong.

6. **Leaving the empirical formula as a decimal ratio.** If you get 1 : 1.5, multiply both by 2 to
   get 2 : 3. Empirical formulas must always use whole numbers. If you get 1 : 1.33, multiply by 3
   to get 3 : 4.

7. **Stating "the equilibrium shifts to the left" without explaining why.** Le Chatelier's principle
   questions require you to state both the direction of the shift AND the reasoning (which change is
   being opposed). For example: "The equilibrium shifts to the right because increasing temperature
   opposes the endothermic direction, which is the backward reaction, so the forward (exothermic)
   reaction is favoured to absorb the excess heat."

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Mole Calculation

**Problem:** What mass of carbon dioxide is produced when 12 g of carbon is burned completely in
oxygen? **Solution:** Equation: C + O2 -> CO2. Mr(C) = 12, so moles of C = 12/12 = 1 mol. Ratio
C:CO2 is 1:1, so moles of CO2 = 1 mol. Mr(CO2) = 44, so mass = 1 x 44 = 44 g.

### Example 2: Titration Calculation

**Problem:** 25.0 cm^3 of sodium hydroxide solution reacts with 20.0 cm^3 of 0.100 mol/dm^3
hydrochloric acid. Calculate the concentration of NaOH. **Solution:** HCl + NaOH -> NaCl + H2O.
Moles of HCl = 0.100 x (20.0/1000) = 0.002 mol. Ratio 1:1, so moles of NaOH = 0.002 mol.
Concentration = 0.002 / (25.0/1000) = 0.080 mol/dm^3.

### Example 3: Yield Calculation

**Problem:** 10 g of CaCO3 is heated to produce CaO and CO2. 5.0 g of CaO is obtained. Calculate the
percentage yield. (Mr CaCO3 = 100, Mr CaO = 56) **Solution:** CaCO3 -> CaO + CO2. Moles CaCO3 =
10/100 = 0.1 mol. Theoretical moles CaO = 0.1 mol. Theoretical mass = 0.1 x 56 = 5.6 g. Percentage
yield = (5.0/5.6) x 100 = 89.3%.

## Summary

GCSE Chemistry covers atomic structure (subatomic particles, isotopes, electron configuration),
bonding (ionic, covalent, metallic, intermolecular forces), chemical changes (reactivity series,
displacement, extraction, acids and bases, electrolysis), quantitative chemistry (moles, reacting
masses, yield, atom economy), organic chemistry (alkanes, alkenes, alcohols, carboxylic acids,
polymers), chemical analysis (chromatography, flame tests, instrumental methods), and rate of
reaction (collision theory, factors affecting rate, Le Chatelier's principle). Key exam skills
include balancing equations with state symbols, showing all working in calculations, and applying Le
Chatelier's principle with full reasoning.
