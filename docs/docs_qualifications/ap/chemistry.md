## Atomic Structure and Periodicity (CED Unit 1)

### Key Concepts

Atoms consist of protons and neutrons in the nucleus and electrons in orbitals described by quantum numbers $(n, \ell, m_\ell, m_s)$. The electron configuration follows the Aufbau principle, with exceptions for half-filled and fully filled d subshells (Cr: $[\mathrm{'\{'}Ar{'\}'}]\,4s^1 3d^5$, Cu: $[\mathrm{'\{'}Ar{'\}'}]\,4s^1 3d^{10}$).

Effective nuclear charge $Z_{\mathrm{'\{'}eff{'\}'}} = Z - S$ underpins all periodic trends:

| Trend             | Across a Period       | Down a Group          |
| ----------------- | --------------------- | --------------------- |
| Atomic radius     | Decreases             | Increases             |
| Ionisation energy | Increases (dips at 13, 16) | Decreases             |
| Electronegativity | Increases             | Decreases             |

The photoelectric effect demonstrates the particle nature of light:

$$KE_{\mathrm{'\{'}max{'\}'}} = h\nu - \phi$$

### Worked Example

The first five ionisation energies of an element are 578, 1817, 2745, 11578, and 14842 kJ/mol. Identify the group.

The large jump between the third (2745) and fourth (11578) ionisation energies indicates the fourth electron is removed from an inner shell. The element has three valence electrons: Group 13 (aluminium).

## Bonding and Molecular Geometry (CED Units 2-3)

### Bond Types and Lewis Structures

Bond type is determined by electronegativity difference: ionic ($\Delta\chi > 1.7$), polar covalent ($0.4$--$1.7$), nonpolar covalent ($< 0.4$).

Formal charge: $\mathrm{'\{'}FC{'\}'} = V - N - B/2$. The best Lewis structure minimises formal charges and places negative charge on more electronegative atoms.

### VSEPR Theory

Electron pairs arrange to minimise repulsion. Lone pair--lone pair > lone pair--bond pair > bond pair--bond pair.

| Domains | Bonding | Lone | Shape             | Bond Angle        |
| ------- | ------- | ---- | ----------------- | ----------------- |
| 2       | 2       | 0    | Linear            | $180^\circ$       |
| 3       | 3       | 0    | Trigonal planar   | $120^\circ$       |
| 3       | 2       | 1    | Bent              | $< 120^\circ$     |
| 4       | 4       | 0    | Tetrahedral       | $109.5^\circ$     |
| 4       | 3       | 1    | Trigonal pyramidal| $< 109.5^\circ$   |
| 4       | 2       | 2    | Bent              | $< 109.5^\circ$   |
| 5       | 4       | 1    | Seesaw            | varies            |
| 6       | 4       | 2    | Square planar     | $90^\circ$        |

### Hybridisation and MO Theory

Hybridisation matches electron domains: 2 = sp, 3 = sp$^2$, 4 = sp$^3$, 5 = sp$^3$d, 6 = sp$^3$d$^2$. Sigma bonds form from head-on overlap; pi bonds form from side-to-side overlap of unhybridised p orbitals.

Molecular orbital (MO) theory treats electrons as delocalised over the entire molecule. For homonuclear diatomics, MO diagrams predict bond order:

$$\text{'\{'}Bond order{'\}'} = \frac{(\text{'\{'}bonding {'\}'} e^-) - (\text{'\{'}antibonding {'\}'} e^-)}{2}$$

$\mathrm{'\{'}O{'\}'}_2$ has bond order 2 and two unpaired electrons in antibonding $\pi^*$ orbitals, explaining its paramagnetism -- a fact VSEPR and Lewis structures cannot explain.

### Intermolecular Forces (weakest to strongest)

London dispersion forces < dipole-dipole < hydrogen bonding < ion-dipole. Hydrogen bonding (H bonded to N, O, or F) produces anomalously high boiling points.

## Stoichiometry and Gases (CED Units 1, 3-4)

### The Mole and Stoichiometry

$$\text{'\{'}moles{'\}'} = \frac{\text{'\{'}mass{'\}'}}{\text{'\{'}molar mass{'\}'}}, \qquad \text{'\{'}particles{'\}'} = \text{'\{'}moles{'\}'} \times N_A$$

For limiting reactant problems, divide each reactant's moles by its stoichiometric coefficient; the smallest result identifies the limiting reactant.

### Ideal Gas Law

$$PV = nRT$$

where $R = 0.08206\;\mathrm{'\{'}L\cdot atm/(mol\cdot K){'\}'}$ or $R = 8.314\;\mathrm{'\{'}J/(mol\cdot K){'\}'}$.

At STP ($273.15\;\mathrm{'\{'}K{'\}'}$, $1\;\mathrm{'\{'}atm{'\}'}$), one mole of ideal gas occupies $22.4\;\mathrm{'\{'}L{'\}'}$.

Dalton's law: $P_{\mathrm{'\{'}total{'\}'}} = P_1 + P_2 + \cdots$, and $P_i = \chi_i P_{\mathrm{'\{'}total{'\}'}}$.

### Kinetic Molecular Theory (KMT)

1. Gas particles are in constant, random, straight-line motion.
2. Particle volume is negligible compared to container volume.
3. No intermolecular forces (except during collisions).
4. Collisions are elastic (kinetic energy is conserved).
5. Average kinetic energy is proportional to temperature: $\overline{KE} = \frac{3}{2}RT$ (per mole).

KMT explains Boyle's law (higher $P$ = more collisions per area), Charles's law (higher $T$ = faster particles = larger $V$), and Graham's law of effusion:

$$\frac{r_1}{r_2} = \sqrt{\frac{M_2}{M_1}}$$

### Worked Example

$0.200\;\mathrm{'\{'}g{'\}'}$ of $\mathrm{'\{'}KClO{'\}'}_3$ decomposes: $2\mathrm{'\{'}KClO{'\}'}_3(s) \to 2\mathrm{'\{'}KCl{'\}'}(s) + 3\mathrm{'\{'}O{'\}'}_2(g)$. Oxygen is collected over water at $25^\circ\mathrm{'\{'}C{'\}'}$ and $0.980\;\mathrm{'\{'}atm{'\}'}$. Vapour pressure of water = $23.8\;\mathrm{'\{'}mmHg{'\}'}$. Find the volume of dry $\mathrm{'\{'}O{'\}'}_2$.

$$n_{\mathrm{'\{'}O{'\}'}_2} = \frac{0.200}{122.55} \times \frac{3}{2} = 0.00245\;\mathrm{'\{'}mol{'\}'}$$

$$P_{\mathrm{'\{'}O{'\}'}_2} = 0.980 - \frac{23.8}{760} = 0.949\;\mathrm{'\{'}atm{'\}'}$$

$$V = \frac{nRT}{P} = \frac{(0.00245)(0.08206)(298)}{0.949} = 0.0630\;\mathrm{'\{'}L{'\}'} = 63.0\;\mathrm{'\{'}mL{'\}'}$$

## Thermodynamics (CED Units 6, 9)

### First Law and Enthalpy

$$\Delta U = q + w, \qquad w = -P\Delta V, \qquad \Delta H = \Delta U + P\Delta V$$

At constant pressure, $\Delta H = q_P$. Standard enthalpy of reaction:

$$\Delta H_{\mathrm{'\{'}rxn{'\}'}}^\circ = \sum n\Delta H_f^\circ(\text{'\{'}products{'\}'}) - \sum m\Delta H_f^\circ(\text{'\{'}reactants{'\}'})$$

By convention, $\Delta H_f^\circ = 0$ for elements in their standard state.

### Calorimetry

Coffee-cup (constant $P$): $q_{\mathrm{'\{'}rxn{'\}'}} = -mc\Delta T$

Bomb (constant $V$): $q_{\mathrm{'\{'}rxn{'\}'}} = -C_{\mathrm{'\{'}cal{'\}'}}\Delta T$; convert to $\Delta H$: $\Delta H = \Delta U + \Delta n_g RT$

### Entropy and Gibbs Free Energy

$$\Delta S_{\mathrm{'\{'}universe{'\}'}} = \Delta S_{\mathrm{'\{'}system{'\}'}} + \Delta S_{\mathrm{'\{'}surroundings{'\}'}} > 0 \quad (\text{'\{'}spontaneous{'\}'})$$

$$\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S, \qquad \Delta G^\circ = -RT\ln K$$

| $\Delta H$ | $\Delta S$ | Spontaneous at     |
| ---------- | ---------- | ------------------ |
| $-$        | $+$        | All $T$            |
| $+$        | $-$        | Never              |
| $-$        | $-$        | Low $T$ only       |
| $+$        | $+$        | High $T$ only      |

### Worked Example

For $\mathrm{'\{'}N{'\}'}_2(g) + 3\mathrm{'\{'}H{'\}'}_2(g) \to 2\mathrm{'\{'}NH{'\}'}_3(g)$: $\Delta H^\circ = -92.2\;\mathrm{'\{'}kJ/mol{'\}'}$, $\Delta S^\circ = -198.8\;\mathrm{'\{'}J/(mol\cdot K){'\}'}$.

$$\Delta G^\circ = -92200 - 298(-198.8) = -92200 + 59242 = -33.0\;\mathrm{'\{'}kJ/mol{'\}'}$$

$$K = e^{33000/(8.314 \times 298)} = e^{13.29} = 5.9 \times 10^5$$

## Equilibrium (CED Unit 7)

### The Equilibrium Constant

For $a\mathrm{'\{'}A{'\}'} + b\mathrm{'\{'}B{'\}'} \rightleftharpoons c\mathrm{'\{'}C{'\}'} + d\mathrm{'\{'}D{'\}'}$:

$$K_c = \frac{[\mathrm{'\{'}C{'\}'}]^c[\mathrm{'\{'}D{'\}'}]^d}{[\mathrm{'\{'}A{'\}'}]^a[\mathrm{'\{'}B{'\}'}]^b}, \qquad K_p = K_c(RT)^{\Delta n}$$

where $\Delta n = (\text{'\{'}moles gaseous products{'\}'}) - (\text{'\{'}moles gaseous reactants{'\}'})$. Solids and pure liquids are omitted.

The reaction quotient $Q$ uses current concentrations. If $Q < K$, the reaction proceeds forward; if $Q > K$, it proceeds in reverse.

### Le Chatelier's Principle

Only temperature changes alter $K$. Concentration and pressure changes shift the equilibrium position but leave $K$ unchanged. Adding an inert gas at constant volume has no effect. A catalyst increases the rate of both forward and reverse reactions equally.

### ICE Tables

ICE (Initial, Change, Equilibrium) tables calculate equilibrium concentrations systematically.

### Worked Example

For $\mathrm{'\{'}H{'\}'}_2(g) + \mathrm{'\{'}I{'\}'}_2(g) \rightleftharpoons 2\mathrm{'\{'}HI{'\}'}(g)$, $K_c = 50.5$. Starting with $1.00\;\mathrm{'\{'}M{'\}'}$ each of $\mathrm{'\{'}H{'\}'}_2$ and $\mathrm{'\{'}I{'\}'}_2$:

| Species        | Initial | Change | Equilibrium    |
| -------------- | ------- | ------ | -------------- |
| $\mathrm{'\{'}H{'\}'}_2$ | 1.00    | $-x$   | $1.00 - x$     |
| $\mathrm{'\{'}I{'\}'}_2$ | 1.00    | $-x$   | $1.00 - x$     |
| $\mathrm{'\{'}HI{'\}'}$  | 0       | $+2x$  | $2x$           |

$$K_c = \frac{(2x)^2}{(1.00 - x)^2} = 50.5 \implies \frac{2x}{1.00 - x} = 7.11 \implies x = 0.781$$

$$[\mathrm{'\{'}HI{'\}'}] = 2(0.781) = 1.56\;\mathrm{'\{'}M{'\}'}$$

## Kinetics (CED Unit 5)

### Rate Laws

$$\mathrm{'\{'}Rate{'\}'} = k[\mathrm{'\{'}A{'\}'}]^m[\mathrm{'\{'}B{'\}'}]^n$$

Orders $m$ and $n$ are determined experimentally, not from stoichiometric coefficients.

### Integrated Rate Laws

| Order  | Integrated Law                              | Half-life          | Linear Plot          |
| ------ | ------------------------------------------- | ------------------ | -------------------- |
| 0      | $[\mathrm{'\{'}A{'\}'}] = -kt + [\mathrm{'\{'}A{'\}'}]_0$       | $[\mathrm{'\{'}A{'\}'}]_0/2k$| $[\mathrm{'\{'}A{'\}'}]$ vs $t$ |
| 1      | $\ln[\mathrm{'\{'}A{'\}'}] = -kt + \ln[\mathrm{'\{'}A{'\}'}]_0$ | $0.693/k$          | $\ln[\mathrm{'\{'}A{'\}'}]$ vs $t$ |
| 2      | $1/[\mathrm{'\{'}A{'\}'}] = kt + 1/[\mathrm{'\{'}A{'\}'}]_0$    | $1/(k[\mathrm{'\{'}A{'\}'}]_0)$| $1/[\mathrm{'\{'}A{'\}'}]$ vs $t$ |

### Arrhenius Equation

$$k = Ae^{-E_a/(RT)}, \qquad \ln\frac{k_2}{k_1} = \frac{E_a}{R}\!\left(\frac{1}{T_1} - \frac{1}{T_2}\right)$$

A catalyst lowers $E_a$ without being consumed, increasing $k$ for both forward and reverse reactions.

### Reaction Mechanisms

Elementary step molecularity determines the rate law for that step. The rate-determining (slow) step governs the overall rate law. Intermediates from fast equilibrium steps are substituted using the equilibrium constant.

### Worked Example

$k = 3.0 \times 10^{-3}\;\mathrm{'\{'}s{'\}'}^{-1}$ at $300\;\mathrm{'\{'}K{'\}'}$, $k = 2.4 \times 10^{-2}\;\mathrm{'\{'}s{'\}'}^{-1}$ at $350\;\mathrm{'\{'}K{'\}'}$.

$$\ln\frac{2.4 \times 10^{-2}}{3.0 \times 10^{-3}} = \frac{E_a}{8.314}\!\left(\frac{1}{300} - \frac{1}{350}\right)$$

$$2.079 = \frac{E_a}{8.314}(4.762 \times 10^{-4}) \implies E_a = 36.3\;\mathrm{'\{'}kJ/mol{'\}'}$$

## Acids and Bases (CED Unit 8)

### pH, pOH, and $K_w$

$$\mathrm{'\{'}pH{'\}'} = -\log[\mathrm{'\{'}H{'\}'}^+], \quad \mathrm{'\{'}pOH{'\}'} = -\log[\mathrm{'\{'}OH{'\}'}^-], \quad \mathrm{'\{'}pH{'\}'} + \mathrm{'\{'}pOH{'\}'} = 14$$

$$K_w = [\mathrm{'\{'}H{'\}'}^+][\mathrm{'\{'}OH{'\}'}^-] = 1.0 \times 10^{-14} \text{'\{'} at {'\}'} 25^\circ\mathrm{'\{'}C{'\}'}$$

### Strong and Weak Acids/Bases

Strong acids ($\mathrm{'\{'}HCl{'\}'}$, $\mathrm{'\{'}HBr{'\}'}$, $\mathrm{'\{'}HI{'\}'}$, $\mathrm{'\{'}HNO{'\}'}_3$, $\mathrm{'\{'}HClO{'\}'}_4$, $\mathrm{'\{'}H{'\}'}_2\mathrm{'\{'}SO{'\}'}_4$) dissociate completely. Weak acids have $K_a$:

$$K_a = \frac{[\mathrm{'\{'}H{'\}'}^+][\mathrm{'\{'}A{'\}'}^-]}{[\mathrm{'\{'}HA{'\}'}]}, \qquad K_a \cdot K_b = K_w$$

### pH of Weak Acids

For a weak acid HA at initial concentration $C_0$:

$$[\mathrm{'\{'}H{'\}'}^+] = \sqrt{K_a \cdot C_0} \quad (\text{'\{'}valid when {'\}'} C_0 / K_a > 100)$$

### Buffers

A buffer resists pH change and consists of a weak acid/conjugate base pair. The Henderson-Hasselbalch equation:

$$\mathrm{'\{'}pH{'\}'} = \mathrm{'\{'}p{'\}'}K_a + \log\frac{[\mathrm{'\{'}A{'\}'}^-]}{[\mathrm{'\{'}HA{'\}'}]}$$

Buffer capacity is maximised when $[\mathrm{'\{'}A{'\}'}^-] = [\mathrm{'\{'}HA{'\}'}]$ (i.e., $\mathrm{'\{'}pH{'\}'} = \mathrm{'\{'}p{'\}'}K_a$).

### Titrations

Strong acid--strong base: $\mathrm{'\{'}pH{'\}'} = 7$ at equivalence point.

Weak acid--strong base: $\mathrm{'\{'}pH{'\}'} > 7$ at equivalence point (conjugate base hydrolysis). The half-equivalence point gives $\mathrm{'\{'}pH{'\}'} = \mathrm{'\{'}p{'\}'}K_a$.

### Solubility Product ($K_{sp}$)

$$\mathrm{'\{'}M{'\}'}_a\mathrm{'\{'}X{'\}'}_b(s) \rightleftharpoons a\mathrm{'\{'}M{'\}'}^{b+}(aq) + b\mathrm{'\{'}X{'\}'}^{a-}(aq), \qquad K_{sp} = [\mathrm{'\{'}M{'\}'}^{b+}]^a[\mathrm{'\{'}X{'\}'}^{a-}]^b$$

Compare $Q_{sp}$ with $K_{sp}$ to predict precipitation. The common ion effect reduces solubility.

### Worked Example: Buffer pH

Calculate the pH of a buffer containing $0.20\;\mathrm{'\{'}M{'\}'}$ $\mathrm{'\{'}CH{'\}'}_3\mathrm{'\{'}COOH{'\}'}$ ($K_a = 1.8 \times 10^{-5}$) and $0.30\;\mathrm{'\{'}M{'\}'}$ $\mathrm{'\{'}CH{'\}'}_3\mathrm{'\{'}COO{'\}'}^-$.

$$\mathrm{'\{'}p{'\}'}K_a = -\log(1.8 \times 10^{-5}) = 4.74$$

$$\mathrm{'\{'}pH{'\}'} = 4.74 + \log\frac{0.30}{0.20} = 4.74 + 0.18 = 4.92$$

### Worked Example: $K_{sp}$ and Common Ion

$K_{sp}(\mathrm{'\{'}AgCl{'\}'}) = 1.8 \times 10^{-10}$. Solubility in $0.10\;\mathrm{'\{'}M{'\}'}$ $\mathrm{'\{'}NaCl{'\}'}$:

$$K_{sp} = s \times (0.10 + s) \approx 0.10s \implies s = 1.8 \times 10^{-9}\;\mathrm{'\{'}M{'\}'}$$

Compare with pure water: $s_0 = \sqrt{1.8 \times 10^{-10}} = 1.3 \times 10^{-5}\;\mathrm{'\{'}M{'\}'}$. The common ion reduces solubility by a factor of $\sim 7,200$.

## Electrochemistry (CED Unit 9)

### Galvanic (Voltaic) Cells

In a galvanic cell, a spontaneous redox reaction produces electrical energy. Oxidation occurs at the anode ($-$); reduction occurs at the cathode ($+$).

Cell potential: $E_{\mathrm{'\{'}cell{'\}'}}^\circ = E_{\mathrm{'\{'}cathode{'\}'}}^\circ - E_{\mathrm{'\{'}anode{'\}'}}^\circ$

A positive $E_{\mathrm{'\{'}cell{'\}'}}^\circ$ indicates a spontaneous reaction ($\Delta G^\circ < 0$).

### Standard Reduction Potentials

Measured relative to the standard hydrogen electrode (SHE), defined as $E^\circ = 0.00\;\mathrm{'\{'}V{'\}'}$. More positive $E^\circ$ = stronger oxidising agent (greater tendency to be reduced).

### Nernst Equation

For non-standard conditions:

$$E = E^\circ - \frac{RT}{nF}\ln Q = E^\circ - \frac{0.0592}{n}\log Q \quad (\text{'\{'}at {'\}'} 298\;\mathrm{'\{'}K{'\}'})$$

where $n$ is moles of electrons transferred, $F = 96485\;\mathrm{'\{'}C/mol{'\}'}$, and $Q$ is the reaction quotient using activities (approximated by concentrations/partial pressures).

### Relationship to Thermodynamics

$$\Delta G^\circ = -nFE^\circ, \qquad \Delta G = -nFE$$

### Worked Example

Calculate $E_{\mathrm{'\{'}cell{'\}'}}^\circ$ for $\mathrm{'\{'}Zn{'\}'}(s) + \mathrm{'\{'}Cu{'\}'}^{2+}(aq) \to \mathrm{'\{'}Zn{'\}'}^{2+}(aq) + \mathrm{'\{'}Cu{'\}'}(s)$.

$$E_{\mathrm{'\{'}cathode{'\}'}}^\circ = E^\circ(\mathrm{'\{'}Cu{'\}'}^{2+}/\mathrm{'\{'}Cu{'\}'}) = +0.34\;\mathrm{'\{'}V{'\}'}$$

$$E_{\mathrm{'\{'}anode{'\}'}}^\circ = E^\circ(\mathrm{'\{'}Zn{'\}'}^{2+}/\mathrm{'\{'}Zn{'\}'}) = -0.76\;\mathrm{'\{'}V{'\}'}$$

$$E_{\mathrm{'\{'}cell{'\}'}}^\circ = 0.34 - (-0.76) = 1.10\;\mathrm{'\{'}V{'\}'}$$

### Worked Example: Nernst Equation

For the cell above, find $E$ when $[\mathrm{'\{'}Zn{'\}'}^{2+}] = 0.10\;\mathrm{'\{'}M{'\}'}$ and $[\mathrm{'\{'}Cu{'\}'}^{2+}] = 1.0\;\mathrm{'\{'}M{'\}'}$ at $298\;\mathrm{'\{'}K{'\}'}$ ($n = 2$):

$$Q = \frac{[\mathrm{'\{'}Zn{'\}'}^{2+}]}{[\mathrm{'\{'}Cu{'\}'}^{2+}]} = \frac{0.10}{1.0} = 0.10$$

$$E = 1.10 - \frac{0.0592}{2}\log(0.10) = 1.10 - (-0.0296) = 1.13\;\mathrm{'\{'}V{'\}'}$$

The lower product concentration ($\mathrm{'\{'}Zn{'\}'}^{2+}$) drives the reaction further forward, increasing the cell potential.

### Electrolysis

In an electrolytic cell, electrical energy drives a nonspontaneous reaction. The cathode is still the site of reduction, but it is the negative electrode (connected to the $-$ terminal of the power supply).

Faraday's law: $1\;\mathrm{'\{'}mol{'\}'}$ of electrons ($96485\;\mathrm{'\{'}C{'\}'}$) reduces $1\;\mathrm{'\{'}mol{'\}'}$ of a $+1$ cation, $0.5\;\mathrm{'\{'}mol{'\}'}$ of a $+2$ cation, etc.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Writing $4s$ before $3d$ for transition metal cations.** Remove $4s$ electrons first when forming cations, even though $4s$ fills first.
2. **Confusing electron domain geometry with molecular geometry.** Molecular geometry excludes lone pairs.
3. **Forgetting to convert temperature to Kelvin** in gas law calculations.
4. **Using the wrong $R$ value.** Match $R$ to the units: $0.08206$ for atm/L, $8.314$ for J/K (thermodynamics).
5. **Unit mismatch in $\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S$.** $\Delta H$ is in kJ/mol; $\Delta S$ is in J/(mol$\cdot$K). Convert one.
6. **Assuming catalysts change $K$.** Catalysts speed both forward and reverse rates equally; $K$ is unchanged.
7. **Including solids and liquids in $K$ expressions.** Only gases and aqueous species appear.
8. **Rate law orders are not stoichiometric coefficients.** Orders must be determined experimentally.
9. **Confusing $\Delta G^\circ$ with $\Delta G$.** $\Delta G^\circ = -RT\ln K$ (standard conditions); $\Delta G = \Delta G^\circ + RT\ln Q$ (any conditions).
10. **Misapplying the Henderson-Hasselbalch equation.** It requires a buffer system (weak acid/conjugate base pair), not just any mixture.
11. **Forgetting to account for dilution** when mixing solutions before calculating concentrations in $K_{sp}$ or $Q$ problems.
12. **Sign errors in electrochemistry.** $E_{\mathrm{'\{'}cell{'\}'}}^\circ = E_{\mathrm{'\{'}cathode{'\}'}}^\circ - E_{\mathrm{'\{'}anode{'\}'}}^\circ$. Subtract the anode potential; do not simply add the more positive value.
13. **Reversing anode and cathode in electrolytic cells.** Oxidation still occurs at the anode, but the anode is connected to the positive terminal.
14. **Using $n$ incorrectly in the Nernst equation.** $n$ is the moles of electrons transferred in the balanced redox equation, not per mole of reactant.

<summary>Question 1: Thermodynamics and equilibrium (FRQ)</summary>

For the reaction $\mathrm{'\{'}N{'\}'}_2\mathrm{'\{'}O{'\}'}_4(g) \rightleftharpoons 2\mathrm{'\{'}NO{'\}'}_2(g)$:

(a) At $298\;\mathrm{'\{'}K{'\}'}$, $\Delta H^\circ = 57.2\;\mathrm{'\{'}kJ/mol{'\}'}$ and $\Delta S^\circ = 175.8\;\mathrm{'\{'}J/(mol\cdot K){'\}'}$. Calculate $\Delta G^\circ$ and $K_p$ at $298\;\mathrm{'\{'}K{'\}'}$.

(b) Is the reaction product-favoured or reactant-favoured at $298\;\mathrm{'\{'}K{'\}'}$? Justify.

(c) Calculate the temperature at which the reaction is at equilibrium ($\Delta G = 0$).

(d) If the volume of the container is decreased, predict the effect on the equilibrium concentration of $\mathrm{'\{'}NO{'\}'}_2$ and the value of $K_p$.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

(a)
$$\Delta G^\circ = \Delta H^\circ - T\Delta S^\circ = 57200 - 298(175.8) = 57200 - 52388 = 4812\;\mathrm{'\{'}J/mol{'\}'} = 4.81\;\mathrm{'\{'}kJ/mol{'\}'}$$

$$K_p = e^{-\Delta G^\circ/(RT)} = e^{-4812/(8.314 \times 298)} = e^{-1.943} = 0.143$$

(b) $K_p = 0.143 < 1$, so reactants are favoured at $298\;\mathrm{'\{'}K{'\}'}$.

(c) At $\Delta G = 0$: $T = \Delta H^\circ / \Delta S^\circ = 57200 / 175.8 = 325.4\;\mathrm{'\{'}K{'\}'}$.

(d) Decreasing volume increases pressure. The system shifts toward fewer moles of gas (left, toward $\mathrm{'\{'}N{'\}'}_2\mathrm{'\{'}O{'\}'}_4$), decreasing $[\mathrm{'\{'}NO{'\}'}_2]$. $K_p$ is unchanged (only temperature affects $K$).

</details>

<summary>Question 2: Acid-base equilibrium and titration (FRQ)</summary>

A $50.0\;\mathrm{'\{'}mL{'\}'}$ sample of $0.100\;\mathrm{'\{'}M{'\}'}$ $\mathrm{'\{'}HCN{'\}'}$ ($K_a = 6.2 \times 10^{-10}$) is titrated with $0.100\;\mathrm{'\{'}M{'\}'}$ $\mathrm{'\{'}NaOH{'\}'}$.

(a) Calculate the initial pH of the $\mathrm{'\{'}HCN{'\}'}$ solution.

(b) Calculate the pH at the half-equivalence point.

(c) Calculate the pH at the equivalence point.

(d) Which indicator would be most appropriate: phenolphthalein ($\mathrm{'\{'}p{'\}'}K_a = 9.7$) or bromothymol blue ($\mathrm{'\{'}p{'\}'}K_a = 7.0$)? Justify.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

(a) Since $K_a$ is very small, $[\mathrm{'\{'}H{'\}'}^+] = \sqrt{K_a \cdot C_0} = \sqrt{(6.2 \times 10^{-10})(0.100)} = 7.87 \times 10^{-6}\;\mathrm{'\{'}M{'\}'}$.

$\mathrm{'\{'}pH{'\}'} = -\log(7.87 \times 10^{-6}) = 5.10$.

(b) At half-equivalence, $[\mathrm{'\{'}HCN{'\}'}] = [\mathrm{'\{'}CN{'\}'}^-]$. By Henderson-Hasselbalch: $\mathrm{'\{'}pH{'\}'} = \mathrm{'\{'}p{'\}'}K_a = -\log(6.2 \times 10^{-10}) = 9.21$.

(c) At equivalence, all $\mathrm{'\{'}HCN{'\}'}$ has been converted to $\mathrm{'\{'}CN{'\}'}^-$. Moles of $\mathrm{'\{'}CN{'\}'}^-$ = $0.100 \times 0.0500 = 0.00500\;\mathrm{'\{'}mol{'\}'}$. Total volume = $100.0\;\mathrm{'\{'}mL{'\}'}$. $[\mathrm{'\{'}CN{'\}'}^-] = 0.0500\;\mathrm{'\{'}M{'\}'}$.

$K_b = K_w / K_a = 1.0 \times 10^{-14} / 6.2 \times 10^{-10} = 1.61 \times 10^{-5}$.

$[\mathrm{'\{'}OH{'\}'}^-] = \sqrt{K_b \cdot C_0} = \sqrt{(1.61 \times 10^{-5})(0.0500)} = 8.97 \times 10^{-4}\;\mathrm{'\{'}M{'\}'}$.

$\mathrm{'\{'}pOH{'\}'} = 3.05$, $\mathrm{'\{'}pH{'\}'} = 14 - 3.05 = 10.95$.

(d) Phenolphthalein ($\mathrm{'\{'}p{'\}'}K_a = 9.7$) is appropriate because the equivalence point pH is 10.95, which falls within the phenolphthalein transition range (approximately 8.2--10.0). Bromothymol blue transitions around pH 7, far from the equivalence point.

</details>

Consider the following standard reduction potentials:

- $\mathrm{'\{'}Ag{'\}'}^+(aq) + e^- \to \mathrm{'\{'}Ag{'\}'}(s)$, $E^\circ = +0.80\;\mathrm{'\{'}V{'\}'}$
- $\mathrm{'\{'}Fe{'\}'}^{3+}(aq) + e^- \to \mathrm{'\{'}Fe{'\}'}^{2+}(aq)$, $E^\circ = +0.77\;\mathrm{'\{'}V{'\}'}$

(a) Write the balanced net ionic equation for the spontaneous reaction and calculate $E_{\mathrm{'\{'}cell{'\}'}}^\circ$.

(d) Calculate $E_{\mathrm{'\{'}cell{'\}'}}$ when $[\mathrm{'\{'}Ag{'\}'}^+] = 0.010\;\mathrm{'\{'}M{'\}'}$, $[\mathrm{'\{'}Fe{'\}'}^{3+}] = 0.10\;\mathrm{'\{'}M{'\}'}$, and $[\mathrm{'\{'}Fe{'\}'}^{2+}] = 0.0010\;\mathrm{'\{'}M{'\}'}$.

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

(a) $\mathrm{'\{'}Fe{'\}'}^{2+}$ has the less positive $E^\circ$, so it is oxidised at the anode. $\mathrm{'\{'}Ag{'\}'}^+$ is reduced at the cathode.

Oxidation (anode): $\mathrm{'\{'}Fe{'\}'}^{2+}(aq) \to \mathrm{'\{'}Fe{'\}'}^{3+}(aq) + e^-$

Reduction (cathode): $\mathrm{'\{'}Ag{'\}'}^+(aq) + e^- \to \mathrm{'\{'}Ag{'\}'}(s)$

Net: $\mathrm{'\{'}Fe{'\}'}^{2+}(aq) + \mathrm{'\{'}Ag{'\}'}^+(aq) \to \mathrm{'\{'}Fe{'\}'}^{3+}(aq) + \mathrm{'\{'}Ag{'\}'}(s)$

$E_{\mathrm{'\{'}cell{'\}'}}^\circ = E_{\mathrm{'\{'}cathode{'\}'}}^\circ - E_{\mathrm{'\{'}anode{'\}'}}^\circ = 0.80 - 0.77 = 0.03\;\mathrm{'\{'}V{'\}'}$

(b) $\Delta G^\circ = -nFE^\circ = -(1)(96485)(0.03) = -2895\;\mathrm{'\{'}J/mol{'\}'} = -2.9\;\mathrm{'\{'}kJ/mol{'\}'}$

(c) $K = e^{-\Delta G^\circ/(RT)} = e^{2895/(8.314 \times 298)} = e^{1.167} = 3.21$

(d) $Q = \frac{[\mathrm{'\{'}Fe{'\}'}^{3+}]}{[\mathrm{'\{'}Fe{'\}'}^{2+}][\mathrm{'\{'}Ag{'\}'}^+]} = \frac{0.10}{(0.0010)(0.010)} = 10000$

$E = E^\circ - \frac{0.0592}{1}\log Q = 0.03 - 0.0592\log(10000) = 0.03 - 0.0592(4) = 0.03 - 0.237 = -0.207\;\mathrm{'\{'}V{'\}'}$

$E < 0$, so the reverse reaction is spontaneous under these conditions (high $[\mathrm{'\{'}Fe{'\}'}^{3+}]$, low $[\mathrm{'\{'}Ag{'\}'}^+]$).

</details>

<summary>Question 4: Kinetics and mechanism (FRQ)</summary>

The reaction $2\mathrm{'\{'}NO{'\}'}(g) + \mathrm{'\{'}Br{'\}'}_2(g) \to 2\mathrm{'\{'}NOBr{'\}'}(g)$ has the experimental rate law $\mathrm{'\{'}Rate{'\}'} = k[\mathrm{'\{'}NO{'\}'}]^2[\mathrm{'\{'}Br{'\}'}_2]$.

(a) What is the overall order of the reaction?

(b) A proposed mechanism is:

Step 1 (fast): $\mathrm{'\{'}NO{'\}'}(g) + \mathrm{'\{'}Br{'\}'}_2(g) \rightleftharpoons \mathrm{'\{'}NOBr{'\}'}_2(g)$

Step 2 (slow): $\mathrm{'\{'}NOBr{'\}'}_2(g) + \mathrm{'\{'}NO{'\}'}(g) \to 2\mathrm{'\{'}NOBr{'\}'}(g)$

Show that this mechanism is consistent with the experimental rate law.

(c) Identify the intermediate in the mechanism.

(d) How would the rate change if the concentration of $\mathrm{'\{'}Br{'\}'}_2$ were doubled while $[\mathrm{'\{'}NO{'\}'}]$ was held constant?

</details>

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

(a) Overall order = $2 + 1 = 3$.

(b) Step 1 is a fast equilibrium: $K = \frac{[\mathrm{'\{'}NOBr{'\}'}_2]}{[\mathrm{'\{'}NO{'\}'}][\mathrm{'\{'}Br{'\}'}_2]}$, so $[\mathrm{'\{'}NOBr{'\}'}_2] = K[\mathrm{'\{'}NO{'\}'}][\mathrm{'\{'}Br{'\}'}_2]$.

The rate is determined by the slow step: $\mathrm{'\{'}Rate{'\}'} = k_2[\mathrm{'\{'}NOBr{'\}'}_2][\mathrm{'\{'}NO{'\}'}]$.

Substituting: $\mathrm{'\{'}Rate{'\}'} = k_2 K[\mathrm{'\{'}NO{'\}'}][\mathrm{'\{'}Br{'\}'}_2][\mathrm{'\{'}NO{'\}'}] = k_2 K[\mathrm{'\{'}NO{'\}'}]^2[\mathrm{'\{'}Br{'\}'}_2]$.

This matches the experimental rate law with $k = k_2 K$.

(c) The intermediate is $\mathrm{'\{'}NOBr{'\}'}_2$ -- it is produced in step 1 and consumed in step 2, so it does not appear in the overall reaction.

(d) Since the reaction is first order in $\mathrm{'\{'}Br{'\}'}_2$, doubling $[\mathrm{'\{'}Br{'\}'}_2]$ would double the rate (rate $\propto [\mathrm{'\{'}Br{'\}'}_2]^1$).

</details>

Which of the following statements is correct?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

(D) is correct. At the equivalence point of a weak acid--strong base titration, the solution contains the conjugate base of the weak acid, which hydrolyses to produce $\mathrm{'\{'}OH{'\}'}^-$, giving $\mathrm{'\{'}pH{'\}'} > 7$.

(A) is incorrect: catalysts do not change $K$.

(B) is incorrect: $\mathrm{'\{'}O{'\}'}_2$ has a bond order of 2. Its Lewis structure shows a double bond, and MO theory confirms bond order $= (8 - 4)/2 = 2$.

(C) is incorrect: $n$ in the Nernst equation is the moles of electrons transferred in the balanced redox equation.
