### Civil War and Reconstruction

<PracticeProblem
  question="Which of the following best explains why the invention of the cotton gin (1793) intensified the institution of slavery in the American South?"
  options={[

    'It was primarily used by Northern abolitionists to boycott Southern cotton',
    'It reduced the global demand for cotton, redirecting Southern agriculture to food crops',
  ]}
  correctAnswer={0}
  explanation="Eli Whitney's cotton gin made processing short-staple cotton (the type that could grow across most of the South) efficient and profitable by quickly separating cotton fibres from seeds. This triggered a massive expansion of cotton cultivation ('King Cotton') across the Deep South, dramatically increasing the demand for enslaved labour. Far from reducing slavery, the cotton gin made it more entrenched and profitable, contributing to the sectional tensions that led to the Civil War."

### Industrialisation and Modern America

<PracticeProblem
  question="The Sherman Antitrust Act (1890) was passed primarily in response to which development in the late 19th century?"
  options={[
    'Labour union strikes and demands for higher wages',
    'The growth of large corporate monopolies (trusts) that restricted competition and controlled markets',
    'Rapid immigration from Southern and Eastern Europe',
    'The decline of agricultural prices and rural economic crisis',
  ]}
  correctAnswer={1}
  explanation="The Sherman Antitrust Act was passed to address the growing power of monopolistic trusts (such as Standard Oil, Carnegie Steel, and the sugar trust) that dominated entire industries, restricted competition, and controlled prices. While the Act was initially weakly enforced, it established the precedent for government regulation of business and was later strengthened by the Clayton Antitrust Act (1914). The rise of big business and the concentration of economic power were defining features of the Gilded Age."

  correctAnswer={1}
  explanation="The Marshall Plan (officially the European Recovery Program) provided approximately $13 billion in aid to Western European countries. It was motivated by both economic self-interest (rebuilding European markets for American goods) and the strategic Cold War goal of preventing communist parties from gaining power in economically desperate nations. By stabilising Western European economies, the US aimed to create a prosperous democratic bloc resistant to Soviet influence. The USSR viewed it as economic imperialism and pressured Eastern European countries to reject the aid."
