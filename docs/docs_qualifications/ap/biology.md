# AP Biology Study Guide

Comprehensive study guide for AP Biology, aligned with the College Board Course and Exam Description.
Covers the full curriculum organised around the four Big Ideas: evolution, energetics, information
transfer, and systems interactions.

## 1. Chemistry of Life

### Water Properties

Water's polarity gives it unique properties essential for life:

1. **Cohesion and adhesion:** Hydrogen bonds between water molecules (cohesion) and between water and
   other surfaces (adhesion) enable capillary action and the transpiration stream in plants.
2. **High specific heat capacity:** $4.18\ \text{J/(g·°C)}$. Water absorbs and releases large
   amounts of energy with minimal temperature change, moderating climate and stabilising organism
   temperature.
3. **High heat of vaporisation:** Significant energy is required to evaporate water, providing
   evaporative cooling (e.g., sweating).
4. **Lower density as a solid:** Hydrogen bonds in ice create an open lattice, so ice is less dense
   than liquid water and floats — insulating aquatic habitats in winter.
5. **Universal solvent:** Polar molecules and ions dissolve readily, enabling biochemical reactions in
   aqueous solution.
6. **High surface tension:** Cohesion at the air-water interface creates a surface film supporting
   small organisms.

### Macromolecules

All biological macromolecules are polymers built from monomers via dehydration synthesis (condensation)
and broken down by hydrolysis.

**Carbohydrates:**

- Monomers: monosaccharides (glucose, fructose, galactose)
- Polymers: polysaccharides (starch, glycogen, cellulose, chitin)
- Functions: energy storage (starch in plants, glycogen in animals), structural support (cellulose in
  plant cell walls)
- All have the general formula $(\text{CH}_2\text{O})_n$

**Lipids:**

- Not true polymers; assembled from glycerol and fatty acids
- Triglycerides: one glycerol + three fatty acids; primary energy storage molecule
- Phospholipids: glycerol + two fatty acids + phosphate group; form cell membranes
- Steroids: four fused carbon rings (cholesterol, testosterone, oestrogen)
- Saturated fatty acids: no double bonds (solid at room temperature); unsaturated: one or more double
  bonds (liquid at room temperature)

**Proteins:**

- Monomers: 20 amino acids, each with an amino group, carboxyl group, and variable R group
- Polypeptides: amino acids linked by peptide bonds (formed by dehydration synthesis)
- Four levels of structure:
  - **Primary:** linear sequence of amino acids
  - **Secondary:** alpha-helices and beta-pleated sheets (hydrogen bonding)
  - **Tertiary:** 3D folding of a single polypeptide (R-group interactions: H-bonds, ionic bonds,
    disulphide bridges, hydrophobic interactions)
  - **Quaternary:** assembly of multiple polypeptide subunits (e.g., haemoglobin)
- Functions: enzymes, transport, structural support, signalling, defence, movement

**Nucleic Acids:**

- Monomers: nucleotides (phosphate group, pentose sugar, nitrogenous base)
- DNA: double-stranded helix; deoxyribose sugar; bases A, T, G, C
- RNA: single-stranded; ribose sugar; bases A, U, G, C
- ATP: adenosine triphosphate — the energy currency of the cell
- Functions: information storage (DNA), protein synthesis (mRNA, tRNA, rRNA), energy transfer (ATP)

### Enzymes

- Enzymes are biological catalysts (typically proteins) that lower the activation energy of reactions
  without being consumed
- **Active site:** specific region that binds the substrate (lock-and-key or induced-fit model)
- **Induced fit:** binding of the substrate causes a conformational change in the enzyme that
  tightens the fit
- **Factors affecting enzyme activity:**
  - Temperature: rate increases with temperature until the enzyme denatures
  - pH: each enzyme has an optimal pH range
  - Substrate concentration: rate increases until all active sites are saturated ($V_{\max}$)
  - Enzyme concentration: rate increases with more enzyme at fixed substrate concentration
- **Inhibition:**
  - Competitive inhibitors: bind to the active site, competing with the substrate
  - Non-competitive (allosteric) inhibitors: bind to a different site, changing the enzyme's shape
  - Feedback inhibition: the end product of a metabolic pathway inhibits an enzyme earlier in the
    pathway

## 2. Cell Structure and Function

### Prokaryotic vs Eukaryotic Cells

| Feature | Prokaryotic | Eukaryotic |
| ------- | ----------- | ---------- |
| Nucleus | No (nucleoid region) | Yes (membrane-bound) |
| Membrane-bound organelles | No | Yes |
| Size | Typically 0.1--5 µm | Typically 10--100 µm |
| DNA | Circular | Linear (in nucleus) |
| Ribosomes | 70S (smaller) | 80S (larger) |
| Cell wall | Peptidoglycan (bacteria) | Cellulose (plants), chitin (fungi) |
| Examples | Bacteria, archaea | Animals, plants, fungi, protists |

### Organelles

| Organelle | Function |
| --------- | -------- |
| Nucleus | Stores DNA, controls gene expression, site of transcription |
| Nucleolus | Ribosomal RNA (rRNA) synthesis, ribosome assembly |
| Rough ER | Protein synthesis (ribosomes attached), folding and modification |
| Smooth ER | Lipid synthesis, detoxification, calcium storage |
| Golgi apparatus | Modifies, sorts, and packages proteins and lipids for transport |
| Mitochondria | Cellular respiration (Krebs cycle, ETC), ATP production |
| Chloroplasts | Photosynthesis (light reactions, Calvin cycle) — plants only |
| Lysosomes | Intracellular digestion (contain hydrolytic enzymes) |
| Peroxisomes | Break down fatty acids, detoxify harmful substances |
| Vacuoles | Storage, turgor pressure (large central vacuole in plants) |
| Ribosomes | Protein synthesis (free in cytoplasm or on rough ER) |
| Cytoskeleton | Structural support, cell movement, intracellular transport (microfilaments, intermediate filaments, microtubules) |

### Cell Membrane

The fluid mosaic model: phospholipid bilayer with embedded proteins that can drift laterally.

- **Phospholipid bilayer:** hydrophilic phosphate heads face outward; hydrophobic fatty acid tails
  face inward — selectively permeable
- **Transport proteins:** channel proteins and carrier proteins facilitate movement of specific
  molecules across the membrane
- **Cholesterol:** modulates membrane fluidity in animal cells

### Membrane Transport

| Type | Direction | Energy Required? | Examples |
| ---- | --------- | ---------------- | -------- |
| Simple diffusion | High to low concentration | No | O$_2$, CO$_2$, small non-polar molecules |
| Facilitated diffusion | High to low concentration | No (protein-assisted) | Glucose, ions, amino acids |
| Osmosis | Water across selectively permeable membrane | No | Water movement |
| Active transport | Low to high concentration | Yes (ATP) | Na$^+$/K$^+$ pump, proton pump |
| Cotransport | Uses gradient established by active transport | Yes (indirect) | Glucose-Na$^+$ symporter |
| Bulk transport | Endocytosis/exocytosis | Yes (vesicle formation) | Large molecules, particles |

**Tonicity:**
- **Hypotonic:** lower solute concentration outside — water enters cell (cell swells)
- **Hypertonic:** higher solute concentration outside — water leaves cell (cell shrinks)
- **Isotonic:** equal solute concentrations — no net water movement

### Cell Cycle and Mitosis

**Interphase** ($\text{G}_1 \to \text{S} \to \text{G}_2$): cell growth, DNA replication ($\text{S}$ phase),
preparation for division.

**Mitosis** (PMAT):

1. **Prophase:** chromatin condenses into chromosomes, spindle fibres form, nucleolus disappears
2. **Metaphase:** chromosomes align at the cell equator (metaphase plate)
3. **Anaphase:** sister chromatids separate and move to opposite poles
4. **Telophase:** nuclear membranes reform, chromosomes decondense, spindle fibres break down

**Cytokinesis:** cytoplasm divides. Animal cells form a cleavage furrow; plant cells form a cell plate.

### Meiosis

Two rounds of division producing four genetically distinct haploid ($n$) cells from one diploid ($2n$)
cell:

- **Meiosis I:** homologous chromosomes separate (crossing over occurs in prophase I — increases
  genetic variation)
- **Meiosis II:** sister chromatids separate (similar to mitosis)

Sources of genetic variation:
1. Crossing over (recombination)
2. Independent assortment of homologous chromosomes
3. Random fertilisation

## 3. Cellular Energetics

### Photosynthesis

Overall equation:

$$
6\text{CO}_2 + 6\text{H}_2\text{O} + \text{light energy} \to \text{C}_6\text{H}_{12}\text{O}_6 + 6\text{O}_2
$$

**Light reactions** (in thylakoid membranes):

- Photosystems I and II absorb light energy, exciting electrons
- Water is split (photolysis): $2\text{H}_2\text{O} \to 4\text{H}^+ + 4\text{e}^- + \text{O}_2$
- Electrons pass through an electron transport chain (ETC), generating a proton gradient
- ATP synthase uses the proton gradient to produce ATP (chemiosmosis)
- NADP$^+$ is reduced to NADPH
- Outputs: ATP, NADPH, O$_2$

**Calvin cycle** (in stroma):

- CO$_2$ is fixed by RuBisCO, combining with RuBP to form a 6-carbon intermediate that splits into
  two molecules of 3-PGA
- 3-PGA is reduced to G3P using ATP and NADPH
- G3P is used to build glucose and regenerate RuBP
- For every 3 CO$_2$ fixed, one G3P net gain (6 G3P produced, 5 used to regenerate 3 RuBP)
- Input: 3 CO$_2$, 9 ATP, 6 NADPH per turn

### Cellular Respiration

Overall equation:

$$
\text{C}_6\text{H}_{12}\text{O}_6 + 6\text{O}_2 \to 6\text{CO}_2 + 6\text{H}_2\text{O} + \text{ATP}
$$

**Glycolysis** (cytoplasm):

- Glucose (6C) is split into two pyruvate (3C) molecules
- Net yield: 2 ATP (substrate-level phosphorylation), 2 NADH
- Does not require oxygen (occurs in both aerobic and anaerobic conditions)

**Pyruvate oxidation** (matrix of mitochondria):

- Each pyruvate (3C) is converted to acetyl-CoA (2C) + CO$_2$
- Produces 1 NADH per pyruvate (2 total per glucose)

**Krebs cycle (Citric Acid Cycle)** (matrix of mitochondria):

- Acetyl-CoA (2C) combines with oxaloacetate (4C) to form citrate (6C)
- Series of redox reactions regenerate oxaloacetate; 2 CO$_2$ released per turn
- Per turn: 3 NADH, 1 FADH$_2$, 1 ATP (GTP)
- Per glucose (2 turns): 6 NADH, 2 FADH$_2$, 2 ATP

**Electron Transport Chain** (inner mitochondrial membrane):

- NADH and FADH$_2$ donate electrons to the ETC
- Electrons pass through protein complexes, pumping protons into the intermembrane space
- Proton gradient drives ATP synthase (chemiosmosis)
- Oxygen is the final electron acceptor, forming water
- Approximate ATP yield: ~26--28 ATP from oxidative phosphorylation

**Total ATP yield per glucose:** approximately 30--32 ATP (varies by cell type and shuttle mechanisms).

### Fermentation

When oxygen is unavailable, pyruvate undergoes fermentation to regenerate NAD$^+$ for glycolysis:

- **Lactic acid fermentation:** pyruvate $\to$ lactate (occurs in animal muscle cells)
- **Alcoholic fermentation:** pyruvate $\to$ ethanol + CO$_2$ (occurs in yeast)

Net yield: 2 ATP per glucose (only from glycolysis).

## 4. Cell Communication and Cell Cycle

### Signal Transduction

1. **Reception:** a signalling molecule (ligand) binds to a receptor protein on (or inside) the target
   cell
   - Cell-surface receptors (G-protein coupled receptors, receptor tyrosine kinases) for
     hydrophilic ligands
   - Intracellular receptors (steroid hormone receptors) for hydrophobic ligands
2. **Transduction:** the signal is relayed and amplified through a signalling cascade (often involving
   phosphorylation by protein kinases and second messengers such as cAMP, Ca$^{2+}$, IP$_3$)
3. **Response:** the signal triggers a specific cellular response (gene expression, enzyme activation,
   cytoskeletal rearrangement)

Apoptosis (programmed cell death) is a controlled process essential for development and removing damaged
cells.

### Cell Cycle Regulation

**Checkpoints:**

- **G$_1$ checkpoint:** cell size, nutrients, growth factors, DNA damage
- **G$_2$ checkpoint:** DNA replication complete and accurate
- **M checkpoint (spindle assembly):** all chromosomes attached to spindle fibres

**Cyclins and CDKs (cyclin-dependent kinases):** Cyclin concentrations rise and fall throughout the
cell cycle, binding to CDKs to form active complexes that drive the cycle forward.

### Cancer

Cancer results from uncontrolled cell division caused by mutations in genes that regulate the cell
cycle:

- **Proto-oncogenes:** normal genes that promote cell division; mutations convert them to
  **oncogenes** that cause excessive division
- **Tumour suppressor genes** (e.g., p53): normal genes that inhibit cell division or trigger
  apoptosis; mutations disable these safeguards

## 5. Heredity

### Mendelian Genetics

**Law of Segregation:** Each individual carries two alleles for each trait; gametes receive one allele
at random.

**Law of Independent Assortment:** Genes on different chromosomes assort independently during meiosis
(provided they are not linked).

**Genetic crosses:**

| Cross | Ratio (phenotype) | Ratio (genotype) |
| ----- | ---------------- | ---------------- |
| Monohybrid (Aa × Aa) | 3:1 | 1:2:1 |
| Dihybrid (AaBb × AaBb) | 9:3:3:1 | — |
| Test cross (Aa × aa) | 1:1 | 1:1 |

**Probability rules:**
- Multiplication rule: $P(A \text{ and } B) = P(A) \times P(B)$ (independent events)
- Addition rule: $P(A \text{ or } B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \text{ and } B)$

### Non-Mendelian Genetics

- **Incomplete dominance:** heterozygote has an intermediate phenotype (e.g., red × white = pink)
- **Codominance:** both alleles are fully expressed (e.g., blood type AB)
- **Multiple alleles:** more than two alleles exist for a gene (e.g., I$^\text{A}$, I$^\text{B}$, i for
  blood type)
- **Polygenic inheritance:** one trait is controlled by multiple genes (e.g., height, skin colour)
- **Epistasis:** one gene masks the expression of another (e.g., Labrador retriever coat colour)
- **Pleiotropy:** one gene affects multiple phenotypic traits (e.g., sickle cell anaemia)
- **Sex-linked traits:** genes on the X chromosome; males are more likely to express recessive
  X-linked traits (e.g., colour blindness, haemophilia)

### Linked Genes and Chromosomal Inheritance

- Genes on the same chromosome are **linked** and tend to be inherited together
- **Crossing over** during meiosis can separate linked genes — the frequency of recombination
  indicates the distance between genes (1% recombination = 1 map unit)
- **Nondisjunction:** failure of chromosomes to separate properly during meiosis, resulting in
  aneuploidy (e.g., Down syndrome = trisomy 21)

### Molecular Genetics

**DNA Replication (semi-conservative):**

1. Helicase unwinds the double helix at the origin of replication
2. Single-strand binding proteins prevent re-annealing
3. Primase synthesises RNA primers
4. DNA polymerase III adds nucleotides in the 5' to 3' direction
5. Leading strand: continuous synthesis; lagging strand: Okazaki fragments (joined by DNA ligase)
6. DNA polymerase I removes RNA primers

**Transcription (DNA → mRNA):**

1. RNA polymerase binds to the promoter region of the gene
2. RNA polymerase synthesises mRNA in the 5' to 3' direction using the DNA template strand (3' to 5')
3. In eukaryotes: pre-mRNA is processed — 5' cap added, poly-A tail added, introns removed by
   splicing (exons joined)
4. Mature mRNA exits the nucleus through nuclear pores

**Translation (mRNA → protein):**

1. mRNA binds to a ribosome
2. The start codon (AUG) establishes the reading frame
3. tRNA molecules bring amino acids matching each codon (anticodon-codon pairing)
4. Peptide bonds form between adjacent amino acids at the ribosome's P site
5. Translation ends at a stop codon (UAA, UAG, UGA); polypeptide is released
6. In eukaryotes: translation occurs at free ribosomes (cytoplasmic proteins) or rough ER
   (membrane/secreted proteins)

### Gene Regulation

- **Operons (prokaryotes):** the lac operon is inducible (turned on by lactose); the trp operon is
  repressible (turned off by tryptophan)
- **Eukaryotic regulation:** transcription factors, enhancers, silencers, histone acetylation
  (activates), DNA methylation (represses), RNA interference (miRNA, siRNA)

## 6. Evolution

### Natural Selection

Darwin's theory rests on four observations:

1. **Variation:** individuals in a population differ in their traits
2. **Inheritance:** traits are heritable (passed from parent to offspring)
3. **Overproduction:** more offspring are produced than can survive
4. **Differential survival and reproduction:** individuals with advantageous traits are more likely
   to survive and reproduce (fitness)

**Types of selection:**
- **Directional:** favours one extreme phenotype
- **Stabilising:** favours the intermediate phenotype
- **Disruptive:** favours both extremes over the intermediate

### Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium

For a population in Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium (no evolution), allele and genotype frequencies remain
constant from generation to generation:

$$
p^2 + 2pq + q^2 = 1
$$

where $p$ = frequency of dominant allele, $q$ = frequency of recessive allele, and $p + q = 1$.

**Conditions for Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium:**
- No mutations
- Random mating
- No natural selection
- Extremely large population (no genetic drift)
- No gene flow (no migration)

### Genetic Drift

Random changes in allele frequencies, more pronounced in small populations:

- **Bottleneck effect:** population size is drastically reduced (disaster), reducing genetic diversity
- **Founder effect:** a small group establishes a new population, carrying only a subset of alleles

### Gene Flow

Movement of individuals (and their alleles) between populations. Gene flow tends to reduce
differences between populations and can introduce new alleles.

### Speciation

- **Allopatric speciation:** geographic separation of populations leads to reproductive isolation
- **Sympatric speciation:** new species arise without geographic isolation (e.g., polyploidy in plants)
- **Prezygotic barriers:** habitat isolation, temporal isolation, behavioural isolation, mechanical
  isolation, gametic isolation
- **Postzygotic barriers:** hybrid inviability, hybrid sterility, hybrid breakdown

### Phylogeny

Phylogenetic trees represent evolutionary relationships among species. Closely related species share
more recent common ancestors. Molecular data (DNA, protein sequences) provide quantitative measures of
evolutionary relatedness through sequence similarity.

### Evidence for Evolution

1. **Fossil record:** shows gradual change over time and transitional forms
2. **Comparative anatomy:**
   - Homologous structures: similar anatomy, different function (e.g., forelimbs of vertebrates) —
     evidence of common ancestry
   - Analogous structures: similar function, different anatomy (e.g., wings of birds vs insects) —
     convergent evolution
   - Vestigial structures: remnants of structures that had a function in ancestors (e.g., appendix,
     pelvic bones in whales)
3. **Molecular biology:** similarities in DNA and protein sequences across species reflect evolutionary
   relatedness
4. **Biogeography:** geographic distribution of species reflects evolutionary history (e.g., Darwin's
   finches on the Galápagos Islands)
5. **Direct observation:** evolution occurs on observable timescales (e.g., antibiotic resistance in
   bacteria, pesticide resistance in insects)

## 8. Exam Tips

1. **Practise writing clear, sequential descriptions.** The AP Biology free-response section rewards
   precise explanations of biological processes. Describe steps in order and use correct terminology.
2. **Connect concepts across units.** The AP exam tests Big Ideas that span multiple topics. For example,
   understand how enzymes (Unit 1) relate to metabolic pathways (Unit 3) and gene regulation (Unit 5).
3. **Use the Claim-Evidence-Reasoning (CER) framework.** State your claim, support it with evidence
   from the question, and provide biological reasoning to connect them.
4. **Know your experimental design.** Be prepared to identify the independent variable, dependent
   variable, controlled variables, and controls in an experiment. Understand the difference between
   a control group and a controlled variable.
5. **Pay attention to directional words.** Words like "compare", "contrast", "predict", "justify",
   and "describe" each require a specific type of response. Read the question carefully.
6. **Practise with data.** The exam frequently provides graphs, tables, and experimental results.
   Be comfortable interpreting data, identifying trends, and drawing conclusions.
7. **Memorise the key processes step-by-step.** Photosynthesis, cellular respiration, DNA replication,
   transcription, and translation are all high-yield. Know the inputs and outputs at each stage.

## 9. Common Mistakes

1. **Confusing mitosis and meiosis.** Mitosis produces two identical diploid cells (growth, repair);
   meiosis produces four genetically distinct haploid cells (gametes). Know the key differences in
   purpose, outcome, and mechanism.
2. **Omitting water in photosynthesis and respiration equations.** Water is a reactant in
   photosynthesis and a product in cellular respiration. These details frequently appear in
   multiple-choice questions.
3. **Confusing active and passive transport.** Passive transport (diffusion, osmosis, facilitated
   diffusion) requires no energy and moves molecules down the concentration gradient. Active
   transport requires ATP and moves molecules against the gradient.
4. **Misidentifying the template and coding strands.** The template strand is read 3' to 5' during
   transcription; the coding (sense) strand has the same sequence as the mRNA (except T → U).
5. **Applying Hardy-Weinberg incorrectly.** $p^2 + 2pq + q^2 = 1$ only holds when all five
   conditions are met. In real populations, evolution occurs precisely because at least one
   condition is violated. Do not confuse allele frequency ($p, q$) with genotype frequency ($p^2, 2pq, q^2$).
6. **Confusing analogous and homologous structures.** Homologous structures share a common ancestor
   (divergent evolution); analogous structures have similar function but different evolutionary
   origin (convergent evolution).
7. **Forgetting the role of enzymes as catalysts.** Enzymes lower activation energy but do not change
   the direction of a reaction or the free energy change ($\Delta G$). They are not consumed by the
   reaction.
