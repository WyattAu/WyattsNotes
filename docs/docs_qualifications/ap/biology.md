## Chemistry of Life (CED Unit 1)

### Water

Water's properties arise from hydrogen bonding between molecules:

- **Cohesion and adhesion:** H-bonds between water molecules (cohesion) and between water and other
  surfaces (adhesion) enable capillary action and transpiration.
- **High specific heat:** $4.18 \, \mathrm{J/(g \cdot ^\circ C){}$; buffers temperature changes.
- **High heat of vaporisation:** $2260 \, \mathrm{J/g{}$; provides evaporative cooling.
- **Lower density of ice:** H-bonds in ice form an open lattice; ice floats, insulating aquatic
  ecosystems.
- **High surface tension:** H-bonds at the surface resist external force.
- **Universal solvent:** Polar molecules and ionic compounds dissolve readily.

### Macromolecules

All macromolecules are polymers built from monomers via dehydration synthesis (condensation) and
Broken down by hydrolysis.

| Macromolecule | Monomer               | Bond           | Key Functions                                    |
| ------------- | --------------------- | -------------- | ------------------------------------------------ |
| Carbohydrates | Monosaccharides       | Glycosidic     | Energy (glucose), structural (cellulose, chitin) |
| Proteins      | Amino acids           | Peptide        | Enzymes, structure, transport, signalling        |
| Nucleic acids | Nucleotides           | Phosphodiester | Genetic info (DNA), protein synthesis (RNA)      |
| Lipids        | Fatty acids, glycerol | Ester          | Energy storage, membranes, signalling            |

Lipids are not true polymers but assemble through non-covalent interactions. Phospholipids have a
Hydrophilic phosphate head and hydrophobic fatty acid tails, forming the basis of cell membranes.

**Amino acid structure:** Central carbon bonded to an amino group ($\text{NH{}_2$), carboxyl group
($\text{COOH{}$), hydrogen, and a variable R group. There are 20 standard amino acids. Peptide bonds
Form between the carboxyl group of one amino acid and the amino group of the next via condensation.

**Protein structure levels:**

1. **Primary:** Linear sequence of amino acids (determined by DNA).
2. **Secondary:** Alpha helices and beta pleated sheets stabilised by H-bonds along the backbone.
3. **Tertiary:** Overall 3D shape stabilised by R-group interactions (H-bonds, ionic bonds,
   disulfide bridges, hydrophobic interactions).
4. **Quaternary:** Assembly of multiple polypeptide subunits (e.g., haemoglobin has four subunits).

### Enzymes

Enzymes are biological catalysts ( proteins, sometimes RNA -- ribozymes) that lower Activation
energy ($E_a$) without being consumed. They are substrate-specific, determined by the Active site
geometry (induced fit model).

**Factors affecting enzyme activity:**

- **Temperature:** Rate increases with temperature until the optimum, then denaturation occurs.
- **pH:** Each enzyme has an optimal pH (pepsin: pH ~2; trypsin: pH ~8).
- **Substrate concentration:** Rate increases until all active sites are saturated ($V_{\max}$).
- **Enzyme concentration:** Rate is directly proportional at constant substrate concentration.

**Competitive inhibition:** Inhibitor resembles the substrate and binds the active site. Can be
Overcome by increasing substrate concentration. **Non-competitive inhibition:** Inhibitor binds an
Allosteric site, changing enzyme shape. Cannot be overcome by adding more substrate.

**Michaelis-Menten kinetics:**

$$v = \frac{V_{\max}[S]}{K_m + [S]}$$

Where $K_m$ is the substrate concentration at half $V_{\max}$. A lower $K_m$ indicates higher
Affinity between enzyme and substrate.

### Common Pitfalls

- Confusing dehydration synthesis with hydrolysis: remember "dehydration" removes water to build
  bonds; "hydrolysis" adds water to break them.
- Assuming all lipids are polymers: lipids are assembled through non-covalent interactions, not
  covalent bonds between monomers.
- Confusing competitive and non-competitive inhibition on a Michaelis-Menten graph: competitive
  inhibition raises the apparent $K_m$ but does not change $V_{\max}$; non-competitive inhibition
  lowers $V_{\max}$ but does not change $K_m$.

## Cell Structure and Function (CED Unit 2)

### Organelles

| Organelle       | Function                                                                          |
| --------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Nucleus         | DNA storage, transcription, mRNA processing                                       |
| Nucleolus       | rRNA synthesis, ribosome assembly                                                 |
| Rough ER        | Protein synthesis (ribosome-studded), folding, modification                       |
| Smooth ER       | Lipid synthesis, detoxification, $\text{Ca{}^{2+}$ storage                        |
| Golgi apparatus | Protein sorting, modification, packaging into vesicles                            |
| Mitochondria    | Aerobic respiration, ATP production (chemiosmosis)                                |
| Chloroplasts    | Photosynthesis (in plants and algae)                                              |
| Lysosomes       | Intracellular digestion (hydrolytic enzymes at pH ~5)                             |
| Peroxisomes     | Breakdown of fatty acids, detoxification of $\text{H{}_2\text{O{}_2$ via catalase |
| Vacuoles        | Storage, turgor pressure (central vacuole in plants)                              |
| Cytoskeleton    | Structural support, intracellular transport, cell division                        |

**Endosymbiotic theory:** Mitochondria and chloroplasts originated from prokaryotic endosymbionts.
Evidence includes double membranes, own circular DNA, 70S ribosomes, and binary fission Replication.

### Membranes and Transport

The fluid mosaic model describes the membrane as a dynamic bilayer of phospholipids with embedded
Proteins, cholesterol, and surface carbohydrates.

**Transport mechanisms:**

| Type                  | Direction                 | Energy Required | Mediator                |
| --------------------- | ------------------------- | --------------- | ----------------------- |
| Simple diffusion      | High to low               | No              | None                    |
| Facilitated diffusion | High to low               | No              | Channel/carrier protein |
| Osmosis               | Water, high to low solute | No              | Aquaporins              |
| Active transport      | Low to high               | Yes (ATP)       | Pump protein            |
| Cotransport           | Low to high               | Yes (gradient)  | Symporter/antiporter    |

**Tonicity:** In hypertonic solutions, water leaves the cell (crenation in animal cells, Plasmolysis
in plant cells). In hypotonic solutions, water enters (lysis in animal cells, turgor Pressure in
plant cells). Isotonic solutions produce no net movement.

### Common Pitfalls

- Confusing osmosis direction: water moves toward the **higher solute concentration**, not toward
  the "more water."
- Assuming all transport through membranes requires energy: passive transport (diffusion,
  facilitated diffusion, osmosis) requires no ATP.
- Misidentifying the smooth ER: it synthesises lipids, not proteins. Rough ER is studded with
  ribosomes for protein synthesis.

## Cellular Energetics (CED Unit 3)

### Photosynthesis

$$6\mathrm{CO{}_2 + 6\mathrm{H{}_2\mathrm{O{} + \text{light energy{} \to \mathrm{C{}_6\mathrm{H{}_{12}\mathrm{O{}_6 + 6\mathrm{O{}_2$$

**Light-dependent reactions (thylakoid membrane):**

- Photosystems II and I absorb light at 680 nm and 700 nm respectively.
- Water is split ($2\mathrm{H{}_2\mathrm{O{} \to 4\mathrm{H{}^+ + 4e^- + \mathrm{O{}_2$).
- Electrons pass through an ETC, pumping $\mathrm{H{}^+$ into the thylakoid lumen.
- $\mathrm{H{}^+$ diffuses through ATP synthase (chemiosmosis), producing ATP.
- NADP$^+$ is reduced to NADPH.

**Calvin cycle (stroma):**

1. **Carbon fixation:** RuBisCO catalyses
   $\mathrm{CO{}_2 + \text{RuBP (5C){} \to 2 \times \text{3-PGA (3C){}$.
2. **Reduction:** 3-PGA is phosphorylated by ATP and reduced by NADPH to G3P.
3. **Regeneration:** Most G3P is used to regenerate RuBP (uses 5 of every 6 G3P molecules; 1 G3P is
   the net product per 3 $\mathrm{CO{}_2$ fixed).

**Net per 3 $\mathrm{CO{}_2$ (one turn of the cycle):** 9 ATP consumed, 6 NADPH consumed, 1 G3P
Produced. To produce one glucose requires 6 turns: 18 ATP + 12 NADPH.

### Cellular Respiration

$$\mathrm{C{}_6\mathrm{H{}_{12}\mathrm{O{}_6 + 6\mathrm{O{}_2 \to 6\mathrm{CO{}_2 + 6\mathrm{H{}_2\mathrm{O{} + \sim 30\text{--{}32\,\mathrm{ATP{}$$

| Stage                     | Location                     | O$_2$ Required? | Net ATP Yield               |
| ------------------------- | ---------------------------- | --------------- | --------------------------- |
| Glycolysis                | Cytoplasm                    | No              | 2 ATP + 2 NADH              |
| Pyruvate oxidation        | Mitochondrial matrix         | No              | 2 NADH                      |
| Citric acid cycle         | Mitochondrial matrix         | No              | 2 ATP + 6 NADH + 2 FADH$_2$ |
| Oxidative phosphorylation | Inner mitochondrial membrane | Yes             | ~26--28 ATP (chemiosmosis)  |

**Chemiosmosis:** The proton gradient ($\mathrm{H{}^+$ concentration gradient) across the inner
Mitochondrial membrane drives ATP synthase. Each NADH yields ~2.5 ATP; each FADH$_2$ yields ~1.5 ATP
(FADH$_2$ drops electrons at complex II, bypassing the first proton pump).

**Fermentation (anaerobic):** Regenerates NAD$^+$ from NADH so glycolysis can continue.

- **Lactic acid fermentation:** Pyruvate $\to$ lactate (muscle cells, some bacteria).
- **Alcoholic fermentation:** Pyruvate $\to$ acetaldehyde $\to$ ethanol + $\mathrm{CO{}_2$ (yeast).

### Common Pitfalls

- Stating that plants do not carry out cellular respiration: plants perform both photosynthesis and
  respiration simultaneously.
- Confusing the products and reactants of the light-dependent reactions and the Calvin cycle. The
  light reactions produce ATP and NADPH; the Calvin cycle consumes them.
- Using the outdated 36--38 ATP yield for respiration. Modern estimates give ~30--32 ATP because
  NADH from glycolysis costs ATP to shuttle into the mitochondrion.

## Cell Communication and Cell Cycle (CED Unit 4)

### Cell Signalling

Three stages of cell signalling:

1. **Reception:** A signalling molecule (ligand) binds a receptor on/in the target cell.

- **G-protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs):** Transmembrane; activate G-proteins that trigger secondary
  messengers (cAMP, IP$_3$DAG).
- **Receptor tyrosine kinases (RTKs):** Dimerise and autophosphorylate upon ligand binding; trigger
  phosphorylation cascades.
- **Intracellular receptors:** Ligands (steroid hormones, thyroid hormones) diffuse through the
  membrane and bind receptors in the cytoplasm or nucleus.

2. **Transduction:** Signal is relayed and amplified, often via phosphorylation cascades (kinases
   add phosphate groups; phosphatases remove them). Secondary messengers (cAMP, $\text{Ca{}^{2+}$)
   amplify the signal.
3. **Response:** Cellular response such as gene expression, enzyme activation, or cytoskeletal
   rearrangement.

### Cell Cycle

| Phase | Events                                                             |
| ----- | ------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| G$_1$ | Cell growth, normal metabolic functions                            |
| S     | DNA replication (synthesis); each chromosome = 2 sister chromatids |
| G$_2$ | Preparation for mitosis; protein synthesis                         |
| M     | Mitosis and cytokinesis                                            |
| G$_0$ | Non-dividing state (some cells enter permanently)                  |

**Mitosis phases:**

- **Prophase:** Chromatin condenses, nucleolus disappears, spindle forms.
- **Prometaphase:** Nuclear envelope breaks down, spindle fibres attach to kinetochores.
- **Metaphase:** Chromosomes align at the metaphase plate.
- **Anaphase:** Sister chromatids separate and move to opposite poles.
- **Telophase:** Nuclear envelopes reform, chromosomes decondense.

**Regulation:** Cyclins and cyclin-dependent kinases (CDKs) control progression. Checkpoints at
G$_1$/S, G$_2$/M, and M (spindle assembly checkpoint) ensure fidelity. P53 is a tumour suppressor
That halts the cycle at G$_1$ when DNA is damaged.

### Common Pitfalls

- Confusing G$_0$ with interphase: G$_0$ is a phase outside the cell cycle where cells are not
  preparing to divide.
- Assuming all cancers are caused by a single mutation: cancer requires mutations in multiple genes
  (oncogenes and tumour suppressor genes).

## Heredity (CED Unit 5)

### Mendelian Genetics

**Law of Segregation:** Each individual carries two alleles for each gene; gametes receive one
Allele at random.

**Law of Independent Assortment:** Genes on different chromosomes assort independently during Gamete
formation.

**Monohybrid cross** ($Aa \times Aa$): genotypic ratio 1 $AA$ : 2 $Aa$ : 1 $aa$; phenotypic ratio 3
dominant : 1 recessive.

**Dihybrid cross** ($AaBb \times AaBb$Unlinked genes): phenotypic ratio 9 : 3 : 3 : 1.

**Incomplete dominance:** Heterozygote shows an intermediate phenotype (e.g., red + white = Pink
snapdragons).

**Codominance:** Both alleles are fully expressed (e.g., $I^A I^B$ blood type = type AB).

**Sex-linked inheritance:** Genes on the X chromosome. Males (XY) express all X-linked alleles.
Recessive X-linked traits (colour blindness, haemophilia) are more common in males because males
Have only one X chromosome.

### Chromosomal Inheritance

**Non-disjunction:** Failure of homologous chromosomes or sister chromatids to separate during
Meiosis, producing gametes with abnormal chromosome numbers.

- **Trisomy 21 (Down syndrome):** Three copies of chromosome 21.
- **Klinefelter syndrome (XXY):** Male with underdeveloped sex characteristics.
- **Turner syndrome (XO):** Female, sterile, short stature.
- **Polyploidy:** More than two complete sets of chromosomes (common in plants).

### Common Pitfalls

- Assuming a 3:1 ratio applies to all crosses: linked genes, incomplete dominance, codominance,
  epistasis, and lethal alleles all produce different ratios.
- Confusing non-disjunction in meiosis I vs meiosis II: meiosis I non-disjunction involves
  homologous chromosomes failing to separate; meiosis II involves sister chromatids.

## Gene Expression and Regulation (CED Unit 6)

### Central Dogma

$$\text{DNA{} \xrightarrow{\text{transcription{}} \text{RNA{} \xrightarrow{\text{translation{}} \text{Protein{}$$

### Transcription (in prokaryotes)

1. **Initiation:** RNA polymerase binds the promoter region. Sigma factor in bacteria helps
   recognise the -35 and -10 consensus sequences.
2. **Elongation:** RNA polymerase synthesises mRNA in the 5' to 3' direction, reading the template
   strand 3' to 5'.
3. **Termination:** At terminator sequences, RNA polymerase dissociates.

**In eukaryotes:** Transcription occurs in the nucleus. RNA polymerase II transcribes mRNA. After
Transcription, pre-mRNA is processed:

- **5' cap:** Added for ribosome binding and mRNA stability.
- **3' poly-A tail:** Added for mRNA stability and export.
- **Splicing:** Introns are removed; exons are joined by the spliceosome. Alternative splicing
  produces multiple protein isoforms from a single gene.

### Translation

1. **Initiation:** Small ribosomal subunit binds mRNA; initiator tRNA (Met) binds the start codon
   (AUG); large subunit joins.
2. **Elongation:** tRNAs bring amino acids matching codons in the A site; peptide bonds form in the
   P site; the ribosome translocates to the next codon.
3. **Termination:** A stop codon (UAA, UAG, UGA) enters the A site; a release factor causes
   polypeptide release and ribosome dissociation.

**Polyribosomes:** Multiple ribosomes translate a single mRNA simultaneously.

### Operons (Prokaryotic Gene Regulation)

**The lac operon (E. Coli):**

- **Structural genes:** lacZ ($\beta$-galactosidase, breaks down lactose), lacY (permease,
  transports lactose), lacA (transacetylase).
- **Promoter:** RNA polymerase binding site.
- **Operator:** Repressor binding site.
- **Regulatory gene (lacI):** Produces the lac repressor.

**Inducible system:** When lactose is absent, the repressor binds the operator, blocking
Transcription. When lactose is present, allolactose (an isomer) binds the repressor, causing it to
Release the operator. CAMP-CAP also activates transcription when glucose is low.

**The trp operon (repressible):**

When tryptophan is abundant, it binds the trp repressor, activating it to block transcription. This
is a negative feedback loop.

### Eukaryotic Gene Regulation

- **Transcription factors:** Proteins that bind enhancers or promoters to activate or repress
  transcription.
- **Epigenetic regulation:** DNA methylation ( represses transcription) and histone modification
  (acetylation opens chromatin; methylation can open or close).
- **RNA interference (RNAi):** Small interfering RNAs (siRNAs) and microRNAs (miRNAs) bind
  complementary mRNA, leading to degradation or translational repression.

### Common Pitfalls

- Confusing introns and exons: exons are expressed (leave the nucleus); introns are intervening
  sequences that are spliced out.
- Assuming the lac operon is active by default: it is normally OFF (repressed) and induced by the
  presence of lactose. The trp operon is normally ON and repressed by tryptophan.

## Natural Selection and Evolution (CED Unit 7)

### Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium

For a gene with two alleles ($A$ and $a$) with frequencies $p$ and $q$:

$$p + q = 1$$

$$p^2 + 2pq + q^2 = 1$$

Where $p^2$ = frequency of $AA$$2pq$ = frequency of $Aa$$q^2$ = frequency of $aa$.

**Conditions for equilibrium (no evolution):**

1. No mutations
2. Random mating
3. No natural selection
4. Extremely large population (no genetic drift)
5. No gene flow (migration)

### Worked Example: Hardy-Weinberg

In a population, the frequency of the recessive allele for cystic fibrosis ($q$) is 0.02. What
Percentage of the population is a heterozygous carrier?

$$p = 1 - q = 1 - 0.02 = 0.98$$

$$2pq = 2(0.98)(0.02) = 0.0392$$

Approximately 3.92% of the population are carriers.

### Types of Selection

| Type        | Effect on Variation                        | Example               |
| ----------- | ------------------------------------------ | --------------------- |
| Directional | Shifts mean toward one extreme             | Antibiotic resistance |
| Stabilising | Reduces variation, favours intermediate    | Human birth weight    |
| Disruptive  | Favors both extremes, reduces intermediate | Beak size in finches  |

### Genetic Drift

Random changes in allele frequencies, stronger in small populations:

- **Bottleneck effect:** Population size is drastically reduced (e.g., natural disaster), reducing
  genetic diversity.
- **Founder effect:** A small group establishes a new population; allele frequencies differ from the
  source population.

### Speciation

- **Allopatric speciation:** Geographic isolation separates populations; reproductive isolation
  evolves (most common mechanism).
- **Sympatric speciation:** Speciation without geographic isolation (e.g., polyploidy in plants,
  sexual selection).
- **Prezygotic barriers:** Habitat isolation, temporal isolation, behavioural isolation, mechanical
  isolation, gametic isolation.
- **Postzygotic barriers:** Hybrid inviability, hybrid sterility (mule), hybrid breakdown.

### Phylogeny

Phylogenetic trees represent evolutionary relationships. Branch points (nodes) represent common
Ancestors. Closely related species share more recent common ancestors. Molecular clocks use mutation
Rates in DNA or protein sequences to estimate divergence times.

**Cladistics:** Groups organisms into clades based on shared derived characteristics
(synapomorphies). A clade includes an ancestor and all its descendants (monophyletic group).

### Common Pitfalls

- Assuming Hardy-Weinberg applies to real populations: it is a null model; deviation from
  equilibrium indicates that evolutionary forces are acting.
- Confusing genetic drift with natural selection: drift is random; selection is non-random.
- Interpreting phylogenetic trees as ladders or progressions: trees show relationships, not
  "advancement." The tips of branches are all equally "evolved."

## Ecology (CED Unit 8)

### Population Dynamics

**Population growth models:**

Exponential (ideal, unlimited resources):

$$\frac{dN}{dt} = rN$$

Logistic (carrying capacity $K$):

$$\frac{dN}{dt} = rN\left(1 - \frac{N}{K}\right)$$

Where $N$ = population size, $r$ = intrinsic rate of increase, $K$ = carrying capacity.

**Survivorship curves:**

- **Type I:** High survival early in life, rapid decline late (humans, large mammals).
- **Type II:** Constant mortality rate throughout life (birds, small mammals).
- **Type III:** High early mortality, high survival after a threshold (insects, plants, marine
  invertebrates).

**Life history strategies:**

- **r-selected species:** Many offspring, low parental care, rapid maturation, short lifespan
  (weeds, bacteria).
- **K-selected species:** Few offspring, high parental care, slow maturation, long lifespan
  (elephants, humans).

### Communities

**Symbiotic relationships:**

- **Mutualism:** Both benefit (+/+) (e.g., mycorrhizae and plant roots).
- **Commensalism:** One benefits, other unaffected (+/0) (e.g., barnacles on whales).
- **Parasitism:** One benefits, other harmed (+/--) (e.g., tapeworms).

**Ecological succession:**

- **Primary succession:** Colonisation of lifeless substrate (e.g., volcanic rock); pioneer species
  (lichens) $\to$ soil formation $\to$ intermediate species $\to$ climax community.
- **Secondary succession:** Recovery of a disturbed area where soil remains (e.g., after a fire);
  occurs faster than primary succession.

**Keystone species:** Species whose removal causes disproportionate changes in community structure
(e.g., sea otters maintain kelp forest ecosystems by controlling sea urchin populations).

**Island biogeography:** Species richness on islands depends on island size (larger = more species)
And distance from mainland (closer = more species). Equilibrium between immigration and extinction
Rates determines species number.

### Biogeochemical Cycles

| Cycle      | Reservoir                       | Key Processes                                           |
| ---------- | ------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------- |
| Carbon     | Atmosphere, ocean, fossil fuels | Photosynthesis, respiration, combustion, decomposition  |
| Nitrogen   | Atmosphere ($\text{N{}_2$)      | Nitrogen fixation, nitrification, denitrification       |
| Phosphorus | Rock, sediment                  | Weathering, absorption by plants, decomposition         |
| Water      | Oceans, atmosphere, glaciers    | Evaporation, condensation, precipitation, transpiration |

**Nitrogen cycle details:** Atmospheric $\text{N{}_2$ is fixed by nitrogen-fixing bacteria
(Rhizobium in legume root nodules, cyanobacteria) or lightning into $\text{NH{}_3$ (ammonia).
Nitrifying bacteria convert $\text{NH{}_3 \to \text{NO{}_2^- \to \text{NO{}_3^-$ (usable by plants).
Denitrifying bacteria convert $\text{NO{}_3^- \to \text{N{}_2$ back to the atmosphere.

### Common Pitfalls

- Confusing $r$-selection and $K$-selection as absolutes: most species fall on a continuum, not at
  either extreme.
- Assuming the phosphorus cycle involves the atmosphere: phosphorus does not have a significant
  gaseous phase, unlike carbon, nitrogen, and water.
- Confusing primary and secondary succession: primary occurs on substrate with no soil; secondary
  occurs where soil is already present after a disturbance.

## Exam Practice Questions

### Multiple Choice

1. A population of flowers has two alleles for petal colour: $R$ (red, dominant) and $r$ (white,
   recessive). If the frequency of $r$ is 0.3, what is the frequency of heterozygous individuals?

(A) 0.09 (B) 0.21 (C) 0.42 (D) 0.49

2. Which of the following best describes the role of oxygen in aerobic respiration?

(A) It donates electrons to the electron transport chain. (B) It is the final electron acceptor,
forming water. (C) It is produced during glycolysis. (D) It directly generates ATP by
substrate-level phosphorylation.

3. A cell with 20 chromosomes undergoes meiosis. How many chromosomes will each daughter cell
   contain?

(A) 10 (B) 20 (C) 40 (D) 80

### Free Response

4. In a certain plant population, flower colour is controlled by a single gene with two alleles:
   $C^R$ (red, dominant) and $C^W$ (white, codominant; heterozygotes are pink). A population of 500
   plants includes 200 red, 200 pink, and 100 white plants. (a) Calculate the allele frequencies of
   $C^R$ and $C^W$. (b) Is this population in Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium? Justify with a chi-square
   test ($\alpha = 0.05$Critical value = 5.99).

**Answers:**

1. **(C)** $p = 0.7$$q = 0.3$. $2pq = 2(0.7)(0.3) = 0.42$.
2. **(B)** Oxygen is the final electron acceptor at complex IV of the ETC, combining with electrons
   and protons to form $\mathrm{H{}_2\mathrm{O{}$.
3. **(A)** Meiosis halves the chromosome number: $20/2 = 10$.
4. (a) Total alleles = $500 \times 2 = 1000$. $C^R$ count: $(200 \times 2) + 200 = 600$. $C^W$
   count: $(100 \times 2) + 200 = 400$. $p(C^R) = 600/1000 = 0.6$. $q(C^W) = 400/1000 = 0.4$. (b)
   Expected: $p^2(500) = 0.36 \times 500 = 180$ (red), $2pq(500) = 0.48 \times 500 = 240$ (pink),
   $q^2(500) = 0.16 \times 500 = 80$ (white).
   $\chi^2 = (200-180)^2/180 + (200-240)^2/240 + (100-80)^2/80 = 2.22 + 6.67 + 5.00 = 13.89$. Since
   $13.89 > 5.99$We reject the null hypothesis. The population is not in Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium.
