## 1. Ecosystems and Levels of Organisation

### 1.1 Levels of Organisation in an Ecosystem

$$\text{Individual} \to \text{Population} \to \text{Community} \to \text{Ecosystem} \to \text{Biosphere}$$

- **Individual:** A single organism
- **Population:** All the organisms of one species in a habitat
- **Community:** All the populations of different species in a habitat
- **Ecosystem:** A community of organisms interacting with their non-living environment
- **Biosphere:** All the ecosystems on Earth

### 1.2 Biotic and Abiotic Factors

**Biotic factors** (living): Predation, competition, disease, food availability.

**Abiotic factors** (non-living): Temperature, light intensity, water availability, soil pH, wind
speed, mineral availability.

### 1.3 Adaptations

Organisms are adapted to their environment. Adaptations can be:

| Type        | Description           | Example                                              |
| ----------- | --------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- |
| Structural  | Physical features     | Thick fur in Arctic animals, spines on cacti         |
| Behavioural | Ways organisms behave | Hibernation, migration, nocturnal activity           |
| Functional  | Internal processes    | Producing venom, efficient kidneys in desert animals |

**Extremophiles:** Organisms adapted to extreme environments, such as:

- **Thermophiles:** High temperature (hot springs, deep-sea vents)
- **Halophiles:** High salt concentration
- **Acidophiles:** Low pH

## 2. Feeding Relationships

### 2.1 Food Chains and Food Webs

A **food chain** shows the transfer of energy from one organism to another:

$$\text{Producer} \to \text{Primary consumer} \to \text{Secondary consumer} \to \text{Tertiary consumer}$$

- **Producer:** An organism that makes its own food by photosynthesis (e.g. plants, algae)
- **Consumer:** An organism that eats other organisms
  - **Primary consumer:** Herbivore (eats plants)
  - **Secondary consumer:** Carnivore that eats herbivores
  - **Tertiary consumer:** Carnivore that eats secondary consumers
- **Decomposer:** Breaks down dead material and waste (e.g. bacteria, fungi)

A **food web** shows many interconnected food chains in an ecosystem.

### 2.2 Trophic Levels

Each step in a food chain is a **trophic level**:

| Trophic Level | Organism           | Example |
| ------------- | ------------------ | ------- |
| 1             | Producer           | Grass   |
| 2             | Primary consumer   | Rabbit  |
| 3             | Secondary consumer | Fox     |
| 4             | Tertiary consumer  | Eagle   |

### 2.3 Pyramids of Number, Biomass, and Energy

**Pyramid of number:** Shows the number of organisms at each trophic level. Can be inverted (e.g.
one oak tree supporting many insects).

**Pyramid of biomass:** Shows the total mass of living material at each trophic level. Usually a
pyramid shape (never inverted in a natural ecosystem).

**Pyramid of energy:** Shows the energy available at each trophic level. Always a pyramid shape.

### 2.4 Energy Transfer

Energy is transferred along a food chain, but much is **lost** at each trophic level:

**Ways energy is lost:**

- Respiration (released as heat)
- Excretion (energy in waste products)
- Movement
- Not all parts of the organism are eaten
- Egestion (undigested food)

Only about **10%** of energy is transferred from one trophic level to the next. This is why food
chains are usually short (typically 3--5 trophic levels).

**Implications:**

- Much more biomass is needed at lower trophic levels to support higher levels
- Eating plants (vegetarian diet) is more energy-efficient than eating meat

### 2.5 Calculating Efficiency

$$\text{Efficiency} = \frac{\text{energy available at next level}}{\text{energy available at current level}} \times 100\%$$

**Worked Example.** 10,000 kJ of energy is available at the producer level. 1,000 kJ is transferred
to the primary consumer. Calculate the efficiency.

$$\text{Efficiency} = \frac{1000}{10000} \times 100\% = 10\%$$

## 3. Material Cycles

### 3.1 The Carbon Cycle

Carbon is constantly recycled between the atmosphere, organisms, and the Earth.

**Processes that remove CO$_2$ from the atmosphere:**

- **Photosynthesis:** Plants convert CO$_2$ into glucose
- **Dissolving in oceans:** CO$_2$ dissolves in seawater

**Processes that return CO$_2$ to the atmosphere:**

- **Respiration:** All living organisms release CO$_2$ during respiration
- **Combustion:** Burning fossil fuels and biomass releases CO$_2$
- **Decomposition:** Decomposers break down dead material and release CO$_2$

**Carbon stores:**

- Atmosphere (as CO$_2$)
- Oceans (as dissolved CO$_2$ and carbonate compounds)
- Fossil fuels (coal, oil, natural gas)
- Limestone (calcium carbonate)
- Living organisms (carbohydrates, lipids, proteins)

### 3.2 The Water Cycle

The water cycle describes the continuous movement of water between the atmosphere, land, and oceans.

**Key processes:**

- **Evaporation:** Water from oceans and lakes evaporates (liquid to gas)
- **Transpiration:** Water evaporates from plant leaves
- **Condensation:** Water vapour cools and forms clouds
- **Precipitation:** Water falls as rain, snow, sleet, or hail
- **Surface runoff:** Water flows over the ground into rivers and streams
- **Infiltration:** Water soaks into the ground
- **Percolation:** Water moves through porous rocks

### 3.3 Decomposition

Decomposers (bacteria and fungi) break down dead organic matter, recycling nutrients back into the
soil.

**Factors affecting the rate of decomposition:**

| Factor              | Effect                                            |
| ------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| Temperature         | Higher temperature increases rate (up to optimum) |
| Water content       | Too dry or too wet slows decomposition            |
| Oxygen availability | Aerobic decomposition is faster than anaerobic    |
| pH                  | Extreme pH slows decomposition                    |

### 3.4 Required Practical: Investigating Decomposition

**Method:**

1. Collect equal masses of organic material (e.g. leaf litter).
2. Place samples in different conditions (e.g. warm/moist, cold/dry, warm/dry).
3. Measure the mass of the samples at regular intervals.
4. The sample that loses mass fastest is decomposing most rapidly.

## 4. Biodiversity and Human Impact

### 4.1 What Is Biodiversity?

**Biodiversity** is the variety of all the different species of organisms on Earth, or within a
particular habitat.

**Measuring biodiversity:**

- **Species richness:** The number of different species in an area
- **Index of diversity:** Takes into account both the number of species and the abundance of each
  species

$$D = \frac{N(N - 1)}{\sum n(n - 1)}$$

where $N$ is the total number of organisms and $n$ is the number of organisms of each species.

A higher index of diversity indicates greater biodiversity.

### 4.2 Threats to Biodiversity

| Threat                 | Description                                                                  |
| ---------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Deforestation          | Destruction of habitats for agriculture, logging, urbanisation               |
| - **Agriculture**      | Monoculture farming reduces biodiversity; pesticides kill non-target species |
| - **Urbanisation**     | Building on natural habitats                                                 |
| - **Pollution**        | Air, water, and land pollution harm organisms                                |
| - **Climate change**   | Rising temperatures alter habitats and migration patterns                    |
| - **Overexploitation** | Overfishing, hunting, collecting                                             |

### 4.3 Maintaining Biodiversity

| Method                        | Description                                                   |
| ----------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Breeding programmes           | Captive breeding of endangered species (e.g. pandas)          |
| Seed banks                    | Storing seeds of endangered plants                            |
| - **Protected areas**         | National parks, nature reserves, marine protected areas       |
| - **Reintroduction**          | Releasing captive-bred animals into the wild                  |
| - **Sustainable development** | Meeting present needs without compromising future generations |
| - **Reducing pollution**      | Regulations on emissions, waste disposal, pesticide use       |
| - **Recycling**               | Reduces the demand for raw materials and landfill             |

### 4.4 Trophic Levels and Toxins

Some substances, such as pesticides and heavy metals, accumulate in organisms. Because only about
10% of biomass is passed on at each trophic level, the concentration of these toxins **increases**
at each level.

This is called **bioaccumulation** or **biomagnification**.

**Example:** DDT (a pesticide) accumulates in the fatty tissue of organisms. Top predators (e.g.
birds of prey) accumulate the highest concentrations, which can cause thinning of eggshells and
population decline.

### 4.5 Eutrophication

**Eutrophication** is the over-enrichment of water bodies with nutrients (nitrates and phosphates),
usually from fertiliser runoff.

**Stages:**

1. Nitrates/phosphates wash into rivers and lakes
2. Algae grow rapidly (algal bloom)
3. Algae block light, killing aquatic plants
4. Dead algae and plants are decomposed by bacteria
5. Bacteria use up oxygen during respiration
6. Oxygen levels drop (hypoxia), killing fish and other aquatic organisms

### 4.6 Global Warming

**Greenhouse gases** (CO$_2$, methane, water vapour, nitrous oxide) trap heat in the atmosphere,
causing the Earth's average temperature to rise.

**Evidence for climate change:**

- Rising global temperatures (measured since the 1800s)
- Rising sea levels
- Melting ice caps and glaciers
- Increased frequency of extreme weather events
- Increased atmospheric CO$_2$ concentration

**Consequences:**

- Loss of habitats (polar regions, coral reefs)
- Changes to migration patterns
- Rising sea levels threatening coastal areas
- Changes in rainfall patterns (droughts and floods)
- Spread of tropical diseases to temperate regions

**Reducing climate change:**

- Using renewable energy sources instead of fossil fuels
- Energy efficiency (insulation, LED bulbs)
- Carbon capture and storage
- Reforestation (absorbs CO$_2$)
- International agreements (Paris Agreement)

## 5. Required Practical: Sampling Organisms

### 5.1 Quadrats

A **quadrat** is a square frame (typically 0.5 m $\times$ 0.5 m or 1 m $\times$ 1 m) used to sample
organisms in a habitat.

**Method:**

1. Place the quadrat randomly in the area (use random numbers for coordinates)
2. Count the number of organisms of each species within the quadrat
3. Repeat many times (at least 10)
4. Calculate the mean number per quadrat
5. Multiply by the total area to estimate the total population

### 5.2 Transects

A **transect** is a line across a habitat. A quadrat is placed at regular intervals along the
transect.

**Types:**

- **Line transect:** Record organisms touching the line
- **Belt transect:** Place quadrats along the line

### 5.3 Sweep Nets and Pitfall Traps

- **Sweep nets:** Used for catching insects in long grass
- **Pitfall traps:** Containers set into the ground to catch small animals (insects, spiders)

### 5.4 Estimating Population Size

**Mark-release-recapture:**

$$\text{Estimated population} = \frac{n_1 \times n_2}{n_3}$$

where $n_1$ = number captured and marked first time, $n_2$ = number captured second time, $n_3$ =
number marked in second capture.

**Assumptions:**

- No birth, death, or migration between captures
- Marked individuals mix fully with the population
- Marks do not affect survival or behaviour
- Each individual has an equal chance of being captured

**Worked Example.** 50 woodlice are captured, marked, and released. In a second capture, 40 woodlice
are caught, of which 10 are marked.

$$\text{Population} = \frac{50 \times 40}{10} = 200$$

## Common Pitfalls

- **Confusing species richness and index of diversity.** Species richness counts the number of
  species; the index of diversity also considers the relative abundance of each species.
- **Drawing food chains with arrows in the wrong direction.** Arrows show the direction of energy
  flow (from what is eaten to what eats it).
- **Confusing bioaccumulation and the water cycle.** Bioaccumulation refers to the build-up of
  toxins; the water cycle is the movement of water.
- **Forgetting that pyramids of energy are always pyramid-shaped** but pyramids of number can be
  inverted.
- **Stating that 90% of energy is "lost" as heat.** Energy is lost through several processes
  (respiration, excretion, movement, etc.), not just heat.
- **Confusing the carbon cycle with the nitrogen cycle.** Focus on the processes that move carbon
  between stores.
