## 1. Cell Structure

### 1.1 Plant and Animal Cells

All living organisms are made of cells. Animal and plant cells share many structures but have key
differences.

| Structure         | Function                                                      | Plant/Animal            |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------- |
| Cell membrane     | Controls what enters and leaves the cell; partially permeable | Both                    |
| Cytoplasm         | Gel-like substance where chemical reactions occur             | Both                    |
| Nucleus           | Contains genetic material (DNA); controls cell activities     | Both                    |
| Mitochondria      | Site of aerobic respiration; produces ATP (energy)            | Both                    |
| Ribosomes         | Site of protein synthesis                                     | Both                    |
| Cell wall         | Made of cellulose; provides structural support                | Plant only              |
| Permanent vacuole | Contains cell sap; helps maintain cell shape                  | Plant only              |
| Chloroplasts      | Site of photosynthesis; contain chlorophyll                   | Plant only (some cells) |

### 1.2 Eukaryotic and Prokaryotic Cells

**Eukaryotic cells** (animals, plants, fungi) have a membrane-bound nucleus and membrane-bound
organelles (mitochondria, chloroplasts).

**Prokaryotic cells** (bacteria) are smaller and have:

- No membrane-bound nucleus (circular DNA is free in the cytoplasm)
- No membrane-bound organelles
- Plasmids (small, extra rings of DNA)
- A cell wall (made of peptidoglycan, not cellulose)
- Flagellum (tail for movement, in some bacteria)

### 1.3 Cell Specialisation

Cells are specialised to perform specific functions:

| Cell Type        | Specialisation                                   | Function                     |
| ---------------- | ------------------------------------------------ | ---------------------------- |
| Sperm cell       | Long tail, many mitochondria, acrosome           | Fertilise egg                |
| Nerve cell       | Long axon, branched dendrites                    | Transmit electrical impulses |
| Muscle cell      | Contain many mitochondria, can contract          | Movement                     |
| Root hair cell   | Elongated projection, large surface area         | Absorb water and minerals    |
| Red blood cell   | Biconcave disc, no nucleus, contains haemoglobin | Transport oxygen             |
| White blood cell | Can change shape, contains enzymes               | Defend against pathogens     |
| Palisade cell    | Many chloroplasts, tall and thin                 | Photosynthesis               |

### 1.4 Required Practical: Microscopy

**Method:**

1. Clip a thin section of onion epidermis onto a slide.
2. Add a drop of iodine solution (stains the cell and makes nuclei visible).
3. Place a cover slip at an angle to avoid air bubbles.
4. Start with the lowest magnification objective lens.
5. Focus using the coarse then fine adjustment.
6. Increase magnification as needed.

**Magnification calculation:**

$$\text{Magnification} = \frac{\text{image size}}{\text{actual size}}$$

**Worked Example.** A cell is observed to be 4.2 mm in a micrograph. The actual size is 0.07 mm.
Calculate the magnification.

$$\text{Magnification} = \frac{4.2}{0.07} = 60$$

## 2. Cell Division

### 2.1 Mitosis

**Mitosis** is cell division that produces two genetically identical daughter cells, each with the
same number of chromosomes as the parent cell. It is used for growth, repair, and asexual
reproduction.

**The cell cycle:**

1. **Interphase:** The cell grows and replicates its DNA. Each chromosome is copied to form two
   identical chromatids joined at the centromere.
2. **Mitosis:** The cell divides. The stages are:
   - **Prophase:** Chromosomes condense and become visible. The nuclear membrane breaks down.
   - **Metaphase:** Chromosomes line up at the centre of the cell.
   - **Anaphase:** Chromatids are pulled apart to opposite poles.
   - **Telophase:** New nuclear membranes form around each set of chromosomes.
3. **Cytokinesis:** The cytoplasm divides, producing two daughter cells.

### 2.2 The Importance of Mitosis

- **Growth:** Multicellular organisms grow by increasing the number of cells.
- **Repair:** Damaged or dead cells are replaced.
- **Asexual reproduction:** Produces genetically identical offspring (clones).

### 2.3 Stem Cells

**Stem cells** are unspecialised cells that can differentiate into specialised cell types.

| Type                 | Source                               | Potential                                         |
| -------------------- | ------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------- |
| Embryonic stem cells | Early embryos                        | Can become any cell type (totipotent/pluripotent) |
| Adult stem cells     | Bone marrow, brain, etc.             | Can become a limited range of cell types          |
| Plant stem cells     | Meristems (tips of shoots and roots) | Can become any plant cell type                    |

**Uses of stem cells:**

- Treating leukaemia (bone marrow transplants)
- Research into regenerative medicine
- Growing replacement tissues or organs
- Drug testing

**Ethical considerations:**

- Embryonic stem cell research involves the destruction of embryos
- Adult stem cells are less controversial but more limited in potential
- Some people believe embryos have the right to life from conception

### 2.4 Cancer

Cancer is the result of uncontrolled cell division. It occurs when mutations in genes that control
the cell cycle cause cells to divide repeatedly without stopping, forming a **tumour**.

- **Benign tumours:** Grow slowly, remain in one place, not usually dangerous
- **Malignant tumours:** Grow rapidly, can spread to other parts of the body (metastasis),
  life-threatening

**Risk factors:** Smoking, UV radiation, ionising radiation, some viruses, certain chemicals,
genetic predisposition.

## 3. Transport Across Cell Membranes

### 3.1 Diffusion

**Diffusion** is the net movement of particles from an area of higher concentration to an area of
lower concentration, down the concentration gradient.

**Key features:**

- Passive process (no energy required)
- Occurs in both liquids and gases
- Rate increases with: larger concentration gradient, higher temperature, larger surface area

**Worked Example.** Oxygen diffuses from the alveoli (high concentration) into the blood (low
concentration). Carbon dioxide diffuses from the blood into the alveoli.

### 3.2 Osmosis

**Osmosis** is the net movement of water molecules across a partially permeable membrane from a
region of higher water potential (dilute solution) to a region of lower water potential
(concentrated solution).

**Key terms:**

- **Hypotonic:** Lower solute concentration than the cell (water enters the cell)
- **Isotonic:** Equal solute concentration (no net movement of water)
- **Hypertonic:** Higher solute concentration than the cell (water leaves the cell)

### 3.3 Required Practical: Osmosis in Potato Chips

**Method:**

1. Cut potato chips of identical size and mass.
2. Place each chip in a beaker with a different concentration of sugar solution (e.g. 0%, 10%, 20%,
   30%).
3. Leave for a set time (e.g. 30 minutes).
4. Remove, dry, and measure the new mass.
5. Calculate the percentage change in mass.

**Expected results:**

- In distilled water (0%): mass increases (water enters by osmosis)
- In concentrated solution (30%): mass decreases (water leaves by osmosis)
- At a specific concentration: no change (isotonic)

### 3.4 Active Transport

**Active transport** is the movement of particles against the concentration gradient (from low to
high concentration). It requires energy from respiration (ATP) and carrier proteins in the membrane.

**Example:** Mineral ions in the soil are absorbed by root hair cells against the concentration
gradient.

## 4. Cell Organisation

### 4.1 Levels of Organisation

$$\text{Cells} \to \text{Tissues} \to \text{Organs} \to \text{Organ Systems} \to \text{Organisms}$$

- **Cell:** The basic unit of life
- **Tissue:** A group of similar cells with the same function
- **Organ:** A group of tissues working together to perform a specific function
- **Organ system:** A group of organs working together
- **Organism:** A living thing made up of various organ systems

### 4.2 Examples in the Human Body

| Level        | Example            |
| ------------ | ------------------ |
| Cell         | Muscle cell        |
| Tissue       | Muscle tissue      |
| Organ        | Heart              |
| Organ system | Circulatory system |
| Organism     | Human              |

## Common Pitfalls

- **Confusing mitosis and meiosis.** Mitosis produces 2 identical daughter cells (growth/repair);
  meiosis produces 4 non-identical gametes (reproduction).
- **Forgetting that osmosis refers to water movement only.** Other substances move by diffusion or
  active transport.
- **Confusing eukaryotic and prokaryotic cells.** Bacteria are prokaryotic (no nucleus, no
  membrane-bound organelles, circular DNA, plasmids).
- **Writing the magnification equation the wrong way round.** Magnification = image size / actual
  size.
- **Stating that diffusion requires energy.** Diffusion is passive; only active transport requires
  energy.
- **Confusing the terms tissue, organ, and organ system.** Make sure you can give specific examples
  at each level.

## Practice Questions

1. Describe three differences between plant and animal cells.

2. Explain how a root hair cell is adapted for its function of absorbing water and mineral ions.

3. Describe the stages of the cell cycle, including what happens during interphase.

4. Explain the ethical issues surrounding the use of embryonic stem cells in medicine.

5. A red blood cell is placed in a very concentrated salt solution. Describe and explain what
   happens to the cell.

6. Calculate the actual size of a cell that measures 8.5 mm in a micrograph taken at a magnification
   of $\times 400$. Give your answer in micrometres ($\mu$m).

7. Explain the difference between diffusion, osmosis, and active transport.

8. Describe how cancer develops and explain two risk factors.

9. Explain why cells need to divide by mitosis rather than simply growing larger.

10. A student sets up an osmosis experiment with potato chips. The results show that chips in 0.2
    mol/dm$^3$ sugar solution increased in mass by 5%, while chips in 0.8 mol/dm$^3$ solution
    decreased in mass by 12%. Explain these results.
