# Chemical Reactions and Bonding

## Higher Chemical Bonding

### Types of Bonding

**Ionic Bonding:**

The electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions formed by electron transfer. Typically
occurs between metals (Groups 1, 2) and non-metals (Groups 6, 7).

**Properties of ionic compounds:**

- High melting and boiling points (strong electrostatic forces)
- Conduct electricity when molten or dissolved (ions are free to move)
- Brittle (repulsion between like charges when layers shift)
- Soluble in polar solvents like water

**Example:** Formation of magnesium oxide.

$$\text{Mg} \to \text{Mg}^{2+} + 2e^-$$ $$\text{O} + 2e^- \to \text{O}^{2-}$$

$$\text{Mg(s)} + \tfrac{1}{2}\text{O}_2\text{(g)} \to \text{MgO(s)}$$

**Covalent Bonding:**

A shared pair of electrons between two atoms. Covalent bonds can be single, double, or triple.

**Properties of covalent substances:**

- Simple molecular: low melting points, do not conduct electricity (e.g., $\text{H}_2\text{O}$,
  $\text{CO}_2$)
- Giant covalent (macromolecular): very high melting points (e.g., diamond, graphite, silicon
  dioxide)

**Metallic Bonding:**

Positive metal ions in a "sea" of delocalised electrons.

**Properties of metals:**

- Good conductors of electricity (delocalised electrons)
- Malleable and ductile (layers of ions can slide)
- High melting points (strong metallic bonding)
- Shiny (delocalised electrons absorb and re-emit light)

### Electronegativity and Bond Polarity

**Electronegativity** is the ability of an atom to attract the bonding electrons towards itself in a
covalent bond.

**Pauling Scale:**

- Fluorine is the most electronegative element (4.0)
- Cesium and francium are the least electronegative (0.7)

**Bond polarity:**

- If the electronegativity difference is 0-0.4: non-polar covalent
- If 0.5-1.7: polar covalent
- If greater than 1.7: ionic

**Dipole moment:** A polar molecule has an overall dipole moment, which is the vector sum of
individual bond dipoles.

**Example:** $\text{H}_2\text{O}$ is a polar molecule because the bond dipoles do not cancel (bent
shape). $\text{CO}_2$ is non-polar because the bond dipoles cancel (linear shape).

### Molecular Shape (VSEPR Theory)

The Valence Shell Electron Pair Repulsion (VSEPR) theory predicts molecular shapes based on electron
pair repulsion.

| Electron Pairs | Shape                | Bond Angle | Example         |
| -------------- | -------------------- | ---------- | --------------- |
| 2              | Linear               | 180°       | $\text{BeCl}_2$ |
| 3              | Trigonal planar      | 120°       | $\text{BF}_3$   |
| 4              | Tetrahedral          | 109.5°     | $\text{CH}_4$   |
| 5              | Trigonal bipyramidal | 90°, 120°  | $\text{PCl}_5$  |
| 6              | Octahedral           | 90°        | $\text{SF}_6$   |

**Lone pairs** occupy more space than bonding pairs, reducing bond angles:

| Bonding/Lone Pairs | Shape              | Bond Angle | Example              |
| ------------------ | ------------------ | ---------- | -------------------- |
| 3 bonding, 1 lone  | Trigonal pyramidal | 107°       | $\text{NH}_3$        |
| 2 bonding, 2 lone  | Bent               | 104.5°     | $\text{H}_2\text{O}$ |

### Intermolecular Forces

**Van der Waals (London dispersion) forces:**

- Weakest intermolecular force
- Caused by temporary dipoles due to electron movement
- Increase with molecular size (number of electrons)
- Present in all molecules

**Permanent dipole-dipole forces:**

- Between polar molecules
- Stronger than London forces
- Examples: $\text{HCl}$, $\text{SO}_2$

**Hydrogen bonding:**

- Strongest type of intermolecular force
- Occurs when hydrogen is bonded to a highly electronegative atom (F, O, or N)
- Responsible for the anomalously high boiling point of water

**Example:** Explain why $\text{H}_2\text{O}$ has a higher boiling point than $\text{H}_2\text{S}$
despite $\text{H}_2\text{S}$ having a larger molar mass.

$\text{H}_2\text{O}$ can form hydrogen bonds between molecules (H bonded to O), which are much
stronger than the dipole-dipole forces in $\text{H}_2\text{S}$. Although $\text{H}_2\text{S}$ has
stronger London forces due to more electrons, hydrogen bonding in water dominates.

### Bond Enthalpy

**Bond enthalpy** is the energy required to break one mole of a particular bond in the gaseous state
(endothermic).

**Average bond enthalpies** are used because bond enthalpies vary slightly depending on the
molecular environment.

| Bond | Enthalpy (kJ/mol) |
| ---- | ----------------- |
| C-C  | 348               |
| C=C  | 612               |
| C-H  | 412               |
| O-H  | 463               |
| C=O  | 743               |
| H-H  | 436               |
| O=O  | 496               |

**Example:** Estimate the enthalpy change for the reaction:
$\text{CH}_4 + 2\text{O}_2 \to \text{CO}_2 + 2\text{H}_2\text{O}$.

Bonds broken:
$4 \times \text{C-H} + 2 \times \text{O=O} = 4(412) + 2(496) = 1648 + 992 = 2640 \text{ kJ/mol}$.

Bonds formed:
$2 \times \text{C=O} + 4 \times \text{O-H} = 2(743) + 4(463) = 1486 + 1852 = 3338 \text{ kJ/mol}$.

$$\Delta H = 2640 - 3338 = -698 \text{ kJ/mol}$$

## Chemical Reactions

### Balancing Equations

**Example:** Balance the equation for the combustion of propane.

$$\text{C}_3\text{H}_8 + 5\text{O}_2 \to 3\text{CO}_2 + 4\text{H}_2\text{O}$$

### Ionic Equations

Only include species that actually change during the reaction (spectator ions are omitted).

**Example:** Write the ionic equation for the reaction of silver nitrate with sodium chloride.

$$\text{Ag}^+(aq) + \text{NO}_3^-(aq) + \text{Na}^+(aq) + \text{Cl}^-(aq) \to \text{AgCl(s)} + \text{Na}^+(aq) + \text{NO}_3^-(aq)$$

Net ionic equation: $\text{Ag}^+(aq) + \text{Cl}^-(aq) \to \text{AgCl(s)}$

### Oxidation and Reduction

**Oxidation:** Loss of electrons (increase in oxidation state).

**Reduction:** Gain of electrons (decrease in oxidation state).

**OIL RIG:** Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain.

**Oxidation states rules:**

1. Elements in their standard state: 0
2. Monatomic ions: equal to the charge
3. Oxygen: usually $-2$ (except in peroxides, $-1$)
4. Hydrogen: usually $+1$ (except in metal hydrides, $-1$)
5. Sum of oxidation states equals the overall charge

**Example:** Determine the oxidation states in $\text{KMnO}_4$.

$\text{K} = +1$, $\text{O} = -2$, so $\text{Mn} + 1 + 4(-2) = 0$, giving $\text{Mn} = +7$.

### Redox Reactions

**Example:** Balance the reaction of $\text{MnO}_4^-$ with $\text{Fe}^{2+}$ in acidic solution.

Oxidation: $\text{Fe}^{2+} \to \text{Fe}^{3+} + e^-$

Reduction: $\text{MnO}_4^- + 8\text{H}^+ + 5e^- \to \text{Mn}^{2+} + 4\text{H}_2\text{O}$

Multiply oxidation by 5:

$$5\text{Fe}^{2+} + \text{MnO}_4^- + 8\text{H}^+ \to 5\text{Fe}^{3+} + \text{Mn}^{2+} + 4\text{H}_2\text{O}$$

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Confusing ionic and covalent bonding:** Ionic bonding involves electron transfer; covalent
   involves electron sharing.

2. **VSEPR with lone pairs:** Lone pairs repel more strongly than bonding pairs, reducing bond
   angles below the ideal values.

3. **Bond polarity vs. molecular polarity:** A molecule can have polar bonds but be non-polar
   overall (e.g., $\text{CCl}_4$, $\text{CO}_2$).

4. **Using average bond enthalpies:** These give approximate values only. They are less accurate
   when bonds are in different molecular environments.

5. **Oxidation state of oxygen in peroxides:** In $\text{H}_2\text{O}_2$, oxygen has oxidation state
   $-1$, not $-2$.

## Practice Questions

1. Draw dot-and-cross diagrams for (a) $\text{MgCl}_2$ and (b) $\text{H}_2\text{S}$. State the type
   of bonding in each.

2. Explain why the boiling point of $\text{NH}_3$ (240 K) is higher than that of $\text{PH}_3$ (185
   K), despite $\text{PH}_3$ having more electrons.

3. Predict the shapes and bond angles of $\text{ClF}_3$ and $\text{XeF}_4$.

4. Using bond enthalpies, estimate $\Delta H$ for the hydrogenation of ethene:
   $\text{C}_2\text{H}_4 + \text{H}_2 \to \text{C}_2\text{H}_6$.

5. Balance the following redox equation in acidic solution:
   $\text{Cr}_2\text{O}_7^{2-} + \text{SO}_2 \to \text{Cr}^{3+} + \text{SO}_4^{2-}$.

6. Explain why graphite conducts electricity but diamond does not.

7. Determine the oxidation state of chromium in $\text{Na}_2\text{Cr}_2\text{O}_7$.

8. Compare and contrast the properties of metallic and ionic bonding, explaining the differences in
   terms of structure.
