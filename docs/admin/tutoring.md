## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Booking an IB Mathematics HL Session

**Problem:** A student needs help with integration by parts for an upcoming IB Math HL exam.
**Solution:** Navigate to the booking page, select "IB Mathematics (HL)" as the subject, choose a
convenient time slot, and note the specific topic ("integration by parts and reduction formulae") in
the booking notes so the tutor can prepare targeted materials.

### Example 2: Choosing Between Individual and Group Sessions

**Problem:** Two students in the same class want help with DSE Physics mechanics. **Solution:** Book
a group session (2-3 students) for a lower per-student rate. Both students benefit from shared
problem-solving and discussion. If the students are at different levels or studying different
topics, book individual sessions instead.

## Scheduling Workflow

1. Student submits a booking request via the booking link, specifying subject, level (HL/SL), and
   specific topic.
2. Tutor confirms availability within 12 hours and sends a calendar invitation with a video call
   link.
3. Student accepts the invitation. Both parties receive reminders 24 hours and 1 hour before the
   session.
4. If the student needs to reschedule, they must notify the tutor at least 12 hours before the
   session start time (see Cancellation Policy below).

## Session Preparation Checklist

For each session, the tutor should:

1. Review the student's syllabus progress to date.
2. Identify the specific sub-topics to cover based on the booking notes.
3. Prepare 3-5 worked examples that increase in difficulty.
4. Prepare 5-10 practice questions for the student to attempt during the session.
5. Have relevant WyattsNotes pages open or bookmarked for quick reference.
6. Check whether the student has an upcoming internal assessment or mock exam that affects priority.

## Student Progress Tracking

Tutors maintain a progress record for each student:

- **Topic mastery grid**: A table mapping syllabus topics to a confidence rating (1-5) as reported
  by the student and observed by the tutor.
- **Error log**: A running list of recurring mistakes the student makes (e.g., sign errors in
  algebra, missing units, incorrect free-body diagrams). Reviewing this log at the start of each
  session reinforces correction.
- **Session summary**: After each session, the tutor writes a brief summary (2-3 sentences) covering
  what was covered, what the student struggled with, and what to prioritise next. This is shared
  with the student after the session.

The topic mastery grid is updated monthly and shared with the student so they can see their progress
quantitatively.

## Subject-Specific Approaches

### IB Mathematics

- Focus on the internal assessment (IA) early: topic selection, data collection plan, and structure.
  Many students lose marks on poor IA planning rather than mathematical content.
- For Paper 2 and Paper 3, practise extended-response questions under timed conditions. Students
  often run out of time on multi-part questions.
- Emphasise the GDC proficiency required for Papers 2 and 3: solving equations numerically, plotting
  functions, and using statistics menus.

### IB Physics and Chemistry

- Prioritise the data-based and practical questions in Paper 3. These require specific skills
  (uncertainty propagation, graph linearisation) that students often under-practise.
- For chemistry, ensure nomenclature, balancing equations, and stoichiometry are fluent before
  tackling more advanced topics. Weakness in fundamentals cascades through the entire syllabus.

### A-Level and DSE

- DSE papers are heavily formula-driven. Ensure students know which formulae are given and which
  must be memorised.
- A-Level synoptic questions require integration across modules. Schedule periodic mixed-topic
  sessions in the final two months before exams.

## Handling Common Student Difficulties

- **Algebraic manipulation errors**: If a student consistently makes sign errors or cannot rearrange
  equations fluently, dedicate part of each session to a short algebra drill. Weak algebra
  undermines every topic.
- **Unable to start a problem**: Teach a structured approach: identify given quantities, identify
  the target quantity, write down relevant formulae, then substitute. Students who freeze on
  blank-paper questions benefit from this scaffolding.
- **Over-reliance on memorisation**: For subjects like physics, emphasise derivations over
  memorisation. If a student can derive a formula from first principles, they are less likely to
  misapply it under exam pressure.

## Feedback Framework

After each session, the tutor provides feedback using a structured format:

1. **What went well**: Specific skills or topics the student demonstrated competence in.
2. **Areas for improvement**: Concrete topics or error patterns that need attention.
3. **Next steps**: 1-2 specific actions for the student to take before the next session (e.g.,
   "complete 10 projectile motion problems from Chapter 3, focusing on resolving vectors").

This framework keeps feedback actionable and avoids vague encouragement.

## Cancellation Policy

- Cancellations made more than 12 hours before the session: no charge. The session is rescheduled at
  mutual convenience.
- Cancellations made less than 12 hours before the session: the session is charged at 50% of the
  full rate.
- No-shows (no notification): the session is charged at the full rate.
- Tutor cancellations: if the tutor cancels less than 12 hours before, the next session is
  discounted by 50%.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Not specifying the topic when booking:** Tutors prepare differently for calculus, mechanics, or
  probability problems. Always state the specific topic or syllabus area in the booking notes.
- **Assuming all tutors cover all boards:** Tutors specialise in specific qualification boards (IB,
  A-Level, DSE). Confirm the tutor's board expertise before booking.
- **Waiting until the last minute:** Booking 24-48 hours in advance gives the tutor time to prepare.
  Last-minute bookings limit preparation quality.
- **Inconsistent attendance:** Sporadic sessions are less effective than a regular weekly schedule.
  Students who attend irregularly lose momentum and spend part of each session re-covering material.
