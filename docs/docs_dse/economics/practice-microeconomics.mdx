### Demand and Supply

const practiceQuestions = [ { question: "Which of the following will cause a movement along the
demand curve for rice?", options: [ "A) A decrease in the price of wheat (a substitute)", "B) A rise
in consumers' income", "C) An increase in the price of rice", "D) A change in consumers' taste in
favour of rice" ], correct: 2, explanation: "A movement along the demand curve is caused only by a
change in the price of the good itself (ceteris paribus). An increase in the price of rice causes a
contraction (decrease) in quantity demanded, shown as an upward movement along the demand curve.
Changes in other factors (income, price of substitutes, tastes) shift the entire demand curve." }, {
question: "If the demand for good X increases and the supply of good X decreases simultaneously,
what happens to the equilibrium price and quantity?", options: [ "A) Both price and quantity
increase", "B) Price increases; quantity change is indeterminate", "C) Both price and quantity
decrease", "D) Price change is indeterminate; quantity decreases" ], correct: 1, explanation: "An
increase in demand raises both equilibrium price and quantity. A decrease in supply raises
equilibrium price but reduces equilibrium quantity. The combined effect on price is unambiguously an
increase (both forces push price up). The effect on quantity is indeterminate because the two forces
work in opposite directions." }, { question: "The law of supply states that, ceteris paribus:",
options: [ "A) As price falls, quantity supplied falls", "B) As price rises, supply shifts to the
right", "C) As quantity demanded increases, price increases", "D) Supply always equals demand at
equilibrium" ], correct: 0, explanation: "The law of supply states that there is a positive
relationship between price and quantity supplied, ceteris paribus. When price rises, quantity
supplied rises (expansion of supply); when price falls, quantity supplied falls (contraction of
supply). This is represented by a movement along the supply curve, not a shift." }, { question: "A
surplus occurs in a market when:", options: [ "A) The market price is below the equilibrium price",
"B) Quantity demanded exceeds quantity supplied at the prevailing price", "C) Quantity supplied

equilibrium" ], correct: 2, explanation: "A surplus exists when quantity supplied exceeds quantity
demanded at the current market price. This typically occurs when the market price is set above the
equilibrium price. The surplus creates downward pressure on price as sellers compete to sell their
excess stock, pushing the price back towards equilibrium." } ];

### Elasticity

const practiceQuestions = [ { question: "The price elasticity of demand (PED) for a good is -0.3.
This means demand is:", options: [ "A) Perfectly elastic", "B) Relatively inelastic (price
inelastic)", "C) Unit elastic", "D) Relatively elastic (price elastic)" ], correct: 1, explanation:
"PED is calculated as the percentage change in quantity demanded divided by the percentage change in
price. A PED of -0.3 has an absolute value less than 1, meaning demand is relatively inelastic
(price inelastic). A change in price leads to a proportionately smaller change in quantity demanded.
Necessities tend to have inelastic demand." }, { question: "If the cross elasticity of demand (XED)
between good X and good Y is +2.5, then X and Y are:", options: [ "A) Complementary goods", "B)
Inferior goods", "C) Substitute goods", "D) Unrelated goods" ], correct: 2, explanation: "A positive
XED indicates that the two goods are substitutes -- when the price of one rises, demand for the
other increases. An XED greater than 1 (in absolute value) means they are close substitutes. A
negative XED would indicate complements (e.g., coffee and sugar). An XED of approximately zero means
the goods are unrelated." }, { question: "If the income elasticity of demand (YED) for a good is
-0.8, the good is:", options: [ "A) A normal good", "B) A luxury good", "C) An inferior good", "D) A
Giffen good" ], correct: 2, explanation: "A negative YED indicates an inferior good: as income
increases, demand for the good decreases. Consumers switch to higher-quality alternatives as they
become wealthier. A YED between 0 and 1 indicates a normal necessity, while a YED greater than 1
indicates a luxury good. Giffen goods are a special theoretical case of inferior goods with an
upward-sloping demand curve." }, { question: "The price elasticity of supply (PES) for fresh
vegetables is likely to be:", options: [ "A) Perfectly elastic in the short run", "B) Relatively
inelastic in the short run due to the growing period", "C) Unit elastic regardless of time period",
"D) Perfectly inelastic in both the short and long run" ], correct: 1, explanation: "Fresh
vegetables have a relatively inelastic supply in the short run because the growing period means
producers cannot quickly increase output in response to a price change. Supply becomes more elastic
in the long run as farmers can plant more crops or switch to higher-price varieties. Generally, PES
is higher the longer the time period considered." } ];
