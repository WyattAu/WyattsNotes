### Modern China

<PracticeProblem question="Which event marked the beginning of the Chinese Communist Party's rise to power in rural China?" options={[ 'The May Fourth Movement (1919)', 'The Long March (1934-1935)', 'The establishment of the PRC (1949)', 'The Hundred Days Reform (1898)', ]} correctAnswer={1} explanation="The Long March (1934-1935) was pivotal for the CCP. Although it began as a military retreat from Nationalist forces, it allowed Mao Zedong to consolidate leadership of the Party and established the Yan'an base from which the CCP conducted guerrilla warfare against Japan and eventually won the Chinese Civil War. The May Fourth Movement preceded the CCP's founding. The PRC establishment was the outcome, not the beginning of the rise." difficulty="easy" />

<PracticeProblem question="The Great Leap Forward (1958-1961) primarily aimed to achieve which objective?" options={[ 'Strengthen diplomatic relations with the Soviet Union', 'Rapidly transform China into an industrialised communist state', 'Introduce capitalist market reforms in rural areas', 'Expand China's territorial control in Southeast Asia', ]} correctAnswer={1} explanation="The Great Leap Forward was Mao Zedong's programme to rapidly industrialise China by mobilising the population into communes and establishing backyard steel furnaces. The goal was to catch up with Western industrial output within a short timeframe. The policy was a catastrophic failure, causing widespread famine and an estimated 15-45 million deaths, making it one of the deadliest famines in history." difficulty="easy" />

<PracticeProblem question="Deng Xiaoping's Household Responsibility System replaced collective farming. What was its main effect on agricultural production?" options={[ 'Agricultural output declined as farmers lacked modern equipment', 'Farmers were given individual plots and production incentives, leading to increased output', 'The government took direct control of all agricultural decisions', 'Agricultural land was redistributed to foreign corporations', ]} correctAnswer={1} explanation="Under the Household Responsibility System, collectives were dissolved and land was contracted to individual households. Farmers could keep or sell surplus production after meeting state quotas, creating economic incentives. This dramatically increased agricultural productivity, ended the chronic food shortages of the Mao era, and became a cornerstone of China's economic reforms." difficulty="medium" />

### Cold War and Decolonisation

<PracticeProblem question="Which of the following best describes the policy of containment during the Cold War?" options={[ 'The Soviet policy of isolating satellite states from Western influence', 'The US strategy to prevent the spread of communism beyond its existing boundaries', 'A mutual agreement between the US and USSR to avoid direct military conflict', 'The European strategy of maintaining colonial empires against independence movements', ]} correctAnswer={1} explanation="Containment was the US foreign policy doctrine, first articulated by George Kennan in 1947, which aimed to prevent the geographical expansion of Soviet communism. It was implemented through the Truman Doctrine (military and economic aid to Greece and Turkey), the Marshall Plan (economic aid to Western Europe), NATO, and military interventions in Korea and Vietnam. The goal was not to roll back existing communist regimes but to prevent further expansion." difficulty="medium" />

<PracticeProblem question="The Non-Aligned Movement (NAM), established in 1961, was primarily a response to which situation?" options={[ 'The economic dominance of the United States over Western Europe', 'The pressure on newly independent countries to choose sides in the Cold War', 'The failure of the United Nations to prevent military conflicts', 'The environmental degradation caused by industrialised nations', ]} correctAnswer={1} explanation="The Non-Aligned Movement was formed by leaders of newly independent countries (Nehru, Tito, Nasser, Sukarno, Nkrumah) who did not wish to align with either the US-led Western bloc or the Soviet-led Eastern bloc. It was a direct response to the bipolar Cold War system that pressured developing nations to choose between capitalism and communism. NAM advocated for peaceful coexistence, anti-colonialism, and economic development independent of superpower control." difficulty="medium" />

<PracticeProblem question="Which of the following was a significant consequence of the Suez Crisis (1956) for Britain and France?" options={[ 'It strengthened their control over Middle Eastern oil resources', 'It exposed their declining global influence and accelerated decolonisation', 'It led to a military alliance between Britain, France, and the Soviet Union', 'It resulted in the creation of the state of Israel', ]} correctAnswer={1} explanation="The Suez Crisis was a major humiliation for Britain and France. Their military intervention (with Israel) to seize the Suez Canal after Egypt's Nasser nationalised it was forced to end under pressure from both the US and the USSR. The crisis exposed the inability of Britain and France to act independently of the superpowers, accelerated the process of decolonisation (especially British withdrawal from the Middle East and Africa), and marked a decisive shift in global power from European empires to the American and Soviet superpowers." difficulty="medium" />

<PracticeProblem question="Japan's rapid industrialisation during the Meiji Restoration was facilitated by which factor?" options={[ 'Abundant natural resources, particularly coal and iron ore', 'Strong democratic institutions established before modernisation', 'Deliberate government policy promoting Western technology, education, and industry', 'Foreign direct investment from European colonial powers', ]} correctAnswer={2} explanation="The Meiji government played the central role in Japan's industrialisation through deliberate policies: sending students abroad to learn Western technology, hiring foreign experts, establishing government model factories, building railways and telegraph networks, reforming the education system, and modernising the military. Japan had limited natural resources and democracy was limited under the Meiji Constitution. While foreign investment played some role, the primary driver was state-directed modernisation." difficulty="medium" />

<PracticeProblem question="The domino theory, used to justify US involvement in Vietnam, was based on which assumption?" options={[ 'Vietnam had vast oil reserves critical to US energy security', 'If one Southeast Asian country fell to communism, neighbouring countries would follow', 'The Soviet Union had placed nuclear weapons in Vietnam', 'Vietnam was the last remaining democratic state in Asia', ]} correctAnswer={1} explanation="The domino theory, popularised by President Eisenhower in 1954, held that if one country in a region fell to communism, neighbouring countries would follow like falling dominoes. This was the primary rationale for US escalation in Vietnam -- preventing the fall of South Vietnam was seen as essential to containing communism throughout Southeast Asia. The theory proved overly simplistic, as post-war Southeast Asia did not universally adopt communism." difficulty="medium" />

### Hong Kong and Regional History

<PracticeProblem question="The Sino-British Joint Declaration (1984) established which principle for Hong Kong's governance after 1997?" options={[ 'Full integration into the Chinese political and legal system', 'One Country, Two Systems, preserving Hong Kong's capitalist system for 50 years', 'Independence from both China and Britain as a sovereign city-state', 'Governance by an international coalition of Western powers', ]} correctAnswer={1} explanation="The Sino-British Joint Declaration established the 'One Country, Two Systems' principle, under which Hong Kong would retain its capitalist economic system, common law legal system, and way of life for 50 years after the 1997 handover. Hong Kong would become a Special Administrative Region (SAR) with a high degree of autonomy in all matters except foreign affairs and defence. The Basic Law would serve as Hong Kong's constitutional document." difficulty="medium" />

<PracticeProblem question="During the Japanese occupation of Hong Kong (1941-1945), which of the following had the most severe impact on the civilian population?" options={[ 'The destruction of the harbour and commercial district', 'Widespread food shortages, hyperinflation, and forced labour', 'The imposition of Japanese language education in schools', 'The withdrawal of British military forces', ]} correctAnswer={1} explanation="While all listed effects occurred during the occupation, widespread food shortages and hyperinflation had the most devastating impact on civilians. Rationing was severe and insufficient, hyperinflation rendered savings worthless, forced labour programmes exploited the population, and an estimated 10,000-50,000 civilians died, primarily from starvation and disease. The occupation created lasting economic and social trauma that shaped post-war Hong Kong." difficulty="hard" />
