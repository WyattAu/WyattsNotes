## A Level Economics — Course Overview

A Level Economics is the study of how societies allocate scarce resources. It develops a rigorous
analytical framework for understanding markets, firms, governments, and the global economy. The
course requires both mathematical fluency and the ability to construct clear, logical arguments.

### Course Structure

#### Microeconomics

The study of individual economic agents — consumers, firms, and markets:

1. **Methodology & the Economic Problem** — Scarcity, choice, opportunity cost, positive vs
   normative economics
2. **Demand, Supply & Market Equilibrium** — The price mechanism, elasticity, and market adjustment
3. **Market Failure & Government Intervention** — Externalities, public goods, information
   asymmetry, and policy responses
4. **Theory of the Firm** — Production, costs, market structures (perfect competition, monopoly,
   oligopoly, monopolistic competition)
5. **Labour Markets** — Demand for and supply of labour, wage determination, trade unions,
   discrimination
6. **Distribution of Income & Wealth** — Inequality measurement, poverty, tax/transfer policies

#### Macroeconomics

The study of the economy as a whole:

1. **Macroeconomic Performance** — GDP, inflation, unemployment, the balance of payments
2. **Aggregate Demand & Aggregate Supply** — The AD/AS model, multipliers, and the price level
3. **The Financial Sector** — Banks, interest rates, monetary policy, the money supply
4. **Fiscal Policy** — Government spending, taxation, budget deficits, and the national debt
5. **Supply-Side Policy** — Productivity, labour market reforms, competition policy
6. **The International Economy** — Trade theory, exchange rates, globalisation, economic development
7. **Macroeconomic Policy Debates** — Monetarism vs Keynesianism, rules vs discretion

## Key Formulae Reference

### Microeconomics

**Elasticities:**
$$PED = \frac{\% \Delta Q_D}{\% \Delta P} = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta P} \times \frac{P}{Q}$$
$$PES = \frac{\% \Delta Q_S}{\% \Delta P} = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta P} \times \frac{P}{Q}$$
$$XED = \frac{\% \Delta Q_A}{\% \Delta P_B}$$
$$YED = \frac{\% \Delta Q_D}{\% \Delta Y}$$

**Welfare:**
$$CS = \int_0^Q [P_D(Q) - P^*] \, dQ = \frac{1}{2}(P_{choke} - P^*) \times Q^*$$
$$PS = \int_0^Q [P^* - P_S(Q)] \, dQ = \frac{1}{2}(P^* - P_{choke\_supply}) \times Q^*$$
$$DWL_{tax} = \frac{1}{2} \times t \times \Delta Q$$
$$DWL_{externality} = \frac{1}{2} \times MEC \times (Q_{mkt} - Q^*)$$

**Tax incidence:**
$$\text{'\{'}Consumer burden{'\}'} = \frac{PES}{|PED| + PES} \times t$$
$$\text{'\{'}Producer burden{'\}'} = \frac{|PED|}{|PED| + PES} \times t$$

**Firm behaviour:**
$$\pi = TR - TC = P \times Q - TC(Q)$$
Profit max: $MR = MC$
$$MC = \frac{dTC}{dQ}, \quad MR = \frac{dTR}{dQ}$$
Shut-down: $P < AVC_{min}$

**Monopsony:**
$$MFCL = w + L \times \frac{dw}{dL}$$
Profit max: $MFCL = MRPL$

### Macroeconomics

**National income:**
$$GDP = C + I + G + (X - M)$$
$$\text{'\{'}Real GDP{'\}'} = \frac{\text{'\{'}Nominal GDP{'\}'}}{\text{'\{'}GDP Deflator{'\}'}} \times 100$$
$$g = \frac{Y_t - Y_{t-1}}{Y_{t-1}} \times 100$$

**Inflation:**
$$\pi_t = \frac{CPI_t - CPI_{t-1}}{CPI_{t-1}} \times 100$$

**Unemployment:**
$$u = \frac{U}{LF} \times 100 = \frac{U}{U + E} \times 100$$

**Multiplier:**
$$k = \frac{1}{1 - MPC(1-t) + MPM}$$
$$k_T = \frac{-MPC}{1 - MPC(1-t) + MPM}$$

**Phillips curve:**
$$\pi = \pi^e - \beta(u - u^*)$$

**Okun's Law:**
$$\frac{Y - Y^*}{Y^*} = -\beta(u - u^*)$$

**Debt dynamics:**
$$\Delta b = (r - g_{nominal})b - p$$

**Solow model:**
$$y = k^\alpha$$
$$\dot{k} = sy - (n + \delta)k = 0 \Rightarrow k^* = \left(\frac{s}{n + \delta}\right)^{1/(1-\alpha)}$$
$$g_Y = g_A + \alpha g_K + (1-\alpha)g_L$$

**Exchange rate:**
$$\text{'\{'}Real exchange rate{'\}'} = \frac{e \times P_{domestic}}{P_{foreign}}$$

**Marshall-Lerner condition:**
$$|PED_X| + |PED_M| > 1$$

**Taylor Rule:**
$$i = r^* + \pi^* + 0.5(\pi - \pi^*) + 0.5(y - y^*)$$

## Exam Technique: Command Words

| Command Word | What You Must Do |
|---|---|
| Analyse | Break down the topic, explain causes and mechanisms, use diagrams |
| Assess | Weigh up the evidence, consider different viewpoints, come to a judgement |
| Compare | Identify similarities AND differences |
| Contrast | Identify differences only |
| Define | Give the precise meaning of a term |
| Discuss | Examine different perspectives, consider arguments for and against |
| Evaluate | Make a judgement about the effectiveness or validity, supported by evidence |
| Explain | Set out causes, reasons, or mechanisms |
| Justify | Provide reasons or evidence to support an argument |
| Outline | Give a brief account (less detail than "explain") |
| Suggest | Propose a hypothesis or explanation (for questions with insufficient information) |

## Data Interpretation Skills

When presented with economic data in an exam:

1. **Trends:** Identify the direction of change (rising, falling, stable) and the rate of change (accelerating, decelerating).
2. **Comparisons:** Compare across groups (countries, regions, time periods). Use phrases like "X is twice Y" or "X grew 3 times faster than Y".
3. **Context:** Relate the data to economic theory. For example, if unemployment is rising and inflation is falling, this is consistent with movement along a Phillips curve.
4. **Significance:** Assess whether the data shows a statistically meaningful change or a normal fluctuation.
5. **Limitations:** Note what the data does NOT show. For example, GDP per capita does not show income distribution.

## Common Exam Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Confusing correlation with causation:** Just because two variables move together does not mean one causes the other. Always consider reverse causality and omitted variable bias.

2. **Making claims without evidence:** Every analytical point should be supported by a diagram, a real-world example, or a data reference.

3. **Ignoring the other side:** Evaluation questions require you to consider both arguments for and against. Failing to do so limits your mark to the analysis level (typically 50-60% of the available marks).

4. **Vague evaluation:** "It depends on many factors" is not evaluation. Specific evaluation: "This policy is more effective in a recession than at full employment because the multiplier is larger when there is spare capacity."

5. **Incorrect diagram labels:** Always label the axes (Price/Cost on vertical, Quantity/Output on horizontal), the curves (D, S, MC, MR, etc.), and the key equilibrium points and areas.

6. **Not reading the question carefully:** If the question asks about "the impact on the CURRENT ACCOUNT", do not write about the BUDGET DEFICIT. If it asks about "the SHORT RUN", do not discuss long-run effects unless explicitly asked.

## Study Plan: 8-Week Revision Schedule

### Week 1-2: Microeconomics Foundation
- The Economic Problem (scarcity, PPF, opportunity cost)
- Demand, Supply, and Equilibrium (elasticity, surplus, intervention)
- Practice: draw PPF diagrams, calculate all four elasticities, analyse tax/subsidy/price control

### Week 3-4: Market Failure and Firm Behaviour
- Market Failure (externalities, public goods, information failure, government failure)
- Theory of the Firm (all market structures, efficiency, regulation)
- Practice: calculate DWL, draw MR/MC diagrams, analyse game theory matrices

### Week 5-6: Macroeconomics Core
- Macroeconomic Performance (GDP, inflation, unemployment, balance of payments)
- AD/AS model (multiplier, shifts, output gaps)
- The Financial Sector (money multiplier, monetary policy transmission, QE)
- Fiscal Policy (multipliers, automatic stabilisers, debt dynamics)
- Practice: calculate equilibrium, derive the multiplier, analyse fiscal rules

### Week 7-8: Applied and Synoptic
- Supply-Side Policy (market-oriented vs interventionist)
- International Economy (comparative advantage, exchange rates, trade blocs)
- Policy Debates (Keynesian vs monetarist, Phillips curve, Taylor Rule)
- Labour Markets (monopsony, trade unions, discrimination)
- Development Economics (Solow model, poverty traps, trade vs aid)
- Practice: full 25-mark essays under timed conditions, complete diagnostic tests

### Each Week: 3-4 Hours Total
- 2 hours: review notes, work through examples, attempt practice questions
- 1 hour: complete diagnostic test questions (marked against solutions)
- 30 minutes: review weak areas identified by diagnostics
- 30 minutes: practice drawing key diagrams from memory

### Final Week (before exam): Consolidation
- Complete all 4 diagnostic tests under timed conditions
- Review the Common Exam Mistakes section
- Practice 4-6 timed essays on the most likely synoptic topics
- Memorise key statistics, elasticity estimates, and economist quotes
- Review the formula reference sheet
