### Homomorphisms and Rings

<PracticeProblem
  question="If $\varphi: G \to H$ is a group homomorphism with $|\ker(\varphi)| = 3$ and $|G| = 15$, what is $|\mathrm{im}(\varphi)|$?"
  options={['5', '3', '12', '15']}
  correctAnswer={0}
  explanation="By the first isomorphism theorem, $G/\ker(\varphi) \cong \mathrm{im}(\varphi)$. So $|\mathrm{im}(\varphi)| = |G|/|\ker(\varphi)| = 15/3 = 5$. The image is a subgroup of $H$ of order 5, which is cyclic."
  difficulty="medium"
/>

<PracticeProblem
  question="In the ring $\mathbb{Z}[x]$, which of the following is a prime ideal?"
  options={['$(2)$', '$(4)$', '$(x^2 + 1)$', '$(2x)$']}
  correctAnswer={0}
  explanation="$(2) = \{2f(x) : f(x) \in \mathbb{Z}[x]\}$ is prime because $\mathbb{Z}[x]/(2) \cong \mathbb{Z}_2[x]$, which is an integral domain (since $\mathbb{Z}_2$ is a field). $(4)$ is not prime: $2 \cdot 2 = 4 \in (4)$ but $2 \notin (4)$. $(2x)$ is not prime: $2 \cdot x = 2x \in (2x)$ but neither $2$ nor $x$ is in $(2x)$. $(x^2+1)$ is prime in $\mathbb{Q}[x]$ but not in $\mathbb{Z}[x]$ because $\mathbb{Z}[x]/(x^2+1) \cong \mathbb{Z}[i]$, which is not an integral domain when considered over $\mathbb{Z}$."
  difficulty="hard"
/>

### Fields and Group Actions

<PracticeProblem
  question="What is the degree of the field extension $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3})$ over $\mathbb{Q}$?"
  options={['4', '2', '6', '8']}
  correctAnswer={0}
  explanation="$[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2},\sqrt{3}) : \mathbb{Q}] = [\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2},\sqrt{3}):\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})] \cdot [\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}):\mathbb{Q}]$. The minimal polynomial of $\sqrt{2}$ over $\mathbb{Q}$ is $x^2 - 2$, giving $[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}):\mathbb{Q}] = 2$. The minimal polynomial of $\sqrt{3}$ over $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})$ is $x^2 - 3$ (since $\sqrt{3} \notin \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})$), so $[\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2},\sqrt{3}):\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})] = 2$. Total degree: $2 \cdot 2 = 4$. A basis is $\{1, \sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3}, \sqrt{6}\}$."
  difficulty="medium"
/>

<PracticeProblem
  question="Let $G$ act on the set $X = \{1, 2, 3, 4\}$ via cyclic permutations. What is the size of the orbit of the element 1?"
  options={['4', '2', '1', '3']}
  correctAnswer={0}
  explanation="The cyclic group of order 4 generated by $(1\;2\;3\;4)$ acts transitively on $X$. The orbit of 1 is $\{g \cdot 1 : g \in G\} = \{1, 2, 3, 4\}$, so $|\mathrm{Orb}(1)| = 4 = |X|$. The orbit-stabilizer theorem confirms: $|\mathrm{Stab}(1)| = |G|/|\mathrm{Orb}(1)| = 4/4 = 1$ (only the identity fixes 1)."
