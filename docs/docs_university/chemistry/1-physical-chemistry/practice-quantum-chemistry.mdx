## The Schrodinger Equation and Model Systems

<PracticeProblem
  question="For a particle of mass $m$ in a one-dimensional box of length $L$, the energy levels are $E_n = \\frac{n^2 h^2}{8mL^2}$. What is the energy of the ground state ($n=1$)?"
  options={[
    '$E_1 = \\frac{h^2}{8mL^2}$',
    '$E_1 = \\frac{2h^2}{8mL^2}$',
    '$E_1 = \\frac{h^2}{4mL^2}$',
    '$E_1 = 0$',
  ]}
  correctAnswer={0}
  explanation"The particle in a 1D box has $V = 0$ inside and $V = \\infty$ outside. Solving $-\\frac{\\hbar^2}{2m}\\frac{d^2\\psi}{dx^2} = E\\psi$ with boundary conditions $\\psi(0) = \\psi(L) = 0$ gives $E_n = \\frac{n^2 h^2}{8mL^2}$ for $n = 1, 2, 3, \\ldots$. The ground state ($n=1$) has energy $E_1 = h^2/(8mL^2) > 0$, demonstrating zero-point energy."
  difficulty="easy"
/>

<PracticeProblem
  question="The Heisenberg uncertainty principle states that $\\Delta x \\cdot \\Delta p \\geq \\hbar/2$. If an electron is confined to a region of width $\\Delta x = 0.1$ nm, what is the minimum uncertainty in its momentum?"
  options={[
    '$\\Delta p \\geq \\hbar / (2 \\times 10^{-10}\\,\\text{m}) = 5.27 \\times 10^{-25}\\,\\text{kg m/s}$',
    '$\\Delta p \\geq 0$ (no uncertainty)',
    '$\\Delta p \\geq \\hbar / \\Delta x = 1.05 \\times 10^{-24}\\,\\text{kg m/s}$',
    '$\\Delta p \\geq m_e c$',
  ]}
  correctAnswer={0}
  explanation"$\\Delta p \\geq \\hbar/(2\\Delta x) = (1.055 \\times 10^{-34}\\,\\text{J s}) / (2 \\times 10^{-10}\\,\\text{m}) = 5.27 \\times 10^{-25}\\,\\text{kg m/s}$. This means confining an electron to a small region necessarily gives it a large minimum momentum (and thus kinetic energy), explaining the zero-point energy in bound systems."
  difficulty="medium"
/>

<PracticeProblem
  question="For the hydrogen atom, the energy levels depend only on the principal quantum number $n$: $E_n = -\\frac{13.6\\,\\text{eV}}{n^2}$. What is the energy required to excite an electron from $n=1$ to $n=3$?"
  options={[
    '$\\Delta E = 13.6(1 - 1/9) = 12.09$ eV',
    '$\\Delta E = 13.6/3 = 4.53$ eV',
    '$\\Delta E = 13.6(1/9) = 1.51$ eV',
    '$\\Delta E = 13.6 \\times 3 = 40.8$ eV',
  ]}
  correctAnswer={0}
  explanation"$E_1 = -13.6$ eV, $E_3 = -13.6/9 = -1.51$ eV. The excitation energy is $\\Delta E = E_3 - E_1 = -1.51 - (-13.6) = 12.09$ eV. The photon wavelength would be $\\lambda = hc/\\Delta E = 1240/12.09 = 102.6$ nm (UV). The degeneracy of $n=3$ is $n^2 = 9$ (one $s$, three $p$, five $d$ orbitals)."
  difficulty="medium"
/>

<PracticeProblem
  question="For a 3D particle in a cubic box of side $L$, the ground state degeneracy is 1 and the first excited state has degeneracy 3. What quantum numbers $(n_x, n_y, n_z)$ correspond to the first excited state?"
  options={[
    '$(2,1,1)$, $(1,2,1)$, $(1,1,2)$ with energy $E = 6h^2/(8mL^2)$',
    '$(2,2,2)$ only',
    '$(2,2,1)$, $(2,1,2)$, $(1,2,2)$ with energy $E = 9h^2/(8mL^2)$',

  correctAnswer={0}
  explanation"$E = \\frac{h^2}{8mL^2}(n_x^2 + n_y^2 + n_z^2)$. Ground state: $(1,1,1)$ with $E = 3h^2/(8mL^2)$, degeneracy 1. First excited: permutations of $(2,1,1)$ all give $n_x^2 + n_y^2 + n_z^2 = 6$, so energy $= 6h^2/(8mL^2)$ with degeneracy 3. Second excited: $(2,2,1)$ permutations give sum = 9, degeneracy 3."
  difficulty="hard"

## Atomic Structure and Molecular Orbital Theory

<PracticeProblem
  question="How many radial nodes and angular nodes does the $3p$ orbital have?"
  options={[
    '1 radial node, 1 angular node (total = $n-1 = 2$)',
    '0 radial nodes, 2 angular nodes',
    '2 radial nodes, 1 angular node',
    '3 radial nodes, 0 angular nodes',
  ]}
  correctAnswer={0}
  explanation"Total nodes $= n - 1 = 3 - 1 = 2$. Radial nodes $= n - \\ell - 1 = 3 - 1 - 1 = 1$. Angular nodes $= \\ell = 1$. For $p$ orbitals, the angular node manifests as a nodal plane (dumbbell shape). For comparison: 3s has 2 radial, 0 angular; 3d has 0 radial, 2 angular (cloverleaf with 2 nodal planes)."
  difficulty="easy"
/>

<PracticeProblem
  question="For the $2s$ orbital of hydrogen, the radial distribution function $P(r) = r^2|R_{20}(r)|^2$ has:"
  options={[
    'One radial node (where $P(r) = 0$ between two maxima) plus a node at $r = 0$',
    'No radial nodes',
    'Two radial nodes',
    'A radial node only at the nucleus',
  ]}
  correctAnswer={0}
  explanation"The $2s$ orbital ($n=2, \\ell=0$) has $n - \\ell - 1 = 1$ radial node. The radial wavefunction $R_{20} \\propto (2 - r/a_0)e^{-r/2a_0}$ has a sign change at $r = 2a_0$, creating one radial node in $P(r)$. Combined with the node at $r = 0$ (from the $r^2$ factor), the radial distribution function shows two maxima separated by one node."
  difficulty="medium"
/>

<PracticeProblem
  question="In the molecular orbital (MO) theory for homonuclear diatomic molecules of the second period (Li$_2$ through F$_2$), which molecule has the highest bond order and is paramagnetic?"
  options={[
    '$\\text{O}_2$: bond order = 2, paramagnetic (two unpaired electrons in $\\pi_g^*$ orbitals)',
    '$\\text{N}_2$: bond order = 3, diamagnetic',
    '$\\text{F}_2$: bond order = 1, diamagnetic',
    '$\\text{B}_2$: bond order = 1, paramagnetic',
  ]}
  correctAnswer={0}
  explanation"O$_2$ has configuration $(\\sigma_g)^2(\\sigma_u^*)^2(\\sigma_g)^2(\\pi_u)^4(\\pi_g^*)^2$. Bond order = $(8-4)/2 = 2$. The two electrons in $\\pi_g^*$ occupy separate orbitals (Hund's rule), making O$_2$ paramagnetic. N$_2$ has bond order 3 but is diamagnetic (all paired). B$_2$ is paramagnetic but bond order is only 1."
  difficulty="hard"
/>

<PracticeProblem
  question="The quantum numbers $\\ell$ and $m_\\ell$ relate to the angular momentum of an electron. What is the magnitude of the orbital angular momentum for an electron in a $d$ orbital?"
  options={[
    '$|\\mathbf{L}| = \\hbar\\sqrt{\\ell(\\ell+1)} = \\hbar\\sqrt{6}$ for $\\ell = 2$',
    '$|\\mathbf{L}| = \\hbar \\ell = 2\\hbar$',
    '$|\\mathbf{L}| = \\hbar(\\ell + 1) = 3\\hbar$',
    '$|\\mathbf{L}| = 0$ for all orbitals',
  ]}
  correctAnswer={0}
  explanation"The magnitude of orbital angular momentum is $|\\mathbf{L}| = \\hbar\\sqrt{\\ell(\\ell+1)}$. For $d$ orbitals ($\\ell = 2$): $|\\mathbf{L}| = \\hbar\\sqrt{2 \\times 3} = \\hbar\\sqrt{6}$. Note this is NOT $\\ell\\hbar$ (the Bohr model result). The $z$-component is $L_z = m_\\ell\\hbar$ with $m_\\ell = -2, -1, 0, +1, +2$."
  difficulty="medium"
/>

<PracticeProblem
  question="The harmonic oscillator model for molecular vibration gives energy levels $E_v = (v + 1/2)\\hbar\\omega$ where $\\omega = \\sqrt{k/\\mu}$. What is the zero-point energy?"
  options={[
    '$E_0 = \\frac{1}{2}\\hbar\\omega$, meaning the molecule vibrates even at absolute zero',
    '$E_0 = 0$ (no vibration at 0 K)',
    '$E_0 = \\hbar\\omega$',
    '$E_0 = kT$ at all temperatures',
  ]}
  correctAnswer={0}
  explanation"The zero-point energy $E_0 = \\hbar\\omega/2$ is a direct consequence of the uncertainty principle: confining the oscillator at the equilibrium position would require $\\Delta x = 0$, giving infinite $\\Delta p$ and thus infinite kinetic energy. The system therefore has non-zero energy in its ground state. For HCl with $\\tilde{\\nu} = 2990$ cm$^{-1}$, $E_0/hc = 1495$ cm$^{-1}$."
  difficulty="medium"
/>

<PracticeProblem
  question="In the rigid rotor model for diatomic molecular rotation, the energy levels are $E_J = J(J+1)\\frac{\\hbar^2}{2I}$ where $I = \\mu r_e^2$. The selection rule for rotational spectroscopy is $\\Delta J = \\pm 1$. What is the energy of the first rotational transition ($J = 0 \\to 1$)?"
  options={[
    '$\\Delta E = 2B = \\frac{\\hbar^2}{I} = 2\\frac{\\hbar^2}{2I}$, expressed as $2B$ in wavenumber units',
    '$\\Delta E = B = \\frac{\\hbar^2}{2I}$',
    '$\\Delta E = 3B$',
    '$\\Delta E = 0$ (no transition allowed)',
  ]}
  correctAnswer={0}
  explanation"$E_{J=1} - E_{J=0} = 1(2)\\frac{\\hbar^2}{2I} - 0 = \\frac{\\hbar^2}{I} = 2B$ where $B = \\frac{\\hbar^2}{2I}$ is the rotational constant. All rotational transitions are equally spaced by $2B$ (in the rigid rotor approximation): $J \\to J+1$ has $\\Delta E = 2B(J+1)$. For $J=0\\to 1$: $\\Delta E = 2B$."
  difficulty="hard"
/>
