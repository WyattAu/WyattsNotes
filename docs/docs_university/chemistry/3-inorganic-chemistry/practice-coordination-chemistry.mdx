## Crystal Field Theory and Splitting

<PracticeProblem
  question="In an octahedral complex, the five $d$ orbitals split into $t_{2g}$ and $e_g$ sets. What are the relative energies and orbital assignments?"
  options={[
    '$e_g$ ($d_{z^2}, d_{x^2-y^2}$) at $+0.6\\Delta_o$; $t_{2g}$ ($d_{xy}, d_{xz}, d_{yz}$) at $-0.4\\Delta_o$',
    '$t_{2g}$ at $+0.6\\Delta_o$; $e_g$ at $-0.4\\Delta_o$',
    'All five orbitals have the same energy',
    '$d_{xy}$ at $+0.6\\Delta_o$; others at $-0.4\\Delta_o$',
  ]}
  correctAnswer={0}
  explanation="In octahedral symmetry, the six ligands approach along the $\\pm x, \\pm y, \\pm z$ axes. The $e_g$ orbitals ($d_{z^2}$ and $d_{x^2-y^2}$) point directly at the ligands (maximum repulsion, higher energy: $+0.6\\Delta_o$). The $t_{2g}$ orbitals ($d_{xy}, d_{xz}, d_{yz}$) point between the axes (less repulsion, lower energy: $-0.4\\Delta_o$). Total: $2(+6\\text{Dq}) + 3(-4\\text{Dq}) = 0$ (barycenter preserved)."
  difficulty="easy"
/>

<PracticeProblem
  question="What is the crystal field stabilization energy (CFSE) for a $d^5$ high-spin octahedral complex?"
  options={[
    '$\\text{CFSE} = 0$ (three electrons in $t_{2g}$ at $-4\\text{Dq}$ each, two in $e_g$ at $+6\\text{Dq}$ each: $3(-4) + 2(+6) = 0$)',
    '$\\text{CFSE} = -20\\text{Dq}$ (all electrons in $t_{2g}$)',
    '$\\text{CFSE} = +20\\text{Dq}$ (all electrons in $e_g$)',
    '$\\text{CFSE} = -10\\text{Dq}$',
  ]}
  correctAnswer={0}
  explanation="High-spin $d^5$: $t_{2g}^3 e_g^2$ (Hund's rule: all five electrons unpaired). CFSE $= 3(-4\\text{Dq}) + 2(+6\\text{Dq}) = -12\\text{Dq} + 12\\text{Dq} = 0$. $d^5$ high spin has zero CFSE. Low-spin $d^5$: $t_{2g}^5 e_g^0$, CFSE $= 5(-4\\text{Dq}) = -20\\text{Dq}$. This large difference explains why $d^5$ complexes with strong-field ligands favour low spin."
  difficulty="hard"

  options={[
    '$[\\text{Cu}(\\text{H}_2\\text{O})_6]^{2+}$ ($d^9$, one electron in $e_g$, creating strong axial elongation)',
    '$[\\text{Fe}(\\text{H}_2\\text{O})_6]^{3+}$ ($d^5$ high spin, symmetric $t_{2g}^3 e_g^2$)',

  ]}
  correctAnswer={0}
  explanation="The Jahn-Teller effect is strongest for degenerate ground states with uneven occupancy. $d^9$ (e.g., Cu$^{2+}$) has $t_{2g}^6 e_g^3$: the $e_g$ set has one electron in each orbital plus a hole in one. The complex elongates along one axis (two ligands move away) to remove the degeneracy, giving two long and four short bonds. This is why Cu$^{2+}$ complexes often show 4+2 distorted octahedral geometry."
