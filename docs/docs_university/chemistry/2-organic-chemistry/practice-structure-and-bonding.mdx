## Hybridization and Resonance

<PracticeProblem question="The C=C double bond in ethylene involves $sp^2$ hybridization. What is the bond angle around each carbon and how many sigma and pi bonds does each carbon form?" options={[ '120 degree bond angle; each carbon forms 3 sigma bonds (2 C-H and 1 C-C) and 1 pi bond (from unhybridized $p_z$ orbitals)', '109.5 degree angle; each carbon forms 4 sigma bonds and 0 pi bonds', '180 degree angle; each carbon forms 2 sigma bonds and 2 pi bonds', '90 degree angle; each carbon forms 4 sigma bonds and 1 pi bond', ]} correctAnswer={0} explanation="$sp^2$ hybridization: one $s$ + two $p$ orbitals form three hybrid orbitals in a trigonal planar arrangement (120 degrees). Each carbon forms 3 $\\sigma$ bonds (2 C-H + 1 C-C) using $sp^2$ hybrids and 1 $\\pi$ bond from the unhybridized $p_z$ orbitals overlapping sideways. The $\\pi$ bond is weaker than the $\\sigma$ bond and restricts rotation." difficulty="easy" />

<PracticeProblem question="Which of the following correctly describes the relationship between $s$ character and bond properties?" options={[ 'More $s$ character gives shorter, stronger bonds with larger bond angles (sp: 180 degree, 50% $s$ > sp2: 120 degree, 33% $s$ > sp3: 109.5 degree, 25% $s$)', 'More $s$ character gives longer, weaker bonds', '$s$ character has no effect on bond length or strength', 'More $s$ character reduces bond angle and bond strength', ]} correctAnswer={0} explanation="$s$ orbitals are closer to the nucleus (lower energy) than $p$ orbitals. More $s$ character in a hybrid orbital means the bond is pulled closer to the nucleus: shorter, stronger, with larger bond angles. sp (50% $s$): 180 degrees, strongest; $sp^2$ (33.3% $s$): 120 degrees; $sp^3$ (25% $s$): 109.5 degrees, weakest. The acidity of C-H bonds also increases with $s$ character." difficulty="medium" />

None of the individual contributing structures alone represents the true molecule." difficulty="easy" />

<PracticeProblem question="Which molecule is aromatic according to Huckel's rule ($4n+2$ pi electrons in a planar, cyclic, conjugated system)?" options={[ 'Benzene (6 pi electrons, $n=1$)', 'Cyclobutadiene (4 pi electrons, $n=1$ for antiaromaticity)', 'Cyclooctatetraene (8 pi electrons, non-planar)', '1,3,5,7-cyclooctatetraene in its planar form', ]} correctAnswer={0} explanation="Huckel’s rule: a planar, cyclic, fully conjugated system with $4n+2$ pi electrons ($n = 0, 1, 2, \\ldots$) is aromatic. ## Stereochemistry and Conformational Analysis

<PracticeProblem question="The Cahn-Ingold-Prelog (CIP) priority rules assign R/S configuration to chiral centers. What is the first rule for determining priority?" options={[ 'Higher atomic number of the atom directly attached to the chiral center gets higher priority', 'Higher molecular weight of the substituent gets higher priority', 'The substituent with more carbon atoms gets higher priority', 'Alphabetical order of the substituent name', ]} correctAnswer={0} explanation="CIP priority rules: (1) Atoms directly attached to the chiral center: higher atomic number = higher priority (I > Br > Cl > F > O > N > C > H). (2) If tied, look at the next atoms outward (by atomic number, one at a time). (3) Double/triple bonds are treated as duplicates of the attached atom. After assigning priorities, view from the lowest-priority side; clockwise = R, anticlockwise = S." difficulty="medium" />

<PracticeProblem question="In the Newman projection of butane, the anti conformation has the two methyl groups at 180 degrees. What is the relative energy order of butane conformations?" options={[ 'Anti ($\\sim 0$ kJ/mol) < gauche ($\\sim 0.9$ kJ/mol, two equivalent) < eclipsed ($\\sim 12$ kJ/mol)', 'Gauche < anti < eclipsed', 'All conformations have the same energy', 'Anti < eclipsed < gauche', ]} correctAnswer={0} explanation="The anti conformation (methyls 180 degrees apart) is most stable due to minimal steric repulsion and favorable staggered arrangement. Gauche (methyls 60 degrees apart) has steric repulsion (~0.9 kJ/mol above anti). Eclipsed (methyls 0 degrees apart) has maximum steric and torsional strain (~12 kJ/mol above anti). The energy barrier between anti and eclipsed is ~12-15 kJ/mol." difficulty="medium" />

<PracticeProblem question="For cyclohexane, which conformation is more stable and by how much?" options={[ 'Chair conformation is more stable by about 23 kJ/mol (no angle strain and minimal torsional strain)', 'Boat conformation is more stable due to reduced steric strain', 'Both are equally stable', 'Chair is only marginally more stable by about 1 kJ/mol', ]} correctAnswer={0} explanation="The chair conformation of cyclohexane has bond angles close to tetrahedral (no angle strain) and all C-H bonds are staggered (minimal torsional strain). The boat has flagpole interactions (1,3-diaxial repulsion) and eclipsed bonds. Energy difference: ~23 kJ/mol. At room temperature, cyclohexane rapidly interconverts between two chair conformations via boat/twist-boat transition states." difficulty="easy" />

<PracticeProblem question="In the chair conformation of methylcyclohexane, the methyl group preferentially occupies the equatorial position. The energy difference between axial and equatorial is approximately:" options={[ '$\\sim 7.3$ kJ/mol (1,3-diaxial interactions between the axial methyl and two axial hydrogens)', '$\\sim 0.5$ kJ/mol', '$\\sim 23$ kJ/mol', '$\\sim 15$ kJ/mol', ]} correctAnswer={0} explanation="An axial methyl group on cyclohexane experiences two 1,3-diaxial gauche interactions with axial hydrogens at C-3 and C-5 positions, each costing ~3.6 kJ/mol, totaling ~7.3 kJ/mol. At room temperature (~94% equatorial), the equilibrium heavily favours the equatorial conformer. With a larger substituent (e.g., tert-butyl), the preference for equatorial is even stronger." difficulty="hard" />

<PracticeProblem question="Furan is a five-membered aromatic heterocycle with oxygen. How many pi electrons does it contribute to aromaticity?" options={[ '6 pi electrons (4 from two C=C double bonds + 2 from the oxygen lone pair in the $p$ orbital)', '4 pi electrons (from the two C=C bonds only)', '8 pi electrons (including all oxygen lone pairs)', '5 pi electrons (one from each atom in the ring)', ]} correctAnswer={0} explanation="Furan satisfies Huckel’s rule with 6 pi electrons. The oxygen contributes one lone pair to the pi system (in the $p$ orbital parallel to the ring), while the other lone pair is in the $sp^2$ plane (perpendicular). Total: 4 from two C=C bonds + 2 from oxygen = 6 pi electrons ($4n+2$ with $n=1$). Thiophene, pyrrole, and pyridine are other aromatic heterocycles with similar lone pair involvement." difficulty="hard" />

<PracticeProblem question="The E/Z nomenclature for alkene stereochemistry is based on the Cahn-Ingold-Prelog priority of substituents on each double-bond carbon. On the right C: Br and CH$_3$; priority Br > CH$_3$. If Br and CH$_3$ are on the same side = Z. If on opposite sides = E. Note: the Z/E system is more reliable than cis/trans for cases with four different substituents or when cis/trans is ambiguous." difficulty="medium" />
