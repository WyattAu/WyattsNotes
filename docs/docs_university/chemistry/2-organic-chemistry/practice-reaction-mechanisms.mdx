## Nucleophilic Substitution and Elimination

<PracticeProblem
  question="Which substrate and conditions favour the SN2 mechanism over SN1?"
  options={[
    'Primary substrate with a strong nucleophile in a polar aprotic solvent',
    'Tertiary substrate with a weak nucleophile in a polar protic solvent',
    'Tertiary substrate with a strong nucleophile in a polar aprotic solvent',
    'Secondary substrate with a weak nucleophile in a polar protic solvent',
  ]}
  correctAnswer={0}
  explanation="SN2 requires a strong nucleophile and minimal steric hindrance (primary > secondary > tertiary; tertiary is essentially inert to SN2). Polar aprotic solvents (DMSO, DMF, acetone) enhance nucleophilicity by not solvating the anion. SN1 is favoured by tertiary substrates, weak nucleophiles, and polar protic solvents that stabilize carbocations."
  difficulty="easy"
/>

<PracticeProblem
  question="The E2 elimination requires a base to abstract a proton anti-periplanar to the leaving group. For (2R,3S)-3-bromo-2-butanol treated with a strong base, which stereochemical outcome is expected?"
  options={[
    'Anti-elimination gives the $(E)$-alkene preferentially if the substrate allows anti-periplanar geometry',
    'The $(Z)$-alkene is the major product regardless of stereochemistry',
    'No elimination occurs; only SN2 substitution',
    'A mixture of $(E)$ and $(Z)$ in equal amounts regardless of conditions',
  ]}
  correctAnswer={0}
  explanation="E2 requires anti-periplanar geometry (H-C-C-LG dihedral angle = 180 degrees). The product stereochemistry depends on which beta-hydrogen is anti to the leaving group. For a substrate with two beta-hydrogens, the one with the anti-periplanar arrangement is abstracted. Bulky bases (t-butoxide) favour less substituted alkenes (Hofmann product) while smaller bases favour more substituted alkenes (Zaitsev product)."
  difficulty="medium"
/>

<PracticeProblem
  question="In the SN1 reaction of $t$-butyl chloride with water, what is the role of water in each step?"
  options={[
    'Step 1: water is not involved (rate-determining ionization); Step 2: water acts as the nucleophile attacking the carbocation',
    'Water acts as the leaving group in both steps',
    'Water deprotonates the carbocation in step 1',
    'Water serves only as the solvent and does not participate chemically',
  ]}
  correctAnswer={0}
  explanation="SN1 proceeds via: (1) slow ionization to form a carbocation ($t$-Bu$^+$ + Cl$^-$), rate $= k[\\text{R-LG}]$, independent of nucleophile; (2) fast nucleophilic attack by water on the carbocation; (3) fast deprotonation. Water is a weak nucleophile, which is why SN1 works well with tertiary substrates (stable carbocations) despite the weak nucleophile."
  difficulty="medium"
/>

<PracticeProblem
  question="A primary alkyl halide undergoes substitution with NaCN in DMSO. What is the mechanism and what stereochemical outcome is expected if the substrate is chiral?"
  options={[
    'SN2 with complete Walden inversion (opposite configuration at the stereocenter)',
    'SN1 with racemization (partial inversion and retention)',
    'SN2 with retention of configuration',
    'E2 elimination with no substitution',
  ]}
  correctAnswer={0}
  explanation="NaCN provides CN$^-$, a strong nucleophile. DMSO is a polar aprotic solvent that enhances nucleophilicity. The primary substrate with strong nucleophile undergoes SN2. The mechanism is a single concerted step with backside attack, giving complete Walden inversion of configuration at the carbon. The product has the opposite stereochemistry to the reactant."
  difficulty="easy"
/>

<PracticeProblem
  question="Which of the following is the correct order of carbocation stability?"
  options={[
    'Tertiary $>$ secondary $>$ primary $>$ methyl $>$ vinyl',
    'Methyl $>$ primary $>$ secondary $>$ tertiary',
    'Vinyl $>$ tertiary $>$ secondary $>$ primary',
    'Primary $>$ tertiary $>$ secondary $>$ methyl',
  ]}
  correctAnswer={0}
  explanation="Carbocation stability: tertiary (3 alkyl groups, hyperconjugation) > secondary > primary > methyl. Alkyl groups stabilize by hyperconjugation and inductive electron donation. Vinyl ($sp^2$) and aryl carbocations are very unstable because the empty $p$ orbital is orthogonal to the $\\pi$ system (no resonance stabilization). Allylic and benzylic carbocations are exceptionally stable due to resonance."
  difficulty="easy"
/>

## Electrophilic Reactions and Rearrangements

<PracticeProblem
  question="In electrophilic aromatic substitution (EAS), the nitration of toluene with $\\text{HNO}_3/\\text{H}_2\\text{SO}_4$ produces predominantly ortho and para products. What is the role of $\\text{H}_2\\text{SO}_4$?"
  options={[
    '$\\text{H}_2\\text{SO}_4$ protonates $\\text{HNO}_3$ to generate the electrophile $\\text{NO}_2^+$ (nitronium ion)',
    '$\\text{H}_2\\text{SO}_4$ acts as the nucleophile',
    '$\\text{H}_2\\text{SO}_4$ reduces $\\text{HNO}_3$ to $\\text{NO}_2$',
    '$\\text{H}_2\\text{SO}_4$ directly substitutes onto the aromatic ring',
  ]}
  correctAnswer={0}
  explanation="The mechanism: $\\text{H}_2\\text{SO}_4 + \\text{HNO}_3 \\to \\text{H}_2\\text{NO}_3^+ + \\text{HSO}_4^-$, then $\\text{H}_2\\text{NO}_3^+ \\to \\text{H}_2\\text{O} + \\text{NO}_2^+$. The nitronium ion ($\\text{NO}_2^+$) is the electrophile that attacks the aromatic ring. Methyl group is ortho/para-directing and activating (electron-donating by hyperconjugation and inductive effects)."
  difficulty="medium"
/>

<PracticeProblem
  question="The 1,2-hydride shift in a carbocation rearrangement is driven by:"

<PracticeProblem
  question="For the electrophilic addition of HBr to 1,3-butadiene, the major product at low temperature is the 1,2-adduct while at high temperature the 1,4-adduct predominates. This is explained by:"
  options={[
    'Kinetic control (1,2-adduct, lower $E_a$) vs. thermodynamic control (1,4-adduct, more stable conjugated diene)',
    'The 1,2-adduct is always more stable',
    'Temperature does not affect the product distribution',
    'The 1,4-adduct forms faster at all temperatures',
  ]}
  correctAnswer={0}
  explanation="At low $T$, the reaction is kinetically controlled: the 1,2-adduct forms faster because it has a lower activation energy (shorter path for the nucleophile to reach the carbocation). At high $T$, thermodynamic control dominates: the system equilibrates, and the more stable 1,4-adduct (with the double bond more substituted and conjugated) predominates. This is a classic example of kinetic vs. thermodynamic control."
  difficulty="hard"
/>

<PracticeProblem
  question="The Williamson ether synthesis involves an SN2 reaction between an alkoxide and an alkyl halide. Which combination of reagents would fail to produce the desired ether?"

    'Sodium ethoxide with methyl iodide',
  ]}
  correctAnswer={0}
  explanation="The Williamson synthesis is an SN2 reaction: $\\text{RO}^- + \\text{R\'-LG} \\to \\text{ROR\'} + \\text{LG}^-$. It fails with tertiary alkyl halides (SN2 blocked by steric hindrance) and with bulky combinations. Tertiary halides undergo E2 instead. Optimal combinations: primary alkyl halide with any alkoxide, or secondary halide with small alkoxides."
  difficulty="medium"
